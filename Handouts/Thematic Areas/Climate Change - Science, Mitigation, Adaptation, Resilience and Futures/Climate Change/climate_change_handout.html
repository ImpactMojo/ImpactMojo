Climate Change 101

IPCC Basics, Vulnerability, Adaptation & Climate Justice in South Asia
ImpactMojo Workshop Series • Understanding Climate Science & Action
75-90 Minutes

Workshop Overview

Target Audience: Development practitioners, environmental activists, policy makers, researchers, and community organizers working on climate and sustainability issues

Prerequisites: Basic understanding of environmental issues helpful but not required

Materials Needed: Laptops/tablets for accessing climate data, notebooks, colored pens for mapping

Learning Objectives

By the end of this workshop, participants will be able to:

  • Understand basic climate science and IPCC findings relevant to South Asia
  • Analyze climate vulnerability patterns across different communities and regions
  • Distinguish between climate adaptation and mitigation strategies
  • Apply climate justice frameworks to assess impacts and responses
  • Design climate-informed development interventions and policies

Part 1: Climate Science Fundamentals - The IPCC Framework

20 minutes

🌡️ The Stark Reality

Global Temperature: Earth has warmed 1.1°C above pre-industrial levels

South Asian Impact: India has warmed 0.7°C since 1900, with accelerating pace

Tipping Point: 1.5°C warming could trigger irreversible changes

Time Window: 8 years left to limit warming to 1.5°C (IPCC AR6, 2023)

Understanding the Greenhouse Effect

🔬 The Science Behind Climate Change

Natural Greenhouse Effect: Essential for life - keeps Earth 33°C warmer than it would be

Enhanced Greenhouse Effect: Human activities add more greenhouse gases, trapping extra heat

Key Greenhouse Gases & Their Sources:

  • Carbon Dioxide (CO₂) - 76%: Fossil fuels, deforestation, cement production
  • Methane (CH₄) - 16%: Agriculture (rice, livestock), landfills, gas leaks
  • Nitrous Oxide (N₂O) - 6%: Fertilizers, fossil fuels, industrial processes
  • Fluorinated gases - 2%: Refrigerants, industrial processes

IPCC Projections for South Asia

Climate Variable Current Trend 2030 Projection 2050 Projection
Temperature +0.7°C since 1900 +1.5-2°C +2-4°C
Monsoon Rainfall Increasing variability More intense, irregular +10-20% intensity
Extreme Heat Days Doubling since 1960 3-4x current levels 5-7x current levels
Sea Level Rise +1.7mm/year +30-60cm +60-110cm
Glacial Retreat -10-15m/year Accelerating loss 30-50% volume loss

Critical Insight: South Asia is a climate hotspot - warming faster than global average, with 1.9 billion people facing severe climate risks by 2050.

🌍 Activity: Climate Science Interpretation (8 minutes)

You are briefing a district collector about climate projections for their region. Using the data above, answer:

Scenario: Agricultural district in Punjab with wheat and rice cultivation, facing groundwater depletion

Analysis Questions (Work in pairs):

  1. Temperature Impact: How will 2-4°C warming affect crop yields and growing seasons?
  2. Water Stress: What happens when glacier-fed rivers reduce flow AND heat increases water demand?
  3. Extreme Events: How might more intense heat waves and erratic monsoons affect farmers?
  4. Timeline: Which impacts require immediate attention vs. long-term planning?

Share-out: Key insights about translating climate science into local impacts (5 minutes)

Part 2: Vulnerability Assessment - Who Gets Hit Hardest?

20 minutes

⚖️ Climate Vulnerability = Exposure × Sensitivity × Adaptive Capacity

Exposure: Degree of climate stress (how much change occurs)

Sensitivity: How affected a system is by climate change

Adaptive Capacity: Ability to adjust, cope, and recover

Vulnerability Patterns in South Asia

🏔️ Mountain Communities

High Exposure: Rapid glacial retreat, changing precipitation

High Sensitivity: Water-dependent livelihoods

Low Adaptive Capacity: Limited resources, remote location

🌾 Small Farmers

High Exposure: Variable rainfall, extreme temperatures

High Sensitivity: Rain-fed agriculture, single crops

Low Adaptive Capacity: Limited credit, technology, insurance

🏘️ Urban Poor

High Exposure: Heat islands, flooding, air pollution

High Sensitivity: Informal settlements, health vulnerabilities

Low Adaptive Capacity: Limited mobility, informal jobs

🏖️ Coastal Communities

High Exposure: Sea level rise, cyclones, storm surge

High Sensitivity: Low-lying areas, fishing dependent

Mixed Adaptive Capacity: Some have resources, others don't

👩 Women

High Exposure: Same climate stress as men

High Sensitivity: Water collection, indoor air pollution

Lower Adaptive Capacity: Limited decision-making power, resources

🏭 Industrial Workers

High Exposure: Heat stress, outdoor work

High Sensitivity: Physical labor, safety risks

Low Adaptive Capacity: Can't avoid work, limited workplace protection

Intersectional Climate Impacts

🔍 Climate Justice Lens

The Paradox: Those who contribute least to climate change suffer the most from its impacts

Group Contribution to Emissions Climate Impact Level Access to Solutions
Rural poor (bottom 50%) Very low (10% of emissions) Very high Very limited
Urban middle class Medium (40% of emissions) Medium Moderate
Wealthy (top 10%) Very high (50% of emissions) Low High

Part 3: Climate Solutions - Mitigation vs. Adaptation

20 minutes

Understanding the Two-Track Approach

🌱 Climate Mitigation

Goal: Reduce greenhouse gas emissions to limit future warming

Focus: Addressing causes of climate change

Strategies:

  • Renewable energy transition
  • Energy efficiency improvements
  • Sustainable transport systems
  • Forest conservation and afforestation
  • Carbon pricing and markets
  • Sustainable agriculture practices

Timeframe: Long-term (10-50 years)

🛡️ Climate Adaptation

Goal: Adjust to current and future climate impacts

Focus: Managing consequences of climate change

Strategies:

  • Climate-resilient infrastructure
  • Drought-resistant crop varieties
  • Early warning systems
  • Ecosystem-based adaptation
  • Social protection systems
  • Livelihood diversification

Timeframe: Immediate to medium-term (1-20 years)

India's Climate Action Framework

🇮🇳 National Climate Commitments

Paris Agreement Targets (Updated 2022):

  • Emissions Intensity: Reduce by 45% by 2030 (from 2005 levels)
  • Non-fossil Capacity: 500 GW renewable energy by 2030
  • Carbon Sink: Create additional 2.5-3 billion tons CO₂ equivalent
  • Net Zero: Achieve carbon neutrality by 2070

National Adaptation Priorities:

  • Water Security: Jal Shakti Mission, groundwater management
  • Climate-Smart Agriculture: Crop insurance, climate-resilient varieties
  • Disaster Risk Reduction: Early warning, infrastructure hardening
  • Ecosystem Services: Coastal protection, urban green spaces

💡 Solution Design Workshop (15 minutes)

Your task: Design a climate intervention for a specific context

Scenario: A municipal corporation in a Tier-2 city (population 500,000) wants to develop a climate action plan. The city faces:

  • Increasing summer temperatures (45°C+ for 15-20 days)
  • Erratic monsoons causing urban flooding
  • Air pollution from traffic and industry
  • Water scarcity in summer months
  • Growing informal settlements

Design Framework (Work in groups of 3-4):

  1. Prioritize (5 min): Which climate challenge should be addressed first? Why?
  2. Strategy (5 min): Choose 2 mitigation + 2 adaptation solutions that fit this context
  3. Implementation (3 min): What are the key steps and stakeholders needed?
  4. Co-benefits (2 min): How do your solutions address multiple challenges?

Presentations: 2 minutes per group to pitch your climate action plan

Part 4: Climate Justice and Governance

10 minutes

Climate Justice Principle: Climate action must address the unequal distribution of climate impacts and ensure that solutions don't worsen existing inequalities.

Governance Challenges & Opportunities

🏛️ Multi-level Climate Governance

Global Level: Paris Agreement, climate finance, technology transfer

National Level: NDCs, climate laws, sectoral policies

State Level: Climate action plans, adaptation strategies

Local Level: Urban climate plans, community adaptation

Challenge: Coordination across levels and alignment of incentives

💰 Climate Finance and Equity

The Numbers:

  • Global Need: $2.8 trillion/year for climate action by 2030
  • Developing Country Need: $1 trillion/year for adaptation alone
  • Current Finance: $100 billion/year (far below need)
  • India's Requirement: $170 billion for 2015-2030 NDCs

Equity Concerns:

  • Rich countries responsible for 79% of historical emissions
  • Climate finance often comes as loans, not grants
  • Adaptation receives less funding than mitigation
  • Local communities often excluded from decision-making

⚖️ Climate Justice Analysis (8 minutes)

Case Study: Large solar park development displaces farming communities

Situation: 5,000 hectares of agricultural land acquired for solar park. Benefits: 750 MW clean energy, reduced emissions. Costs: 2,000 farming families displaced, loss of traditional livelihoods.

Justice Questions to Consider:

  1. Distributive Justice: Who bears costs vs. who receives benefits?
  2. Procedural Justice: Were affected communities consulted in decision-making?
  3. Recognition Justice: Are traditional land rights and livelihoods valued?
  4. Corrective Justice: What compensation and alternatives are provided?

Discussion: How could this project be redesigned to be more just? What principles should guide climate interventions?

Key Takeaway

Climate change is not just an environmental issue - it's a development challenge that intersects with poverty, inequality, health, and justice. Effective climate action must be grounded in both science and equity.
