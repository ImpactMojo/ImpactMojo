Part 1: Understanding Community-Led Development

18 minutes

🏘️ Three Stories of Community-Led Development in India

Barefoot College Model (Rajasthan): Illiterate rural women become solar engineers through peer-to-peer learning. No formal education required - communities identify women who will stay and serve their villages. Women return to electrify their communities and train others.

Kudumbashree (Kerala): Women's community network covering 4.5 million families. Self-help groups evolved into microenterprises, local government partnerships, and disaster response systems. Women lead village-level planning and budgeting processes.

Gram Vikas MANTRA (Odisha): Tribal and Dalit communities drive their own development through "movement and action network for transformation." 100% participation requirement - no household left behind. Communities decide priorities and contribute labor/resources.

Defining Community-Led Development

📊 Participation Paradox: 89% of development programs claim to be "participatory" but only 23% involve communities in key decisions

Beyond Participation: Community-led development goes beyond consulting communities to putting communities in the driver's seat of their own development processes. It's about shifting power, not just gathering input.

🪜 Ladder of Community Participation

8. Community Control

Communities have full decision-making power over programs affecting them

7. Delegated Power

Communities negotiate and share decision-making with external actors

6. Partnership

Power is genuinely shared through joint structures and processes

5. Placation

Communities have voice but final decisions rest with external actors

4. Consultation

Communities are asked for input but may not see changes

3. Informing

One-way information flow from program to community

2. Therapy

Communities are "helped" to understand their problems

1. Manipulation

Communities are co-opted to support predetermined plans

🎯 Participation Assessment Exercise (12 minutes)

Instructions: Reflect on a community program you know well. Map it on the participation ladder.

Program Analysis Questions:

Decision-Making Power (3 minutes):

  • Who decided the program was needed?
  • Who chose the interventions/activities?
  • Who controls the budget and resources?
  • Who evaluates success and failure?

Participation Analysis (3 minutes):

  • Which community members participate? Who is excluded?
  • What form does participation take? (meetings, labor, feedback, etc.)
  • Can communities say "no" or change program direction?
  • How are conflicts and disagreements handled?

Power Analysis (3 minutes):

  • Where are the real power centers in this program?
  • What would happen if the community wanted to do something different?
  • How does this program interact with existing power structures?

Ladder Placement (3 minutes):

  • Where would you place this program on the participation ladder?
  • What would need to change to move it up one level?
  • What structural barriers prevent higher levels of participation?

Share Back: Pairs share their ladder placement and one key insight about power dynamics in their program.

Part 2: Core Principles of Community-Led Development

20 minutes

Foundational Principles

🔄 Process Over Product

How communities make decisions matters more than what they decide. Strong processes build lasting capacity.

📚 Communities as Experts

Communities have deep knowledge of their contexts, needs, and solutions. External actors bring resources, not answers.

⚖️ Addressing Power Imbalances

Deliberate strategies needed to ensure marginalized voices are heard and valued in community processes.

🌱 Building on Existing Strengths

Communities already have assets, networks, and capabilities. Development builds on these foundations.

🔗 Collective Action Focus

Individual change happens through collective processes. Strengthen community organization and solidarity.

📊 Accountability to Communities

Primary accountability is to communities, not donors or organizations. Communities set success measures.

Navigating Tensions and Dilemmas

⚠️ Common Tensions in Community-Led Development
  • Time vs. Urgency: Participatory processes take time, but communities often need immediate help
  • Inclusion vs. Efficiency: Including everyone slows decisions but exclusion undermines legitimacy
  • Local vs. Technical Knowledge: Balancing community wisdom with technical expertise
  • Consensus vs. Conflict: Healthy disagreement vs. paralyzing conflict in decision-making
  • Sustainability vs. Dependency: Building independence while providing necessary support

🧭 Framework for Navigating Tensions

The SPACE Framework for Participatory Development:

Dimension Key Questions Strategies
S - Spaces Where and how do people come together? Create safe, accessible, culturally appropriate meeting spaces
P - Power Who has voice and influence? Actively address power imbalances and create new leadership
A - Awareness What do people understand about issues? Facilitate critical reflection and collective analysis
C - Capacity What skills and resources exist? Build on existing strengths and develop new capabilities
E - Engagement How do people participate in decisions? Design meaningful participation opportunities at all levels

🎭 Case Study: Participatory Budgeting in Kerala Panchayats

Context: Kerala's Panchayati Raj system allocates 35-40% of plan funds through participatory planning processes involving 30,000+ local institutions.

Process Innovation:

  • Gram Sabhas: Village assemblies where every adult can participate in planning
  • Beneficiary Groups: Self-help groups, farmer groups, and cooperatives drive sector planning
  • Ward Committees: Neighborhood-level planning for infrastructure and services
  • Social Audits: Community-led monitoring of fund utilization
  • Kudumbashree Integration: Women's network ensures gender-responsive planning

Results: ₹1.2 lakh crore allocated through participatory processes, significant improvements in local infrastructure, women's participation increased from 15% to 65%

Persistent Challenges:

  • Elite Capture: Educated and politically connected families dominate some processes
  • Technical Capacity: Complex planning procedures exclude less literate community members
  • Caste Dynamics: Traditional hierarchies influence participation patterns
  • Political Interference: Party politics affects community decision-making

Lessons Learned: Legal frameworks enable participation, but continuous effort needed to address power imbalances and build inclusive processes.

🔍 SPACE Analysis Exercise (15 minutes)

Scenario: You're working with a community that wants to address water scarcity through collective action.

Community Context:

  • Mixed caste village with 200 households
  • Traditional water sources drying up
  • Some families have private borewells, others depend on common wells
  • Women walk 2km daily for water during summer
  • Village council dominated by upper-caste men
  • Active women's self-help groups exist

SPACE Analysis Task (work in small groups):

Spaces (3 minutes): Where and when should community discussions happen to ensure broad participation?

Power (3 minutes): Who currently has voice in village decisions? Who is excluded? How can power dynamics be addressed?

Awareness (3 minutes): What do different community members understand about the water crisis? What analysis process would help build shared understanding?

Capacity (3 minutes): What existing skills, resources, and organizations can be built upon?

Engagement (3 minutes): How can all community members meaningfully participate in solution development?

Presentation: Each group presents one key insight from their SPACE analysis and one practical strategy for inclusive participation.

Part 3: Participatory Learning and Action (PLA) Tools

25 minutes

Introduction to PLA Methods

PLA Philosophy: Participatory Learning and Action tools enable communities to research their own situations, analyze problems, and plan solutions. These tools shift power from external "experts" to community members as co-researchers and decision-makers.

📊 Tool Effectiveness: PLA methods can increase community ownership by 67% and improve project sustainability by 45%

Core PLA Tool Categories

🗺️ Mapping & Visualization

Community maps, resource maps, mobility maps, body maps

📊 Ranking & Scoring

Wealth ranking, preference ranking, impact scoring, problem prioritization

📅 Time-based Analysis

Seasonal calendars, historical timelines, daily activity charts, life cycle analysis

🔄 Systems Analysis

Venn diagrams, flow charts, network mapping, cause-effect analysis

🎭 Creative Methods

Role plays, storytelling, photo voice, community theater, drawing

🔍 Investigation Tools

Transect walks, household interviews, focus groups, community surveys

Deep Dive: Community Mapping

🗺️ Community Mapping Process

Purpose: Help communities visualize their resources, challenges, and opportunities while building collective understanding of their situation.

Materials: Large sheets of paper, colored markers, sticky notes, local materials (seeds, stones, leaves)

Step-by-Step Process:

  1. Preparation (5 minutes): Explain purpose, form diverse groups, gather materials
  2. Boundary Setting (10 minutes): Groups agree on geographic boundaries of their map
  3. Basic Features (15 minutes): Draw roads, rivers, major landmarks, residential areas
  4. Resource Mapping (20 minutes): Add schools, health centers, markets, water sources, etc.
  5. Problem Identification (15 minutes): Mark problem areas with symbols or colors
  6. Opportunity Mapping (10 minutes): Identify potential sites for interventions
  7. Sharing & Discussion (15 minutes): Groups present maps and discuss differences
  8. Synthesis (10 minutes): Create combined map or identify key themes

Facilitation Tips:

  • Include diverse voices - mix age, gender, caste, economic status
  • Encourage use of local languages and symbols
  • Ask probing questions: "What's missing?" "Who uses this space?"
  • Address disagreements openly - they reveal important perspectives
  • Document maps and discussions for future reference
🔍 Power Analysis During PLA

Who speaks most? Monitor participation patterns and actively invite quieter voices

Whose knowledge counts? Validate different types of knowledge and experience

What gets prioritized? Ensure problems affecting marginalized groups are not overlooked

How are decisions made? Use democratic processes, not just majority rule

📍 Case Study: Participatory Mapping in Uttarakhand Villages

Context: Hill villages facing climate change impacts needed to plan adaptation strategies. Traditional top-down planning failed to account for local knowledge about changing weather patterns.

PLA Process:

  • Resource Mapping: Communities mapped water sources, agricultural land, forests, and infrastructure
  • Seasonal Calendars: Tracked changing rainfall patterns, crop cycles, and labor migration
  • Historical Timelines: Documented environmental changes over past 20 years
  • Vulnerability Mapping: Identified areas most at risk from floods, landslides, and drought
  • Adaptation Planning: Communities designed location-specific adaptation strategies

Key Insights from PLA:

  • Local Climate Knowledge: Communities had detailed observations about changing weather patterns not captured in meteorological data
  • Gendered Impacts: Women identified water scarcity impacts on health and time use that men overlooked
  • Traditional Solutions: Elders shared indigenous practices for water conservation and soil management
  • Spatial Variations: Even within villages, different areas had different vulnerabilities and adaptation needs

Outcomes: Village-level adaptation plans implemented with government support, community ownership increased, local knowledge documented and shared

🛠️ PLA Tool Practice: Wealth Ranking Exercise (20 minutes)

Scenario: You need to understand economic diversity in a community to design an inclusive livelihood program.

Wealth Ranking Process:

Setup (5 minutes):

  • Form groups of 4-5 people from different economic backgrounds
  • Give each group cards with names of hypothetical households in a village
  • Households: Kumar (government job), Meera (landless agricultural worker), Ravi (small shop owner), Sita (small farmer), Prakash (migrant worker), Gita (widow, house help)

Ranking Process (10 minutes):

  1. Groups arrange household cards from richest to poorest
  2. Discuss: What criteria are you using? (land, income, job security, assets, etc.)
  3. Create 3-4 wealth categories (e.g., well-off, comfortable, struggling, very poor)
  4. Place households in categories and discuss borderline cases

Analysis (5 minutes):

  • What characteristics define each wealth category?
  • Which households are most vulnerable to shocks?
  • How might gender, caste, or age affect these rankings?
  • What livelihood opportunities would help each category?

Debrief: Groups share their wealth categories and discuss how this analysis would inform program design. What did you learn about community economic dynamics?

Synthesis and Implementation

Key Principles for Ethical Facilitation

🎯 Facilitator's Ethical Framework

Before the Process:

  • ☐ Understand your own positionality and biases
  • ☐ Learn about community context, history, and power dynamics
  • ☐ Design inclusive processes that address barriers to participation
  • ☐ Prepare to handle conflicts and difficult conversations
  • ☐ Establish clear agreements about confidentiality and use of information

During the Process:

  • ☐ Monitor who speaks and who doesn't - actively invite quieter voices
  • ☐ Acknowledge different perspectives and validate diverse forms of knowledge
  • ☐ Facilitate dialogue, don't dominate or direct conversations
  • ☐ Address power imbalances and exclusion when you see them
  • ☐ Stay neutral on content while ensuring fair process

After the Process:

  • ☐ Ensure communities control decisions about next steps
  • ☐ Share analysis and documentation back to communities
  • ☐ Support communities to act on their own priorities
  • ☐ Reflect on what worked well and what could be improved
  • ☐ Advocate for community priorities with external stakeholders
