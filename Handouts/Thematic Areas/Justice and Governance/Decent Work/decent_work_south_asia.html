What is Decent Work in South Asia?

ILO Definition Applied: Work that provides fair income, workplace security, social protection, better prospects for personal development, freedom of expression, organization, and participation in decisions affecting workers' lives - with special focus on transforming South Asia's vast informal economy.

South Asian Reality: With over 80% workers in informal economy, decent work requires addressing fundamental challenges of social protection, basic rights, and economic security for hundreds of millions.

South Asian Decent Work Challenges

  • Scale of Informality: 80-90% of workers in informal economy across region
  • Agricultural Dependence: 40-60% still employed in agriculture with seasonal vulnerabilities
  • Gender Gaps: Women earn 34% less than men, concentrated in most vulnerable work
  • Youth Unemployment: High rates despite growing education levels
  • Social Exclusion: Caste, religion, ethnicity affect work opportunities and wages

The Four Pillars in South Asian Context

PILLAR 1: EMPLOYMENT CREATION

Goal: Generate quality jobs for South Asia's growing workforce

Employment Challenges in South Asia:

  • Job Creation Gap: Need million new jobs annually to absorb labor force growth
  • Skills Mismatch: Education system not aligned with job market needs
  • Regional Imbalances: Limited opportunities in rural/remote areas
  • Sector Transitions: Moving from agriculture to manufacturing and services

Country Examples:

  • India: Skill India mission, PM Mudra Yojana for micro-enterprises
  • Bangladesh: RMG sector employment for rural women
  • Pakistan: China-Pakistan Economic Corridor job creation
  • Sri Lanka: IT sector development and export processing zones

PILLAR 2: WORKPLACE RIGHTS

Goal: Ensure fundamental labor rights in formal and informal sectors

Rights Violations in South Asian Workplaces:

  • Child Labor: 12 million children in hazardous work in India alone
  • Bonded Labor: Prevalent in brick kilns, agriculture, carpet weaving
  • Workplace Safety: Poor safety standards, industrial accidents
  • Freedom of Association: Limited unionization, especially in informal sector
  • Discrimination: Caste, gender, religion-based exclusion from better jobs

Policy Responses:

  • India: New Labour Codes consolidating 44 laws into 4 codes
  • Bangladesh: Accord on Fire and Building Safety post-Rana Plaza
  • Pakistan: Provincial labor inspection reforms
  • Nepal: New constitution guaranteeing right to employment

PILLAR 3: SOCIAL PROTECTION

Goal: Extend social security to informal workers and vulnerable groups

Social Protection Gaps:

  • Coverage: Only 20-30% of workforce has formal social protection
  • Informal Workers: 73% lack any form of social security
  • Women Workers: Lower access due to informal work concentration
  • Rural Workers: Limited access to healthcare, pensions

Innovative Social Protection Models:

  • India: eShram portal for informal worker registration
  • India: PM-SYM (pension scheme for informal workers)
  • Bangladesh: Health insurance for RMG workers
  • Sri Lanka: Social security scheme for self-employed

PILLAR 4: SOCIAL DIALOGUE

Goal: Strengthen worker voice and representation across sectors

Social Dialogue Challenges:

  • Union Density: Low formal unionization rates (2-5% in most countries)
  • Informal Sector: Lack of traditional collective bargaining structures
  • Gender Representation: Women underrepresented in union leadership
  • Sectoral Gaps: Weak representation in agriculture, domestic work, platform work

Alternative Organizing Models:

  • SEWA India: Union-cooperative model for informal women workers
  • HomeNet South Asia: Home-based workers' network
  • Digital platforms: WhatsApp groups, mobile apps for worker organizing
  • Community organizations: SHGs, cooperatives as worker voice platforms

Sectoral Focus in South Asia

Agriculture (40-60% of South Asian workforce)

Decent Work Challenges:

  • Seasonal unemployment: 4-6 months without regular work
  • Climate vulnerability: Droughts, floods affecting livelihoods
  • Pesticide exposure: Health risks, lack of protective equipment
  • Women's work invisibility: Unpaid family labor not recognized
  • Debt cycles: Crop failure leading to indebtedness

Solutions:

  • MGNREGA: Employment guarantee during lean seasons
  • Crop insurance: PM Fasal Bima Yojana protecting farmers
  • MSP systems: Minimum support prices for key crops
  • FPOs: Farmer Producer Organizations improving bargaining power

Garment Industry (Major employer across region)

Worker Profile:

  • Bangladesh: million workers, 80% women
  • Working conditions: Long hours, workplace safety concerns
  • Wages: Often below living wage standards
  • Rights: Limited collective bargaining, union restrictions

Post-Rana Plaza Improvements:

  • Building safety inspections and remediation
  • Worker safety training programs
  • Grievance mechanisms and worker participation

Domestic Work (Largely invisible sector)

Challenges:

  • Legal recognition: Often excluded from labor laws
  • Workplace abuse: 91% face some form of abuse
  • Wage theft: Delayed or non-payment of wages
  • Social isolation: Working alone in private homes

Organizing Efforts:

  • National Domestic Workers Movement (India): Policy advocacy
  • Legal reforms: Some states recognizing domestic work
  • Technology: Apps connecting workers and employers safely

Gender Dimensions in South Asian Context

Women's Work Patterns in South Asia

Labor Force Participation:

  • India: 23% women's LFPR (one of world's lowest)
  • Bangladesh: 36% LFPR with RMG sector driving growth
  • Pakistan: 25% LFPR with cultural constraints
  • Sri Lanka: 35% LFPR with relatively better conditions

Sectoral Concentration:

  • Agriculture: 75% of women workers, mostly unpaid family labor
  • Home-based work: 50 million women in South Asia
  • Domestic work: Predominantly female with minimal protections
  • Manufacturing: Garments, textiles, food processing

Key Barriers:

  • Care responsibilities: 10x more unpaid care work than men
  • Mobility restrictions: Cultural and safety constraints
  • Skills gaps: Limited access to technical/digital training
  • Workplace harassment: 68% report harassment
  • Wage gaps: 34% lower wages than men

Women's Economic Empowerment Strategies

  • SEWA model: Organizing, financial services, capacity building
  • SHG movement: 12+ million women in self-help groups (India)
  • Digital platforms: Online work opportunities, skill training
  • Policy support: Maternity benefits, childcare provisions
  • Safe transport: Women-only transport options

Migration & Decent Work

Internal Migration Patterns

  • Scale: 450+ million internal migrants in South Asia
  • Seasonal migration: Agriculture to construction, urban services
  • Distress migration: Drought, conflict, economic shocks
  • Circular migration: Temporary moves maintaining rural ties

Migrant Worker Challenges:

  • Documentation: Lack of identity proofs in destination areas
  • Exploitation: Wage theft, poor working conditions
  • Social exclusion: Limited access to healthcare, education
  • Housing: Overcrowded, unhygienic living conditions

COVID-19 Impact:

  • Mass return: Millions of migrants returned to villages
  • Job losses: Disproportionate impact on informal workers
  • Policy response: Employment guarantee, food security measures

Technology & Future of Work in South Asia

Digital Platforms & Gig Economy

Growth Sectors:

  • Ride-hailing: Uber, Ola drivers across major cities
  • Food delivery: Swiggy, Zomato, Foodpanda workers
  • E-commerce: Amazon, Flipkart delivery personnel
  • Digital services: Data entry, content moderation, customer service

Worker Challenges:

  • Classification: Independent contractors vs. employees
  • Social protection: No health insurance, pension rights
  • Algorithmic management: Rating systems affecting earnings
  • Income volatility: Irregular and unpredictable earnings

Organizing Efforts:

  • Driver unions: Protests over fare cuts, working conditions
  • Policy advocacy: Demands for social security coverage
  • Digital organizing: WhatsApp groups, online platforms

Climate Change & Green Jobs

Green Job Opportunities in South Asia

  • Renewable energy: Solar panel manufacturing, installation, maintenance
  • Waste management: Recycling, waste-to-energy projects
  • Sustainable agriculture: Organic farming, water conservation
  • Green construction: Energy-efficient buildings, green infrastructure
  • Forest restoration: Afforestation, ecosystem restoration projects

Just Transition Needs:

  • Coal workers: Reskilling for renewable energy jobs
  • Farmers: Climate-resilient agriculture training
  • Urban workers: Green skills development programs

Major Case Studies from South Asia

SEWA (Self-Employed Women's Association) - India

Founded: 1972 in Gujarat, India

Impact: 2+ million women members across 18 states

Model: Union + Cooperative + Movement

Key Innovations:

  • Organizing informal workers: Street vendors, home-based workers, agricultural laborers
  • Financial inclusion: SEWA Bank providing credit, savings, insurance
  • Capacity building: Training in trades, leadership, technology
  • Policy advocacy: Influencing labor laws, social security policies

Outcomes:

  • Increased earnings and asset ownership among members
  • Better working conditions and social protection access
  • Enhanced women's leadership and decision-making power
  • Policy recognition of informal workers' rights

Bangladesh Garment Industry Transformation

Crisis: Rana Plaza collapse (2013) - 1,134 workers died

Industry Size: million workers, 80% women

Safety Improvements:

  • Accord & Alliance: International safety inspection programs
  • Building improvements: Structural safety, fire safety measures
  • Worker training: Safety awareness, evacuation procedures
  • Grievance mechanisms: Worker complaint systems

Ongoing Challenges:

  • Living wages: Wages still below living wage calculations
  • Union rights: Limited freedom of association
  • Gender-based violence: Harassment and discrimination persist
  • Sustainability: Post-Accord monitoring and compliance

Sri Lankan Tea Industry - MJF Group Initiative

Context: Tea plantation workers, predominantly Tamil estate workers

Challenges: Poor housing, limited healthcare, educational access

Multi-stakeholder Approach:

  • Housing improvement: Better accommodation facilities
  • Healthcare access: On-site medical facilities, health insurance
  • Education: Schools, vocational training, scholarships
  • Worker participation: Estate development committees

Results:

  • Improved living conditions and health outcomes
  • Better educational opportunities for workers' children
  • Enhanced worker voice in estate management
  • Model for industry-wide improvements

Waste Pickers Organization - India

Context: Informal waste collectors in urban areas

Organizing efforts: Kagad Kach Patra Kashtakari Panchayat (Pune)

Achievements:

  • Recognition: Formal recognition as waste management workers
  • Integration: Inclusion in municipal waste management systems
  • Benefits: Identity cards, health insurance, protective equipment
  • Training: Skills development, safety training

Lessons:

  • Community organizing can transform informal work into recognized occupation
  • Environmental services provide pathway to decent work
  • Policy advocacy essential for systemic change

Key Takeaways for South Asia

  1. Informal economy transformation is central to decent work progress in South Asia
  2. Women's economic participation requires addressing care work, safety, and skills gaps
  3. Agricultural workers need seasonal employment support and climate resilience
  4. Migration policies must protect workers moving between states and sectors
  5. Technology offers opportunities but risks creating new forms of precarious work
  6. Social protection extension to informal workers is urgent and feasible
  7. Alternative organizing models (like SEWA) show pathways for worker voice
  8. Regional cooperation needed for migrant worker protection and standards
