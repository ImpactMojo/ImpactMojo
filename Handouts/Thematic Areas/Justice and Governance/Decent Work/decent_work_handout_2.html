Part 1: The Platform Economy - New Work, Old Challenges?

25 minutes

🛵 Three Platform Workers, Different Realities

Amit (Uber Driver, Delhi):

  • Earnings: ₹800-1,200/day (after fuel, maintenance)
  • Hours: 12-14 hours daily to meet targets
  • Flexibility: Can choose when to work, but platform incentives pressure long hours
  • Benefits: None - classified as "partner," not employee
  • Risks: Car loan burden, no insurance, traffic violations

Sneha (Content Creator/Freelancer, Bangalore):

  • Earnings: ₹40,000-80,000/month (irregular)
  • Work: Graphic design, social media management
  • Flexibility: High autonomy, multiple clients
  • Challenges: Payment delays, no contracts, skill upgrading costs
  • Growth: Building personal brand, recurring clients

Ravi (Delivery Worker, Mumbai):

  • Earnings: ₹15,000-18,000/month (food delivery)
  • Conditions: Rain, traffic, customer complaints affect ratings
  • Equipment: Own bike, phone, fuel costs
  • Security: Algorithm-based work allocation, sudden deactivation risks
  • Health: Physical strain, accidents, no health coverage

Platform work offers flexibility but often shifts risks from employers to workers. How do we ensure decent work in this new economy?

Mapping the Platform Economy Landscape

🚗 Location-Based Work

Examples: Ride-hailing, food delivery, home services

Characteristics: Physical presence required, local market, algorithmic management

Workers: 3+ million in India

💻 Web-Based Work

Examples: Freelancing, content creation, virtual assistance

Characteristics: Remote work, global market, skill-based

Workers: 15+ million globally competitive

🏠 Micro-Work

Examples: Data entry, surveys, content moderation

Characteristics: Task-based, low pay, high volume

Workers: Often invisible, difficult to quantify

🎯 Asset-Based Work

Examples: Renting space, vehicles, equipment

Characteristics: Capital investment required, passive income potential

Workers: Often supplement other income

Platform Work vs. Traditional Employment

Traditional Employment

  • Contract: Written employment contract
  • Schedule: Fixed hours, workplace
  • Management: Human supervisors
  • Benefits: EPF, ESI, paid leave
  • Job Security: Notice periods, termination procedures
  • Collective Voice: Union representation possible

Platform Work

  • Contract: Terms of service, "partner" agreements
  • Schedule: Flexible timing, but incentive pressures
  • Management: Algorithmic task assignment, ratings
  • Benefits: Usually none - independent contractor status
  • Job Security: Can be "deactivated" without appeal
  • Collective Voice: Limited - platform policies prohibit organizing

🎯 The Decent Work Challenge: Platform work offers flexibility and income opportunities but often lacks basic protections, stable earnings, and worker voice. The challenge is extending decent work principles without destroying the innovation and efficiency that makes platforms valuable.

Part 2: Problem Set - Wage Analysis and Living Wage Calculations

25 minutes

💰 Advanced Problem Set: Platform Worker Earnings Analysis

Scenario: You're analyzing whether platform work provides decent earnings. Calculate effective hourly wages and compare with living wage benchmarks.

Case Study Data: Food Delivery Workers in Mumbai Worker Profile - Rajesh: • Platform: Swiggy/Zomato • Active hours: 10 hours/day, 26 days/month • Total deliveries/month: 650 • Earnings per delivery: ₹25-40 (average ₹32) • Platform incentives: ₹3,500/month (if >600 deliveries) • Tips: ₹1,200/month average Monthly Costs: • Fuel: ₹4,500 • Bike maintenance: ₹1,200 • Phone/internet: ₹800 • Bike insurance: ₹400 • Safety gear replacement: ₹300 Family Expenses (Mumbai): • Rent (1 BHK outer suburb): ₹12,000 • Food: ₹8,000 • Children's education: ₹3,000 • Healthcare/medicines: ₹2,000 • Transportation (family): ₹1,500 • Utilities: ₹2,000 • Savings target: ₹2,000
Problem 1: Earnings Calculation (8 minutes)

Calculate Rajesh's monthly income statement:

  • Gross delivery earnings: ₹_____ (650 deliveries × ₹32)
  • Platform incentives: ₹_____ (if target met)
  • Tips: ₹_____
  • Total gross income: ₹_____
  • Total work-related costs: ₹_____ (fuel + maintenance + phone + insurance + gear)
  • Net monthly income: ₹_____
  • Total working hours: _____ hours (10 × 26)
  • Effective hourly wage: ₹_____ (net income ÷ hours)
Problem 2: Living Wage Assessment (7 minutes)

Calculate living wage requirements:

  • Total family expenses: ₹_____ per month
  • Required hourly wage for living wage: ₹_____ (expenses ÷ working hours)
  • Income adequacy ratio: _____ (actual income ÷ required income)
  • Monthly income gap: ₹_____ (shortfall or surplus)

Analysis Questions:

  1. Does platform work provide a living wage for this family?
  2. What would need to change to reach living wage? (Higher rates? Lower costs? Fewer hours?)
  3. How does this compare to Mumbai's minimum wage of ₹348/day for unskilled work?
Problem 3: Policy Design Challenge (10 minutes)

Design interventions to improve earnings adequacy:

Option A: Minimum Earnings Guarantee

  • Platform must guarantee ₹100/hour during active time
  • If earnings fall below this, platform tops up the difference
  • Calculate: What would be the monthly top-up cost for Rajesh?

Option B: Social Protection Levy

  • Platform contributes 4% of gross worker earnings to social security fund
  • Covers health insurance (₹300/month) and accident insurance (₹200/month)
  • Calculate: Platform contribution for Rajesh and net benefit to worker

Option C: Collective Bargaining Framework

  • City-wide worker associations negotiate minimum rates
  • Target: ₹40/delivery + ₹50/hour waiting time compensation
  • Calculate: Impact on Rajesh's earnings (assume 2 hours waiting time daily)

Which option would you recommend and why? Consider effectiveness, feasibility, and impact on platform business model.

Part 3: Formalization Strategies - Moving from Informal to Decent Work

20 minutes

Understanding India's Informality Challenge

Sector Informal Employment % Key Challenges Formalization Barriers
Agriculture 98% Small landholdings, seasonal work, low productivity Land records, market access, technology
Construction 93% Migrant workers, project-based work, safety issues Registration complexity, contractor resistance
Domestic Work 100% Invisibility, gender issues, household workplace Legal recognition, employer compliance
Manufacturing 84% Small enterprises, cost pressures, skill gaps Compliance costs, administrative burden
Trade/Commerce 88% Street vendors, small shops, family enterprises Licensing, taxation, space constraints

Formalization Strategy Framework

🎯 Multi-Pronged Approach to Formalization

1. Incentive-Based Measures (Carrots)

  • Tax benefits: Lower tax rates for newly formalized businesses
  • Credit access: Priority lending, collateral-free loans
  • Market access: Government procurement preferences
  • Social protection: Subsidized insurance, pension schemes
  • Skill development: Free training and certification

2. Simplification Measures (Removing Sticks)

  • Single-window clearances: Online registration portals
  • Simplified compliance: Reduced reporting requirements for small enterprises
  • Threshold adjustments: Higher limits before complex regulations apply
  • Presumptive taxation: Fixed tax rates based on turnover

3. Enabling Infrastructure

  • Financial inclusion: Bank accounts, digital payments
  • Technology platforms: e-Shram registration, digital identity
  • Business development services: Incubation, mentoring
  • Physical infrastructure: Industrial parks, markets

4. Targeted Interventions

  • Sector-specific approaches: Tailored for different industries
  • Gender-sensitive measures: Address women's specific constraints
  • Youth focus: First-time entrepreneurs, startup support
  • Migrant worker programs: Portable benefits, inter-state coordination

📊 Case Study Analysis: e-Shram Registration Impact (12 minutes)

Background: e-Shram is India's first national database of unorganized workers, launched in 2021 to provide social security benefits.

e-Shram Performance Data (as of 2024): • Total registrations: 290 million workers • Sectors covered: Construction (35%), Domestic work (18%), Agriculture (22%), Others (25%) • Gender breakdown: Male 52%, Female 48% • Benefits linked: Life insurance (₹2 lakh), accident insurance (₹2 lakh), pension schemes • Average monthly contribution for pension: ₹55-200 • Administrative cost: ₹1,200 crores over 3 years • Benefit claims processed: 180,000 cases • Grievances registered: 450,000

Assessment Questions:

  1. Coverage Analysis: What percentage of India's estimated 400 million informal workers are registered? Is this adequate?
  2. Benefit Uptake: Calculate the claims rate (claims ÷ registrations × 100). What does this suggest about program effectiveness?
  3. Cost-Effectiveness: Calculate cost per registered worker. Is this reasonable for a social protection program?
  4. Gender Inclusion: How does the 48% female registration compare to women's labor force participation (25%)? What explains the difference?
  5. Implementation Challenges: The grievance rate is about 0.15%. What might this indicate about program design or implementation?

Policy Recommendations: Based on this analysis, what 3 specific improvements would you suggest for e-Shram to better promote formalization and decent work?

Part 4: Future of Work - Policy Design for Emerging Challenges

15 minutes

Key Policy Questions for the Future of Work

🏛️ Policy Design Workshop: Portable Social Security System

Challenge: Design a social security system that works for traditional employees, gig workers, informal workers, and the self-employed.

Core Design Principles:
  • Portability: Benefits travel with the worker across jobs and sectors
  • Universality: Coverage for all workers regardless of employment type
  • Adequacy: Meaningful benefits that provide real protection
  • Sustainability: Financially viable funding model
  • Simplicity: Easy to understand and administer

Design Your System (10 minutes):

1. Coverage Scope:

  • Which benefits would you include? (Health, pension, unemployment, accident, maternity)
  • What should be the minimum benefit levels?
  • Should there be different tiers based on contribution capacity?

2. Funding Model:

  • Who contributes what percentage? (Worker, employer/platform, government)
  • How do you handle workers with multiple income sources?
  • What about workers who can't afford contributions?

3. Administration:

  • What technology platform would you use?
  • How would you handle compliance and enforcement?
  • What grievance and appeals process?

4. Implementation Strategy:

  • Which sectors/workers would you pilot with first?
  • What timeline for full rollout?
  • How would you handle transition from existing systems?

Present your 2-minute policy proposal covering the key design elements above.

Key Takeaway

The future of work requires adaptive policy frameworks that can extend decent work principles to new forms of employment while addressing persistent challenges in traditional sectors. Innovation in social protection and worker voice mechanisms is essential.
