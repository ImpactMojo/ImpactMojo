Workshop 1: Power, Institutions & Development Outcomes

Learning Objectives

Part 1: What is Political Economy?

15 minutes

Understanding Political Economy in Development

🎯 Political Economy Definition

Political Economy is the study of how political processes, institutions, and power relationships shape economic outcomes and development trajectories.

Key Questions Political Economy Asks:

  • Who has power and how do they use it?
  • What are the formal and informal rules of the game?
  • Who wins and who loses from current arrangements?
  • How do interests, institutions, and ideas interact?
  • Why do some policies succeed while others fail?

Why Political Economy Matters for Development

📊 Development Outcomes

Political factors often explain why similar economic policies produce different results in different contexts. Understanding power dynamics helps predict and improve development outcomes.

💡 Policy Design

Effective policies must be politically feasible, not just technically sound. Political economy analysis helps design implementable and sustainable interventions.

⚖️ Equity & Inclusion

Development isn't just about growth—it's about who benefits. Political economy reveals how power structures can perpetuate or reduce inequality and exclusion.

  2. Who supported the program? Who opposed it?
  3. What political factors influenced its implementation?
  4. How might political economy analysis have improved the design?

Share one insight with a partner about how politics shaped this program's success or failure

Part 2: Dimensions of Power in Development

20 minutes

Three Faces of Power Framework

🏛️ Visible Power

Observable political processes: Elections, formal decision-making, public debates, policy processes. Easy to see but only part of the story.

🔒 Hidden Power

Behind-the-scenes influence: Agenda-setting, keeping issues off the table, informal networks, corruption. Controls what gets discussed.

🧠 Invisible Power

Shaping consciousness: Social norms, beliefs, ideologies that influence what people think is possible or desirable. Most fundamental level.

🏘️ Case Study: Urban Slum Upgrading in Mumbai

Context: Municipal corporation proposes slum redevelopment project affecting 50,000 residents

Visible Power:

  • Municipal council debates and votes on redevelopment plan
  • Public hearings where residents can voice concerns
  • Media coverage of the development proposal

Hidden Power:

  • Real estate developers lobby politicians behind closed doors
  • Certain community voices excluded from consultation process
  • Alternative development models not considered in official discussions

Invisible Power:

  • Assumption that "development" necessarily means displacement
  • Stigma against slum dwellers affecting their perceived rights
  • Beliefs about what constitutes "proper" urban development

🔍 Power Analysis Exercise (10 minutes)

Working in groups of 3-4, analyze a development issue using the three faces of power:

Choose one scenario:

  • Implementation of digital payment systems in rural areas
  • Introduction of mid-day meal schemes in schools
  • Land acquisition for industrial development
  • Implementation of quota systems in education/employment

For your chosen scenario, identify:

  1. Visible Power: What formal processes and public debates occur?
  2. Hidden Power: What behind-the-scenes influences shape outcomes?
  3. Invisible Power: What assumptions and beliefs affect the issue?

Present: Each group shares one key insight about how different power dimensions affect development outcomes

Part 3: Institutions - The Rules of the Game

20 minutes

Understanding Institutions in Development

🏗️ What Are Institutions?

Institutions are the formal and informal rules, norms, and practices that structure social interactions and shape behavior. They're the "rules of the game" that determine how society operates.

📜 Formal Institutions

Written rules and structures:

  • Constitution and laws
  • Government agencies
  • Electoral systems
  • Courts and legal procedures
  • Regulatory frameworks
🤝 Informal Institutions

Unwritten norms and practices:

  • Caste and social hierarchies
  • Gender roles and expectations
  • Traditional authority structures
  • Clientelism and patronage
  • Community decision-making
💼 Economic Institutions

Market and economic rules:

  • Property rights systems
  • Banking and finance
  • Labor market regulations
  • Trade policies
  • Taxation systems
🏛️ Political Institutions

Governance structures:

  • Democratic vs. authoritarian
  • Federal vs. unitary systems
  • Civil service structures
  • Accountability mechanisms
  • Decentralization frameworks

🇮🇳 Indian Institutional Context

India's Complex Institutional Landscape:

Formal-Informal Interactions:

  • Reservation System: Formal quotas interact with informal caste dynamics
  • Panchayati Raj: Formal local governance meets traditional authority structures
  • Women's Participation: Legal rights vs. social norms and gender expectations
  • Land Rights: Formal titling systems vs. customary land practices

Institutional Challenges:

  • Implementation Gaps: Strong formal institutions, weak implementation capacity
  • Multiple Authorities: Federal structure creates coordination challenges
  • Elite Capture: Formal programs captured by powerful local interests
  • Bureaucratic Culture: Risk-averse administrative culture affects innovation

🏛️ Institutional Mapping Exercise (8 minutes)

Map the institutions affecting women's economic participation in rural India:

Part 4: Political Actors and Interests

15 minutes

Mapping the Political Landscape

🏛️ State Actors

Politicians, bureaucrats, military, police, judiciary - varying interests and capacities

💼 Business/Private

Formal companies, informal enterprises, business associations, professional groups

🤝 Civil Society

NGOs, community organizations, social movements, religious groups, unions

🌍 International

Donors, multilateral agencies, foreign governments, global advocacy networks

👥 Social Groups

Caste communities, religious groups, tribal populations, gender-based groups

🗳️ Political Parties

National parties, regional parties, ideological movements, electoral coalitions

📊 Stakeholder Analysis Framework

For each actor, consider:

Interests: What do they want? What are their goals and motivations?

Influence: How much power do they have to affect outcomes?

Position: Do they support, oppose, or remain neutral on the issue?

Resources: What assets (money, information, legitimacy) do they control?

Relationships: Who are their allies and opponents?

⚡ Case Study: Rural Electrification Politics

Issue: Implementing grid electrification vs. distributed solar energy in rural Rajasthan

Key Actors and Interests:

  • State Electricity Board: Wants to expand grid, protect utility monopoly
  • Solar Companies: Push for distributed generation, market opportunities
  • Rural Communities: Want reliable, affordable electricity for homes and livelihoods
  • Politicians: Seek visible development achievements before elections
  • Environmental Groups: Advocate for renewable energy and sustainability
  • International Donors: Support climate-friendly solutions and innovation

Political Dynamics: How do different power levels and institutional contexts shape which approach gets adopted?

Part 5: Applied Political Economy Analysis

15 minutes

The Policy Process Through a Political Economy Lens

🔄 Political Economy of Policy Making

Problem Definition
→
Agenda Setting
→
Policy Design
→
Implementation
→
Evaluation

Political Economy Questions at Each Stage:

  • Problem Definition: Who defines the problem? Whose voices are heard?
  • Agenda Setting: What issues get priority? Who controls the agenda?
  • Policy Design: Whose interests are served? What coalitions form?
  • Implementation: Who has incentives to implement well? Where are the bottlenecks?
  • Evaluation: Who benefits from success/failure narratives?

🎯 Applied Analysis Exercise (10 minutes)

Apply political economy analysis to a current development challenge:

Choose one issue:

  • Digital India implementation in rural areas
  • National Health Mission service delivery
  • Skill development and employment programs
  • Climate adaptation in agriculture

Analysis Framework:

  1. Power Mapping: Who are the key actors? What are their interests and influence?
  2. Institutional Analysis: What formal and informal rules shape this issue?
  3. Political Economy Diagnosis: What are the main political constraints to progress?
  4. Strategic Recommendations: How could political economy insights improve outcomes?

Output: One key political economy insight that could improve policy design or implementation

Key Takeaway

Development is inherently political. Understanding power, institutions, and political actors isn't just academic—it's essential for designing effective, sustainable, and equitable development interventions.
