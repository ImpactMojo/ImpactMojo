Part 1: What is Political Economy?

20 minutes

🏛️ Core Definition

Political Economy is the study of how political and economic institutions interact to shape policy outcomes, resource allocation, and development trajectories. It examines the incentives, institutions, and power relationships that determine who gets what, when, and how.

The Three Pillars of Political Economy Analysis

🎭 Actors & Interests

Who are the key players?

  • Political leaders and parties
  • Bureaucrats and civil servants
  • Business groups and organized labor
  • Civil society and interest groups
  • International actors (donors, investors)

What do they want? What are their preferences and constraints?

🏢 Institutions & Rules

What are the rules of the game?

  • Constitutional framework
  • Electoral systems and party structures
  • Administrative procedures
  • Informal norms and practices
  • Legal and regulatory framework

How do formal and informal rules shape behavior?

⚡ Power & Incentives

How are decisions actually made?

  • Resource control and access
  • Information asymmetries
  • Coalition building and bargaining
  • Electoral and bureaucratic incentives
  • Rent-seeking opportunities

What motivates different actors? Who has leverage?

💡 Key Insight: Why Good Policies Sometimes Fail

Political economy helps explain why technically sound policies often fail in implementation. Success depends not just on economic efficiency, but on whether the policy is politically feasible given existing institutions, interests, and power dynamics.

Part 2: Indian Political Economy - Historical Foundations

25 minutes

🇮🇳 Case Study: The Political Economy of India's Economic Reforms

The Puzzle: Why did India's economic liberalization happen in 1991, not earlier or later? What explains the particular pattern of reforms?

Pre-1991: The License Raj

  • Institutional Framework: Centralized planning, industrial licensing, import controls
  • Key Actors: Congress party, bureaucratic apparatus, protected industries
  • Incentive Structure: Rent-seeking through licenses, patronage politics, import substitution ideology

Crisis and Reform (1991):

  • External Shock: Balance of payments crisis, gold reserves depleted
  • Political Opening: Minority government, new leadership (Narasimha Rao, Manmohan Singh)
  • Institutional Change: Bureaucratic insulation, technocratic leadership
  • Coalition Dynamics: Crisis created space for reform constituency

🔍 Analysis Exercise: Reform Trajectory (10 minutes)

Question: Why were some sectors (telecommunications, software) liberalized faster than others (agriculture, labor)?

🚀 Fast Reform Sectors

Telecommunications, IT Services, Aviation

  • Limited organized opposition
  • Clear efficiency gains
  • Export potential
  • New technology = new actors
🐌 Slow Reform Sectors

Agriculture, Labor Laws, Land

  • Strong vested interests
  • Electoral consequences
  • State vs. federal jurisdiction
  • Ideological resistance

Reflection: How do different actor configurations and institutional arrangements explain these varying reform speeds? What does this tell us about the political feasibility of future reforms?

🔄 Institutional Complementarity and Path Dependence

Why do institutions persist?

  • Complementarity: Formal and informal institutions reinforce each other
  • Increasing Returns: Existing institutions create vested interests
  • Coordination Problems: Difficult to change multiple institutions simultaneously
  • Legitimacy: Institutions derive power from social acceptance

Example: India's federal structure (formal) interacts with regional party systems (informal) to create particular patterns of coalition politics and policy bargaining.

Part 4: Power Dynamics and Policy Making

15 minutes

Mapping Power: Who Really Decides?

🔺 Three Faces of Power (Lukes Framework)

1. Decision-Making Power (Observable)

  • Who participates in formal policy processes?
  • Who wins when there are conflicts?
  • Indian Example: Parliamentary debates, cabinet decisions

2. Agenda-Setting Power (Mobilization of Bias)

  • Who determines what issues get discussed?
  • What topics are kept off the agenda?
  • Indian Example: Media influence, party manifestos, bureaucratic gatekeeping

3. Ideological Power (Shaping Preferences)

  • Who defines the terms of debate?
  • What counts as legitimate policy options?
  • Indian Example: Development discourse, "Gujarat model," "minimum government, maximum governance"

Part 5: Practical Application - Political Economy Assessment

10 minutes

🛠️ PE Assessment Framework for Policy Analysis

Step 1: Context Analysis

  • Historical background and path dependence
  • Current institutional environment
  • Recent political and economic developments

Step 2: Stakeholder Mapping

  • Identify all relevant actors and their interests
  • Assess their power resources and influence
  • Understand their relationships and coalitions

Step 3: Institutional Analysis

  • Map formal rules and procedures
  • Identify informal norms and practices
  • Analyze incentive structures and constraints

Step 4: Scenario Development

  • Best case: All actors align, institutions support
  • Most likely: Mixed coalition, partial resistance
  • Worst case: Strong opposition, institutional barriers

Step 5: Strategy Design

  • Build supportive coalitions
  • Sequence reforms strategically
  • Address institutional constraints
  • Design implementation mechanisms
