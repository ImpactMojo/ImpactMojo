Part 1: Advanced Analytical Frameworks

25 minutes

Comprehensive Political Economy Analysis

🎯 PESTLE + Power Analysis Framework

Systematic approach combining traditional analysis with power dynamics:

Political: Governance structures, policy processes, political stability

Economic: Growth patterns, inequality, resource distribution

Social: Demographics, social movements, cultural norms

Technological: Digital divides, innovation capacity, tech adoption

Legal: Regulatory frameworks, rule of law, rights protection

Environmental: Climate impacts, resource scarcity, sustainability

+ Power Dynamics: Who benefits? Who decides? Who is excluded?

📊 Political Settlement Analysis

Examines how power is distributed and maintained among elite groups, and how this affects development outcomes and policy implementation.

🔄 Systems Thinking

Analyzes feedback loops, unintended consequences, and complex interactions between political, economic, and social systems.

⚖️ Political Economy of Inequality

Focuses on how power structures create and maintain different forms of inequality (economic, social, political, spatial).

🏥 Case Study: Universal Health Coverage Politics in India

Context: Implementing Ayushman Bharat - world's largest government health insurance scheme

Political Settlement Analysis:

  • Central Government: Seeks electoral benefits from visible health coverage expansion
  • State Governments: Mixed incentives - credit for benefits vs. implementation costs
  • Private Healthcare: Supports scheme due to increased patient volume and guaranteed payments
  • Public Health Advocates: Concerned about privatization of healthcare delivery

Systems Analysis:

  • Feedback Loop: Insurance payments incentivize over-treatment, driving up costs
  • Unintended Consequence: Focus on insurance reduces investment in public health infrastructure
  • Complex Interaction: Federal structure creates coordination challenges between levels

Inequality Analysis:

  • Economic: Scheme reduces financial barriers but quality gaps remain
  • Social: Awareness and access vary by caste, gender, and education
  • Spatial: Urban areas have better provider networks than rural

🔍 Advanced Framework Application (15 minutes)

Apply the PESTLE + Power framework to analyze a development challenge:

  • Implementation of the New Education Policy 2020
  • Digital agriculture extension services rollout

Analysis Template:

  1. PESTLE Analysis: What are the key factors in each dimension?

  3. Systems Thinking: What are the key feedback loops and interactions?

Output: Present one key insight about how advanced analysis reveals non-obvious constraints or opportunities

Part 2: Comparative Political Economy Analysis

20 minutes

Learning from Variation Across Contexts

🔄 Comparative Analysis Value

Why Compare? Understanding variation helps identify what drives success or failure, test theories, and adapt lessons across contexts.

Types of Comparison:

  • Cross-National: Same policy across different countries
  • Sub-National: Variation across states, districts, or communities
  • Temporal: Same context at different time periods
  • Sectoral: How political economy varies across sectors
🌾 Agricultural Reform

Success Case: Punjab Green Revolution

  • Strong state capacity
  • Farmer political organization
  • Elite alignment with reform

Challenge Case: Eastern UP agricultural stagnation

  • Weak extension systems
  • Fragmented land holdings
  • Elite capture of resources
🏫 Education Outcomes

Success Case: Kerala literacy achievement

  • Social movement pressure
  • Political competition on education
  • Women's empowerment

Challenge Case: Bihar education lag

  • Caste-based political fragmentation
  • Teacher absenteeism
  • Elite use of private schools
🏛️ Governance Innovation

Success Case: AP digital governance

  • Political champion commitment
  • Bureaucratic modernization
  • Citizen demand for services

Challenge Case: Traditional governance persistence

  • Rent-seeking incentives
  • Resistance to transparency
  • Limited citizen awareness
💰 Social Protection

Success Case: Tamil Nadu PDS reform

  • Political competition dynamics
  • Universal targeting strategy
  • Technology adoption

Challenge Case: Targeted PDS failures

  • Exclusion errors
  • Corruption in targeting
  • Weak monitoring systems

📋 Comparative Analysis Framework

Factor Success Context Challenge Context Key Difference
Political Leadership Strong champion with long tenure Frequent leadership changes Continuity and commitment
Bureaucratic Capacity Professional, motivated civil service Politicized, demotivated bureaucracy Institutional quality
Social Coalitions Broad-based reform coalitions Elite capture, fragmented groups Inclusiveness of coalitions
Economic Incentives Aligned with political incentives Misaligned incentive structures Incentive compatibility

🔬 Comparative Analysis Exercise (12 minutes)

Compare political economy factors across two contrasting cases:

Choose one comparison:

  • MGNREGA implementation: Rajasthan (success) vs. Jharkhand (challenges)
  • Women's SHG movement: Andhra Pradesh (scale) vs. Other states (limited)
  • Renewable energy adoption: Gujarat (leader) vs. Coal-dependent states
  • Urban governance: Surat (innovation) vs. Traditional city governance

Comparative Framework:

  1. Context Factors: What are the key political, economic, social differences?
  2. Process Differences: How did implementation vary between contexts?
  3. Outcome Variation: What different results were achieved?
  4. Explanatory Factors: Which political economy differences best explain variation?
  5. Lessons: What could the challenging case learn from the success case?

Part 3: Sectoral Political Economy Analysis

25 minutes

Understanding Sector-Specific Dynamics

🏭 Why Sectoral Analysis Matters

Different sectors have distinct political economy characteristics - different actors, interests, institutional arrangements, and reform challenges. Understanding these patterns helps design sector-appropriate strategies.

🌾 Agriculture Sector

Key Actors: Farmers, input suppliers, traders, food processors

Political Economy:

  • Large farmer political influence
  • Subsidy politics and electoral cycles
  • Climate and market risk management
  • Land rights and tenancy issues
🏥 Health Sector

Key Actors: Public providers, private hospitals, pharmaceutical companies, doctors

Political Economy:

  • Professional associations' influence
  • Public-private provision tensions
  • Resource allocation across levels
  • Quality vs. access trade-offs
🎓 Education Sector

Key Actors: Teachers, parents, education bureaucracy, private schools

Political Economy:

  • Teacher union power dynamics
  • Elite flight to private education
  • Federal-state coordination challenges
  • Quality measurement and accountability
⚡ Energy Sector

Key Actors: State electricity boards, private producers, industrial consumers

Political Economy:

  • Subsidy politics and fiscal burden
  • Renewable vs. traditional energy interests
  • Regulatory capture issues
  • Cross-subsidy and pricing politics

💧 Deep Dive: Water Sector Political Economy

Multi-level complexity in Indian water governance:

Institutional Fragmentation:

  • Central Level: Policy formulation, inter-state disputes, major projects
  • State Level: Water resource management, pricing, allocation decisions
  • Local Level: Service delivery, maintenance, user fee collection

Competing Uses and Users:

  • Agricultural: Farmers demand free/cheap water, resist pricing reforms
  • Urban: Middle class wants reliable supply, willing to pay for quality
  • Industrial: Requires large quantities, can pay higher prices
  • Environmental: Ecological needs often marginalized in allocation

Political Economy Challenges:

  • Electoral Politics: Free electricity and water promises common in campaigns
  • Elite Capture: Large farmers and industries get disproportionate allocations
  • Technological Solutions: Drip irrigation adoption blocked by subsidy structures
  • Inter-state Politics: River water disputes create federal tensions

Reform Opportunities:

  • Crisis-driven Change: Severe droughts create windows for reform
  • Technology Integration: Smart meters and digital payment systems
  • Participatory Management: User associations and community ownership
  • Performance Incentives: Linking transfers to service delivery outcomes

Part 4: Politically Informed Programming

20 minutes

From Analysis to Strategy

🎯 Political Economy-Informed Design Principles

Core Principles:

  • Work with the grain: Understand existing incentives and interests rather than fighting them
  • Think politically feasible: Design interventions that can actually be implemented given political constraints
  • Build coalitions: Identify and strengthen reform constituencies
  • Adapt iteratively: Learn from implementation and adjust strategy based on political feedback
  • Address power imbalances: Consider how to include marginalized voices and interests
📊 Program Design

Political Economy Integration:

  • Stakeholder analysis in program logic
  • Political risk assessment
  • Incentive alignment analysis
  • Coalition building strategies
  • Adaptive management approaches
🎯 Implementation Strategy

Politically Smart Approaches:

  • Sequencing reforms strategically
  • Building demonstration effects
  • Creating feedback loops
  • Managing elite resistance
  • Strengthening reform constituencies
📈 Monitoring & Learning

Political Economy Indicators:

  • Stakeholder influence changes
  • Coalition strength measures
  • Institutional change indicators
  • Political risk factors
  • Unintended political consequences
🔄 Adaptive Management

Learning and Adapting:

  • Regular political economy updates
  • Strategy adjustment mechanisms
  • Stakeholder feedback systems
  • Course correction protocols
  • Learning from political failures

📋 Political Economy Assessment Template

Pre-Program Assessment Questions:

  1. Problem Analysis: What political economy factors create/maintain this development problem?
  2. Stakeholder Interests: Who benefits from status quo? Who would benefit from change?
  3. Institutional Analysis: What formal and informal rules affect this issue?
  4. Political Feasibility: What approaches are most likely to be politically acceptable?
  5. Coalition Potential: What reform coalitions could be built or strengthened?
  6. Implementation Challenges: What political obstacles will arise during implementation?
  7. Monitoring Strategy: How will we track political economy changes over time?

🚀 Success Story: Politically Informed SHG Programming

Mahila Shakti program design in Odisha:

Political Economy Analysis:

  • Power Structure: Traditional male-dominated village hierarchies marginalize women
  • Economic Interests: Moneylenders resist financial inclusion for women
  • Cultural Norms: Women's mobility and economic participation restricted
  • Political Opportunity: State government committed to women's empowerment

Politically Informed Design:

  • Work with traditional structures: Engage village elders rather than bypassing them
  • Gradual approach: Start with savings before moving to larger economic activities
  • Build female leadership: Create new power structures through federation building
  • Address male concerns: Include men in awareness activities about women's empowerment benefits

Results: High participation rates, low dropout, sustainable institutions, reduced resistance

Part 5: Practical Application Workshop

20 minutes

🎯 Capstone Exercise: Design Politically Informed Strategy

Apply all frameworks to develop a comprehensive political economy-informed program strategy

🏗️ Strategy Development Workshop (18 minutes)

Working in teams, develop a politically informed strategy for one development challenge:

Challenge Options:

  • Improving maternal health outcomes in high-mortality districts
  • Reducing air pollution in major Indian cities
  • Increasing girls' secondary education completion in rural areas
  • Strengthening primary healthcare systems in remote regions

Strategy Development Process:

Step 1: Political Economy Diagnosis (5 minutes)

  • Who are the key actors and what are their interests?
  • What institutional factors shape this issue?
  • What are the main political constraints to progress?

Step 2: Strategy Design (8 minutes)

  • What approach would be most politically feasible?
  • How would you sequence interventions to build momentum?
  • What coalitions would you try to build or strengthen?
  • How would you address likely sources of resistance?

Step 3: Implementation Plan (5 minutes)

  • What would be your first three actions to test political feasibility?
  • How would you monitor political economy changes?
  • What contingency plans would you have for political setbacks?

Presentation: Each team presents their politically informed strategy highlighting key political economy insights that shaped their approach

Key Takeaway

Effective development practice requires moving beyond technical solutions to politically informed strategies. Understanding power, institutions, and interests isn't just analytical—it's essential for designing implementable and sustainable interventions that create real change.
