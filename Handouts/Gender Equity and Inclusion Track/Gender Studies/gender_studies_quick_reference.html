Gender Studies 101 - Quick Reference

Essential Concepts, Frameworks, and Tools

Core Concepts & Definitions

Sex

Biological characteristics: chromosomes, hormones, anatomy. Examples: male, female, intersex variations.

Gender

Social roles, behaviors, expressions associated with sex. Learned and varies across cultures.

Gender Identity

Internal sense of being male, female, both, neither, or other gender. Personal and innate.

Gender Expression

How gender is presented through clothing, behavior, voice, mannerisms. Can vary by context.

Sexual Orientation

Pattern of romantic/sexual attraction. Independent of gender identity.

Patriarchy

System where men hold primary power in political, social, economic spheres. Affects all genders.

Power Analysis Framework (Rowlands)

Power Over

Domination, control, coercion. Traditional patriarchal power.

Power To

Ability to act, make decisions, solve problems. Individual agency.

Power With

Collective action, solidarity, mutual support. Collaborative power.

Power Within

Self-confidence, dignity, sense of self. Internal strength and recognition.

✓ Intersectional Analysis Questions

  • Which groups are most marginalized in this context?
  • How do different identities create unique experiences?
  • What specific barriers do multiply marginalized groups face?
  • How can interventions address intersecting inequalities?
  • Whose voices are missing from decision-making?

Gender Analysis for Development Programs

🔍 Essential Questions for Any Initiative

  1. Who participates? Who is included/excluded? Who makes decisions?
  2. Who benefits? How are benefits distributed? Who controls resources?
  3. Who bears costs? What are risks and burdens for different groups?
  4. How are power relations affected? Does intervention challenge or reinforce inequalities?
Analysis Level Key Areas to Examine Tools/Methods
Individual Knowledge, skills, confidence, access to resources Surveys, interviews, life histories
Household Division of labor, decision-making, resource control Time-use studies, household surveys
Community Social norms, institutions, leadership structures Focus groups, community mapping
Institutional Policies, laws, organizational practices Policy analysis, institutional assessment

LGBTQIA+ Inclusive Language & Practice

💬 Inclusive Language Tips

  • Use: "Partner" instead of assuming "husband/wife"
  • Use: "Chosen name" and correct pronouns
  • Use: "People of all genders" instead of "both genders"
  • Ask: "What pronouns do you use?" when relevant
  • Avoid: Assumptions about family structures, relationships
  • Create: Safe spaces for disclosure and discussion

Key Terms

Cisgender: Gender identity matches assigned sex
Transgender: Gender identity differs from assigned sex
Non-binary: Gender identity outside male/female binary

Pronouns

She/her: Traditional feminine pronouns
He/him: Traditional masculine pronouns
They/them: Gender-neutral pronouns

Legal Milestones in India

⚖️ Key Legal Developments

  • 2009: Delhi High Court decriminalizes homosexuality (Naz Foundation)
  • 2013: Supreme Court reverses decision, reinstates Section 377
  • 2014: NALSA judgment recognizes transgender rights as fundamental right
  • 2018: Navtej Singh Johar - Section 377 struck down, homosexuality decriminalized
  • 2019: Transgender Persons (Protection of Rights) Act passed
  • Ongoing: Marriage equality cases, conversion therapy bans, workplace discrimination

Feminist Theory Quick Guide

Wave/Approach Focus Key Achievements Limitations
First Wave (1850s-1920s) Legal rights, suffrage Voting rights, property rights Focused on privileged women
Second Wave (1960s-1980s) Workplace equality, reproductive rights Recognition of domestic violence, unpaid work Centered educated, urban women
Third Wave (1990s-2000s) Intersectionality, diversity Multiple identities, global perspectives Sometimes individualistic focus
Current/Fourth Wave Digital activism, trans inclusion #MeToo, online organizing, LGBTQIA+ rights Digital divides, backlash

Red Flags: Recognizing Gender Bias

⚠️ Warning Signs in Programs/Policies

  • Language: "Helping women" vs. "transforming gender relations"
  • Participation: Women included but not in leadership/decision-making
  • Benefits: Benefits flow primarily to men or higher-status women
  • Burden: Programs increase women's workload without addressing root causes
  • Assumptions: Assumes heterosexual nuclear families as norm
  • Intersectionality: Ignores multiple identities and their interactions

Quick Assessment Checklist

✅ Gender-Responsive Program Checklist

  • □ Conducted gender analysis during design phase
  • □ Disaggregated data by gender and other identities
  • □ Meaningful participation of diverse women and gender minorities
  • □ Addressed structural barriers, not just individual capacity
  • □ Considered unpaid care work and time burdens
  • □ Included strategies to address gender-based violence
  • □ Used intersectional analysis for program design
  • □ Monitored differential impacts on various groups
  • □ Challenged harmful gender norms and stereotypes
  • □ Created safe and inclusive spaces for all participants
