Marginalized Identities 101

Advanced Tools for Intersectional Analysis and Inclusive Practice
ImpactMojo Workshop Series • Handout 2: Methods, Advocacy, and Implementation
75-90 Minutes

Workshop Overview

Target Audience: Development practitioners, advocacy professionals, researchers, and community organizers with basic understanding of intersectionality

Prerequisites: Completion of Marginalized Identities 101 - Handout 1 or equivalent knowledge

Materials Needed: Program documents for analysis, laptops/tablets for assessment tools, flip chart paper, sticky notes

Learning Objectives

By the end of this workshop, participants will be able to:

  • Conduct intersectional analysis using systematic assessment tools and frameworks
  • Design inclusive development programs that address multiple forms of marginalization
  • Develop advocacy strategies that build solidarity across identity groups
  • Monitor and evaluate programs using intersectional indicators and methods
  • Build organizational capacity for intersectional practice and allyship

Part 1: Advanced Intersectional Analysis Tools

25 minutes

🎯 Success Story: Inclusive Skill Development in Jharkhand

Challenge: A state skill development program was achieving only 15% women's participation, with almost no Adivasi women and no persons with disabilities enrolled.

Intersectional Analysis Applied:

  • Data Disaggregation: Tracked enrollment and completion by gender, caste, tribe, disability, district
  • Barrier Mapping: Identified transportation costs affecting tribal women, lack of sign language interpreters, timing conflicts with agricultural seasons
  • Stakeholder Analysis: Found male gatekeepers in tribal households, accessible training centers absent in Adivasi areas

Targeted Interventions:

  • Multi-level Engagement: Work with male family members, tribal leaders, and women's groups simultaneously
  • Accessibility Retrofit: Made centers wheelchair accessible, provided assistive technology
  • Flexible Scheduling: Adjusted timings around agricultural calendar, provided childcare
  • Cultural Competency: Hired bilingual trainers from tribal communities

Results After 2 Years: 62% women's participation, 34% tribal women, 8% persons with disabilities

🎯 Hands-On Intersectional Assessment (15 minutes)

Choose a Real Program: Use a development program you're familiar with (education, health, livelihoods, etc.)

Individual Work (8 minutes):

  1. Identity Mapping (3 min): Fill out the intersectional matrix for your program context
  2. Barrier Analysis (5 min): Identify top 3 barriers for the most marginalized intersection you identified

Peer Review (7 minutes):

  • Pair up and share your analysis
  • Ask: "What intersections did I miss?" and "What barriers did I overlook?"
  • Provide feedback on each other's assessment

Part 2: Inclusive Program Design Methods

20 minutes
🎯 Targeted Universalism

Principle: Set universal goals, but use targeted strategies to achieve them

Application: Universal goal of education access, but specific interventions for tribal girls, disabled children, etc.

Example: Sarva Shiksha Abhiyan with special provisions for marginalized groups

🔄 Participatory Co-Design

Principle: "Nothing about us without us" - meaningful participation in all stages

Application: Include marginalized groups as co-designers, not just beneficiaries

Example: Disabled persons designing accessibility features, not just consulting on them

📊 Differential Impact Analysis

Principle: Analyze how interventions affect different identity groups differently

Application: Test program design for unintended negative consequences

Example: Digital payment systems may exclude illiterate women and elderly persons

🌐 Multi-Level Intervention

Principle: Address individual, interpersonal, institutional, and structural levels simultaneously

Application: Combine skill building, attitude change, policy reform, and legal advocacy

Example: Violence prevention: counseling + community engagement + legal reform + police training

Inclusive Design Principles in Practice

🏗️ The ImpactMojo Inclusive Design Framework

1. Center the Most Marginalized

  • Start program design from experiences of those facing multiple discriminations
  • If it works for them, it will work for everyone
  • Example: Designing toilets for women with disabilities ensures they work for all women

2. Build Intersectional Leadership

  • Ensure diverse representation in program governance and decision-making
  • Create pathways for marginalized groups to move into leadership roles
  • Example: Reserve leadership positions for women from SC/ST communities

3. Use Intersectional Data Systems

  • Collect disaggregated data across multiple identity dimensions
  • Monitor outcomes for different intersectional groups
  • Example: Track not just "women's participation" but "Dalit women's participation"

4. Address Multiple Systems of Oppression

  • Recognize that discrimination operates through interconnected systems
  • Design interventions that tackle multiple forms of oppression simultaneously
  • Example: Address both gender norms and caste hierarchies in women's empowerment programs

🎯 Redesigning for Inclusion (12 minutes)

Scenario: A microfinance program has 85% upper-caste women participation. Only 12% are from SC/ST communities, 3% are Muslim women, and no women with disabilities are enrolled.

Small Group Redesign (groups of 4-5, 8 minutes):

  1. Root Cause Analysis (3 min): Why are marginalized women excluded?
    • What structural barriers exist?
    • What cultural/social barriers operate?
    • What individual/household barriers are present?
  2. Intersectional Redesign (5 min): How would you redesign the program?
    • What changes to targeting and outreach?
    • What modifications to program design?
    • What new partnerships or approaches?
    • How would you measure success differently?

Gallery Walk (4 minutes): Post redesign ideas and do a silent gallery walk to see other groups' solutions

Part 3: Advocacy and Organizing Strategies

15 minutes
⚖️ Legal and Policy Advocacy

Strategic Litigation:

  • Test cases challenging intersectional discrimination
  • Public Interest Litigation (PIL) for systemic issues
  • Use of multiple constitutional articles simultaneously

Policy Reform:

  • Advocate for intersectional data collection in government surveys
  • Push for inclusive design in policy formulation
  • Ensure implementation guidelines address multiple marginalities

Success Example: NALSA judgment (2014) recognized transgender rights using gender equality AND dignity principles

🤝 Grassroots Organizing and Alliance Building

Coalition Building:

  • Create alliances across identity groups around shared issues
  • Build solidarity while respecting different experiences
  • Address power dynamics within coalitions

Community Organizing:

  • Train marginalized communities in advocacy skills
  • Support leadership development from within communities
  • Use participatory research to document experiences

Success Example: All India Dalit Women's Rights Forum bringing together caste and gender movements

Building Solidarity Across Differences

🌟 Solidarity-Building Strategies

1. Find Common Ground

  • Identify shared experiences of discrimination (e.g., exclusion from public spaces)
  • Focus on common goals (e.g., dignity, justice, equality)
  • Build campaigns around issues affecting multiple groups

2. Practice Intersectional Allyship

  • Acknowledge privilege within marginalized communities
  • Center voices of those facing multiple oppressions
  • Support without co-opting or speaking over

3. Address Internal Hierarchies

  • Recognize how marginalized groups can oppress each other
  • Create safe spaces for discussing difficult conversations
  • Develop conflict resolution processes

4. Share Resources and Platforms

  • Privileged groups use their platforms to amplify marginalized voices
  • Share funding, networks, and opportunities
  • Create joint programming and mutual support

Part 4: Monitoring and Evaluation with Intersectional Lens

15 minutes

📈 Intersectional M&E Framework

Outcome Indicators (Disaggregated):

Program Goal Standard Indicator Intersectional Indicator
Increased school enrollment % girls enrolled % tribal girls, Dalit girls, Muslim girls, girls with disabilities enrolled
Improved healthcare access % women accessing services % transgender persons, Dalit women, disabled women accessing services
Economic empowerment % increase in women's income % increase in income for women by caste, religion, disability status
Political participation % women in leadership % SC/ST women, Muslim women, women with disabilities in leadership

Process Indicators:

  • Participation Quality: Are marginalized groups meaningfully involved in decision-making?
  • Accessibility: Are services accessible to people with different abilities, languages, locations?
  • Safety: Do marginalized groups feel safe participating in programs?
  • Cultural Relevance: Are interventions culturally appropriate for different communities?

✅ Intersectional Program Assessment Checklist

Design Phase:

Conducted intersectional barrier analysis
Included marginalized groups in program design
Planned targeted strategies for different identity groups
Designed intersectional data collection systems

Implementation Phase:

Monitor participation across identity intersections
Track differential outcomes by identity groups
Regular feedback from marginalized participants
Address emerging barriers promptly

Evaluation Phase:

Analyze results using intersectional lens
Document lessons learned about inclusive practice
Share findings with marginalized communities first
Use results to improve future programming

Key Takeaway

Effective intersectional practice requires systematic analysis, inclusive design methods, solidarity-building approaches, and comprehensive monitoring that tracks outcomes across identity intersections. The goal is not just inclusion, but transformation of systems that create and maintain multiple oppressions.
