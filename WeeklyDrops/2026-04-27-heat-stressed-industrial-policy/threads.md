# Threads post — The Heat Floor

Long-form Threads post (single thread, ~8–10 posts). Evidence-led, citation-dense, ImpactMojo voice.

---

**Post 1/9** *(hook)*

The World Bank just told South Asia to do more industrial policy.

The heat-productivity literature says informal workers — the ones manufacturing is supposed to absorb — are 17× more exposed to heat-stressed earnings loss.

Both can't be right. 🧵

---

**Post 2/9** *(set up the WB)*

April 8, 2026. The Bank's *South Asia Economic Update* drops. Headline: growth slowing to 6.3% in 2026 from 7%.

The chapter people argued about: industrial policy.

South Asia uses IP at **2× the rate** of other emerging markets. Half goes to manufacturing.

[1] worldbank.org/en/news/press-release/2026/04/08/south-asia-economic-update-april-2026

---

**Post 3/9** *(the quiet finding)*

The Bank's own data has a quieter number underneath.

Services — not manufacturing — drives **net non-farm job creation** in South Asia.

But services has barely been the target of industrial policy. The mismatch is structural. The Bank flags it. Doesn't fully resolve it.

---

**Post 4/9** *(the heat number, headline)*

Here's what the report doesn't measure.

A 2025 *Nature* paper found India loses ~**259 billion labor hours/year** to heat (2001–2020 avg).

A 1°C rise in mean temp → **−16% net earnings**.
A 1°C rise in wet-bulb temp → **−19% earnings**.

[2] nature.com/articles/d44151-025-00193-0

---

**Post 5/9** *(the distributional finding — the 17×)*

The headline distributional fact:

Informal-sector workers in South India are **17× more likely** to experience heat-related productivity loss than formal-sector workers exposed to the **same temperature**.

During heatwaves, informal earnings dropped **40%**.

These are the workers manufacturing is supposed to absorb.

---

**Post 6/9** *(the JPE foundational evidence)*

This isn't new. Somanathan, Somanathan, Sudarshan & Tewari (JPE 2021) showed it five years ago using worker-day-level data from Indian manufacturing:

— Hot days reduce within-shift productivity ✓
— Hot days reduce attendance ✓
— Climate-control investments only **partially** offset the loss ✓

[3] journals.uchicago.edu/doi/full/10.1086/713733

The labor and IP literatures don't talk to each other.

---

**Post 7/9** *(the synthesis — three redesigns, part 1)*

If heat is the binding constraint on the IP bet — not weather, not externality, but the floor — three design changes follow.

**1. Cooling infrastructure as industrial subsidy.**

Make factory cooling, ventilation, worker shelters, shaded transit eligible PLI/PSI inputs. If uncooled lines lose 5–8% of output, the math works in 3 years.

---

**Post 8/9** *(redesigns 2 and 3)*

**2. Follow the jobs engine into services.**

Services drives net jobs. Most services work is indoor and heat-resilient. IP keeps targeting manufacturing because it's politically legible. The jobs aren't there.

**3. Parametric heat-day wage insurance for informal workers.**

Top-up on wet-bulb days above a threshold. IMD station data triggers payout automatically. Costed against GDP loss, pays for itself. MGNREGA-complement, not competitor.
