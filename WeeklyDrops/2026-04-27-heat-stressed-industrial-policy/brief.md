# Weekly Drop Brief — 2026-04-27

## Topic

**Industrial Policy in a Heat-Stressed Economy: Why South Asia's Manufacturing Bets Miss the Climate Floor**

The World Bank's *South Asia Economic Update* (April 2026) makes industrial policy the centrepiece of its jobs argument: South Asia runs industrial policy at roughly 2× the rate of other emerging markets, and ~half is aimed at manufacturing. Meanwhile, the heat-productivity literature is converging on an uncomfortable finding — informal-sector workers in South India are 17× more likely to lose earnings on heat-stressed days than formal-sector workers, India loses ~259 billion labor hours/year to heat, and 1°C of mean-temperature rise cuts net earnings 16%. The angle: industrial policy that doesn't budget for the heat floor is building factories where the workforce can't actually show up.

## Why this matters now

- Pre-monsoon heatwave season just opened across the Indo-Gangetic plain (late April).
- The WB report dropped April 8, 2026 — fresh primary source.
- Industrial-policy is the active policy debate in India (PLI scheme review), Bangladesh (post-LDC industrial strategy), and Sri Lanka (IMF-program restructuring).

## Sources

1. **World Bank** — *South Asia Economic Update, April 2026: Working with Industrial Policy* (April 8, 2026)
   - Takeaway: Growth slows to 6.3% in 2026 from 7% in 2025. South Asia uses industrial policy ~2× as intensively as other EMs. Half targets manufacturing — but **services drives net job creation**. Import-restricting measures shrink imports; export-promoting measures don't measurably grow exports.
   - URL: https://www.worldbank.org/en/news/press-release/2026/04/08/south-asia-economic-update-april-2026

2. **Nature (Singh, Aggarwal et al.)** — *"Heatwaves deepen India's economic divide"* (2025)
   - Takeaway: Informal-sector workers in South India are **17× more likely** to experience heat-related productivity loss than formal-sector workers at the same temperature. During heatwave periods, informal earnings dropped **40%**. A 1°C rise in mean temperature → **−16% net earnings**; 1°C wet-bulb → **−19% earnings**.
   - URL: https://www.nature.com/articles/d44151-025-00193-0

3. **Somanathan, Somanathan, Sudarshan, Tewari** — *"The Impact of Temperature on Productivity and Labor Supply: Evidence from Indian Manufacturing"*, *Journal of Political Economy* 129(6), 2021
   - Takeaway: Hot days cut **manufacturing output** materially (worker-level absence + within-shift productivity loss). The effect persists after climate-control investments — i.e., adaptation is partial. Foundational econometric evidence behind the Nature paper's macro estimate.
   - URL: https://www.journals.uchicago.edu/doi/full/10.1086/713733

## Angle (the ImpactMojo POV)

The three sources don't sit in the same room. The WB document discusses industrial policy as if labor were temperature-invariant. The Nature paper documents the heat shock but doesn't connect to the industrial-policy lever. The JPE paper provides the micro-econometrics but is now five years old and pre-dates the latest WB framing.

**Our synthesis:** if industrial policy in South Asia keeps targeting manufacturing without accounting for a heat floor that already costs ~5% of GDP-equivalent labor hours, the marginal return on industrial subsidies is overstated. Three concrete asks:

1. **Cooling infrastructure as industrial subsidy.** Treat factory-floor cooling, ventilation, and worker shelters as eligible PLI/PSI inputs.
2. **Services-sector targeting.** Where services work is heat-resilient (indoor, AC-eligible), industrial policy should follow the actual jobs engine — not the legacy manufacturing reflex.
3. **Heat-indexed wage insurance for informal workers.** Pay parametric on wet-bulb days. Cheaper than the GDP loss; politically tractable post-MGNREGA.
