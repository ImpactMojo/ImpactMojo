## [00:14 — 01:30] PROBLEM — "The report everyone read"

> *(VISUAL: B-roll of factories, ports, PLI announcement. Cut to the WB report cover.)*

**Narrator:** "Every six months, the World Bank publishes the South Asia Economic Update. The April 2026 edition has a headline number — growth slowing to 6.3 percent — and a longer chapter that policy circles actually argue about. It's about industrial policy.

The data is striking. South Asia uses industrial policy at roughly twice the rate of other emerging markets. Production-linked incentives, tariff carve-outs, special economic zones — the full toolkit. Half of it goes to manufacturing.

The report's verdict is cautiously affirming. Industrial policy can work. So let's get better at it.

But there's a quieter finding underneath. The sector that creates the most non-farm jobs in South Asia isn't manufacturing. It's services. And services has barely been touched by industrial policy."

## [01:30 — 03:00] AGITATE — "The number industrial policy skips"

> *(VISUAL: Animated map of India, heat overlay pulsing through April-June. Then cut to a Nature paper screenshot.)*

**Narrator:** "Last year, *Nature* published a study with the cleanest macro estimate we have for what heat does to Indian labor. Between 2001 and 2020, India lost roughly 259 billion working hours per year to heat. That's not a typo. 259 billion.

A one-degree rise in mean temperature cut net earnings by 16 percent. A one-degree rise in *wet-bulb* temperature — the measure that captures humidity-stressed cooling — cut earnings by 19 percent.

But the distributional finding is the one that should change how you think about industrial policy. Informal-sector workers in South India were 17 times more likely to lose earnings on heat-stressed days than formal-sector workers exposed to the same temperature. During heatwave periods, informal earnings dropped 40 percent.

These aren't the workers in the air-conditioned factory. These are the workers building it. Or the workers manufacturing is supposed to absorb."

**Narrator:** "Now overlay this on the manufacturing map."

## [03:00 — 04:30] AGITATE II — "The foundational paper"

> *(VISUAL: JPE paper screenshot, then a hand-drawn diagram of the worker-day data: temperature axis, output axis, scatter cloud.)*

**Narrator:** "This isn't a new finding. In 2021, four economists — Somanathan, Somanathan, Sudarshan, and Tewari — published a paper in the *Journal of Political Economy* using worker-day-level data from Indian manufacturing.

They found two things. Hot days reduce within-shift productivity. And hot days reduce attendance. Workers don't show up.

Crucially — and this is the part to remember — climate-control investments only *partially* offset the loss. Fans, partial cooling, even ventilation upgrades. Adaptation is real. But adaptation is incomplete.

That paper has been cited in nearly every climate-and-labor working paper since. It has not been cited, as far as we can tell, in any major industrial-policy document from the region. The two literatures don't talk to each other.

So when a policy document tells you industrial policy will create jobs, ask one question."

**[HOOK at 04:30]** *(VISUAL: cut to a close-up of a wet-bulb thermometer)*
**Narrator:** "What does that buy you if labor is wet-bulb-bounded?"

## [04:30 — 07:30] SOLVE — "Three redesigns"

> *(VISUAL: A hand-drawn flowchart appearing piece by piece — three boxes labelled with the three redesigns.)*

**Narrator:** "If you accept that heat is the binding constraint, not the weather and not an externality, three design changes follow.

**One. Cooling infrastructure as industrial subsidy.**"

> *(VISUAL: factory schematic with cooling units being added in animation)*

"Make factory-floor cooling, ventilation, worker shelters, and shaded transit eligible inputs under PLI, PSI, and state industrial-subsidy schemes. The cost-benefit is straightforward. If uncooled lines lose 5 to 8 percent of output to heat, a subsidy that pays back cooling capex in three years is competitive with most existing industrial sweeteners. The instrument exists. Just expand its definition.

**Two. Follow the jobs engine into services.**"

> *(VISUAL: bar chart — services bar growing, manufacturing bar steady)*

"The Bank's own data says services drives net job creation. Most services work is indoor and heat-resilient. The reflex to keep targeting manufacturing is partly historical, partly political. Manufacturing is legible. Voters see it. Ribbon-cuttings work. But if industrial policy is supposed to be employment policy in disguise, then it needs to follow where the employment actually grows.

**Three. Parametric heat-day insurance for informal workers.**"

> *(VISUAL: a wage slip with a "heat-day top-up" line item)*

"Pay informal workers a wage top-up on wet-bulb days above a threshold. Index it automatically from India Meteorological Department station data. Parametric, fast-payout, politically tractable as an MGNREGA complement rather than a competitor. Costed against the GDP-equivalent loss, it pays for itself. The design literature exists — the Handbook of Social Protection has it. The political moment exists too.

None of these three are invented. The pieces are all there. They just haven't been put on the same desk."
