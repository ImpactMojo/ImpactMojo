The Heat Floor

Why South Asia's industrial-policy debate skips the one number that already decides who shows up to work.

The report everyone read

On April 8, the World Bank released its South Asia Economic Update. The headline was the slowdown — growth dropping to 6.3% in 2026 from 7% in 2025. The chapter that policy circles actually argued about was the one on industrial policy.

The data is striking. South Asia runs industrial policy at roughly twice the rate of other emerging markets. About half of those policies target manufacturing — production-linked incentives, tariff carve-outs, special economic zones, the usual stack. The Bank's tone is cautiously affirming: industrial policy can work, especially when aimed at activities with better-paid employment, so let's get better at it.

There's a quieter finding tucked underneath. The sector that actually generates net new non-farm jobs in South Asia isn't manufacturing. It's services. And services has rarely been the target of industrial policy. That's a structural mismatch the Bank flags, but doesn't fully resolve.

Here's what the report doesn't measure: what happens to all of this when the workforce can't physically be on the factory floor for half of April, May, and June.

The number industrial policy skips

A 2025 paper in Nature — "Heatwaves deepen India's economic divide" — gave us the cleanest macro estimate to date. Between 2001 and 2020, India lost roughly 259 billion labor hours per year to heat. A 1°C rise in mean temperature cut net earnings by 16%. A 1°C rise in wet-bulb temperature — the measure that captures humidity-stressed cooling capacity — cut earnings by 19%.

The distributional finding is sharper. Informal-sector workers in South India were 17 times more likely to experience heat-related productivity loss than formal-sector workers exposed to the same temperatures. During heatwave periods, informal-sector earnings dropped 40%.

Now overlay this onto the manufacturing map.

What the foundational paper said five years ago

This isn't a new finding. Somanathan, Somanathan, Sudarshan, and Tewari, writing in the Journal of Political Economy in 2021, used worker-day-level data from Indian manufacturing to show two things: (1) hot days reduce within-shift productivity, and (2) hot days reduce attendance. Crucially, climate-control investments — fans, partial cooling, ventilation upgrades — only partially offset the productivity loss. Adaptation is real, but it's incomplete.

That paper has been cited in every climate-and-labor working paper since. It has not, as far as we can tell, been cited in any major industrial-policy white paper from the region. The literatures don't talk to each other.

So what does industrial policy actually buy you if the labor input is wet-bulb-bounded?

Three redesigns

If you accept that heat is the binding constraint on the industrial-policy bet — not weather, not an externality, but the floor — three design changes follow.

1. Cooling infrastructure as industrial subsidy

Make factory-floor cooling, ventilation, worker shelters, and shaded transit eligible inputs under PLI, PSI, and state-level industrial subsidies. The cost-benefit is straightforward: if uncooled lines lose 5–8% of output to heat, a subsidy that pays back cooling capex in three years is competitive with most existing industrial sweeteners.

2. Follow the jobs engine into services

The Bank's own data says services drives net job creation. Most services work is indoor and heat-resilient. The reflex to keep targeting manufacturing is partly historical and partly political — manufacturing is legible, voters see it, ribbon-cuttings work. But if industrial policy is supposed to be employment policy in disguise, then the policy needs to follow where the employment actually grows.

3. Parametric heat-day insurance for informal workers

Pay informal workers a wage top-up on wet-bulb days above a threshold, indexed automatically from IMD station data. This is parametric, fast-payout, and politically tractable as an MGNREGA complement rather than competitor. Costed against the GDP loss, it pays for itself.

None of this is invented. The pieces exist. They just haven't been put on the same desk.
