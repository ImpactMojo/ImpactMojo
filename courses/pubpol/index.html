Why Study Public Policy?

Development practice happens inside policy systems. Understanding how those systems work is not optional.

India's policy landscape is among the most complex in the world. A single social programme might be designed by the Union government, funded through a Finance Commission formula, implemented by state bureaucracies, delivered by district officials who are simultaneously accountable to multiple levels of government, monitored by an audit institution that reports to Parliament, and litigated before courts that have their own interpretive frameworks. Practitioners who understand only the programme — but not the system — are operating blind.

This course builds that systemic understanding. It covers the full architecture: how the Indian state is structured, how policy moves from idea to implementation, where public money comes from and how it gets spent, how regulatory bodies actually function, and why some reforms succeed while most stall. Every module is grounded in real Indian evidence — not generic policy frameworks imported from the West.

Fiscal Architecture
Understand where government money comes from, how it is divided between Centre and states, and why budget numbers rarely match on-ground reality.
Institutional Reality
Go beyond formal rules to understand how the IAS, regulators, courts, and political systems actually function in practice.
Reform Political Economy
Why do good policies fail? Why do bad ones persist? Learn to read the incentive structures that determine policy outcomes.
Evidence-Based Practice
Use CAG reports, Finance Commission awards, PRS data, and evaluation evidence to assess what works in Indian governance.
"The central issue of development is not whether to have planning or the market, but how to build the state capacity that makes either work."
— Pranab Bardhan, The Political Economy of Development in India

Module 01 — Foundations

What is Public Policy?

Why policy matters, what distinguishes it from politics, and the tools governments use to change social outcomes.

Essential Reading
Howlett, M., Ramesh, M. & Perl, A. (2009). Studying Public Policy, Chapters 1–3. Oxford University Press.
Bardhan, P. (1998). The Political Economy of Development in India, Chapters 1–2.
Stiglitz, J. (2000). Economics of the Public Sector, Chapter 3 (Market Failures).
Varna
Practitioners often come to policy analysis with programme-level knowledge but without the systemic frame. This module is foundational — if you find the market failure / government failure framework abstract, I'd be glad to work through a specific programme from your sector in a coaching session.
Book 1:1 Coaching Interactive Labs
Check Your Understanding
Multiple Choice
1Which of the following best describes a "public good" in the economic sense?
A public good has two properties: non-excludability (you cannot stop free-riders from benefiting) and non-rivalry (one person's use doesn't reduce availability for others). National defence and flood control are classic examples. Government-subsidised goods are not public goods in this technical sense.
Multiple Choice
2The "policy cycle" model is useful for organising concepts but empirically misleading because:
The policy cycle is a heuristic, not a description of reality. In practice, India has implemented programmes before fully formulating them (RTE), evaluated programmes mid-implementation (NREGA), and revisited agenda-setting after failed implementation (Direct Benefit Transfer design). The model's value is analytical, not predictive.
Multiple Choice
3Rent-seeking, as described by Bardhan in the Indian context, refers to:
Rent-seeking describes socially wasteful competition for policy-created rents — industrial lobbying for import licences, contractors bribing procurement officials, farmers' organisations blocking subsidy rationalisation. Bardhan's contribution was to show how India's dominant proprietary classes turned the planning apparatus into a vehicle for capturing rents.
Reflection
4Choose a policy problem you work on — say, low immunisation rates in a district, or high open defecation in peri-urban areas. Identify: (a) what market failure justifies intervention, (b) what government failure risks must be managed, and (c) which of the five policy instruments would you choose and why.
There is no single correct answer. The quality of your analysis depends on specificity — name the exact failure mechanism, the likely bureaucratic risk, and the instrument fit.
Sources: Howlett, Ramesh & Perl (2009); Bardhan (1998); Stiglitz (2000); Buchanan & Tullock (1962) The Calculus of Consent; PRS India legislative database.
Module 02 — Foundations

The Indian State: Architecture & Capacity

How the Indian state is structured, where its capacities lie, and where it characteristically fails.

Essential Reading
Pritchett, L. (2009). "Is India a Flailing State?" HKS Faculty Research Working Paper RWP09-013.
Kapur, D. & Mehta, P.B. (2017). "The Indian State" in Rethinking Public Institutions in India. OUP.
Iyer, L. & Mani, A. (2012). "Traveling Agents: Political Change and Bureaucratic Turnover in India." Review of Economics and Statistics.
Vandana Soni
The "flailing state" concept often surprises practitioners who have only seen either high-performing or dysfunctional parts of the system. Working through how your own organisation navigates this bifurcation — leveraging elite capacity while managing frontline delivery gaps — is worth a dedicated conversation.
Book 1:1 Coaching Interactive Labs
Check Your Understanding
Multiple Choice
1Under India's Seventh Schedule, education falls in the:
Education was originally in the State List but was moved to the Concurrent List by the 42nd Constitutional Amendment (1976). This is why both the Union government (through the Right to Education Act) and state governments (through state education acts) can legislate on education, and why the NEP 2020 requires state adoption and implementation even though it was a central policy.
Multiple Choice
2Pritchett's "flailing state" concept describes India as:
Pritchett's metaphor is of a body whose head is functioning but whose arms and legs are unreliable. India has elite-level competence in ISRO, IITs, RBI, and macro-economic management — but systematically fails at the "last mile" of delivering education, health, and welfare services. This bifurcation, not overall weakness, is what distinguishes a flailing state from a failed one.
Reflection
3The IAS rotation policy means officers rarely stay in a department long enough to develop expertise or be held accountable for outcomes. What are the strongest arguments for and against replacing the generalist model with specialist cadres in sectors like health and education? What political economy obstacles would any reform face?
Consider how the IAS's generalist identity intersects with its role as an elite service and its relationships with political principals.
Sources: Pritchett (2009) HKS; Kapur & Mehta (2017) OUP; Iyer & Mani (2012) REStat; Ministry of Personnel Annual Reports; Supreme Court PIL jurisprudence.
Module 03 — Foundations

History of Indian Planning

From Nehruvian central planning to NITI Aayog — and the question of what, if anything, replaced planning.

Essential Reading
Srinivasan, T.N. & Bhagwati, J. (1993). India's Economic Reforms, Chapters 1–3. Oxford University Press.
Panagariya, A. (2008). India: The Emerging Giant, Chapters 2–4. Oxford University Press.
Rajaraman, I. (2012). "The Finance Commission." in Kapur & Mehta (eds.) Public Institutions in India.
Varna
The transition from the Planning Commission to NITI Aayog is still working itself out institutionally. If you're working in a sector affected by Centrally Sponsored Schemes — and most development practitioners are — understanding what changed in 2015 is directly relevant to your funding architecture. Happy to discuss this in depth.
Book 1:1 Coaching Interactive Labs
Check Your Understanding
Multiple Choice
1The Mahalanobis model for India's Second Five Year Plan prioritised:
Mahalanobis drew on a Soviet-inspired two-sector model: invest heavily in capital goods (machines to make machines) first, which enables greater consumer goods production later. This logic justified steel mills, heavy engineering, and public sector dominance. Its weakness was neglecting agriculture (leading to food crises) and generating urban industrial growth without broadly-shared consumption gains.
Multiple Choice
2The key difference between NITI Aayog and the Planning Commission is that NITI Aayog:
This is the central distinction. The Planning Commission had direct budget authority — it could allocate Plan expenditure to states and ministries. NITI Aayog is an advisory and strategy body. Resource allocation now happens through the Finance Commission (tax devolution), Finance Ministry (budget allocations), and Centrally Sponsored Schemes. NITI produces strategy but controls no money.
Reflection
3The Planning Commission was created by executive order and had no constitutional basis. NITI Aayog was also created by executive order. What does this tell us about how India creates — and destroys — powerful policy institutions? What are the risks of institutional structures that depend on political will rather than constitutional entrenchment?
Consider also the contrast with the Finance Commission, which is constitutionally mandated under Article 280 and therefore cannot be abolished by executive order.
Sources: Srinivasan & Bhagwati (1993); Panagariya (2008); Rajaraman (2012); Planning Commission Five Year Plan documents; NITI Aayog Three-Year Action Agenda (2017–18 to 2019–20); RBI Handbook of Statistics.
Module 04 — Policy Process

How Policy Gets Made in India

The formal and informal channels through which ideas become law, budgets, and administrative action.

Essential Reading
Kingdon, J.W. (2003). Agendas, Alternatives, and Public Policies, Chapters 1–2. Longman.
Jha, P. (2019). "The Parliament and Public Policy" in Kapur & Mehta (eds.) Rethinking Public Institutions in India.
PRS India Legislative Research (prsindia.org) — parliamentary productivity reports.
Vandana Soni
Many CSO practitioners want to influence policy but don't know where to intervene in the process. The Standing Committee stage is often the most accessible — public consultations are sometimes held, and committee reports are publicly available and actually read by bureaucrats. Let's discuss entry points relevant to your work.
Book 1:1 Coaching Interactive Labs
Check Your Understanding
Multiple Choice
1Kingdon's "policy window" concept argues that policy change happens when:
Kingdon argues that problems do not automatically generate solutions — policy change requires three streams to converge: a recognised problem, a politically receptive environment, and a workable solution already "floating" in the policy community. The 1991 BOP crisis (problem) + Congress mandate + pre-existing liberalisation proposals (solution) created India's reform window. Any one stream alone is insufficient.
Multiple Choice
2The most significant criticism of India's increasing use of ordinances for major policy changes is:
Ordinances are constitutionally valid but their overuse bypasses the scrutiny that Parliamentary committees provide. The Land Acquisition Amendment Ordinance, for example, was promulgated three times when it couldn't pass Parliament — effectively circumventing the legislature's verdict. The Standing Committee process, imperfect as it is, does catch errors, incorporate feedback, and build consensus in ways that ordinance-route legislation misses.
Reflection
3Choose a policy change you believe is needed in your sector. Map it through Kingdon's three-stream framework: What is the problem as currently understood by policy-makers? What political conditions would make action feasible? What solutions are currently "floating" in the policy community? Where are the gaps, and what would you need to do to help streams converge?
Be specific about who the political entrepreneurs are, what the policy community looks like, and what a realistic political window might be.
Sources: Kingdon (2003); PRS India Parliamentary Productivity Reports; Jha (2019) in Kapur & Mehta; NITI Aayog advisory committee records; Cabinet Secretariat procedural documentation.
Module 05 — Policy Process

Federalism & Centre-State Relations

India's quasi-federal design, the Finance Commission, GST, and the ongoing contest between central control and state autonomy.

Essential Reading
Rao, M.G. & Singh, N. (2005). The Political Economy of Federalism in India. OUP.
15th Finance Commission Report (2020). Finance Commission in Covid Times. Government of India.
Khanna, G. & Mukherjee, P. (2021). "GST and Fiscal Federalism." NIPFP Working Paper.
Varna
The CSS architecture directly affects how development organisations work — conditionalities shape what programmes can and cannot do. If you're navigating Centre-state funding structures in your work, we can walk through the fiscal architecture of a specific scheme relevant to your sector.
Book 1:1 Coaching
Check Your Understanding
Multiple Choice
1Southern states' criticism of the Finance Commission horizontal devolution formula centres on:
The population criterion in horizontal devolution historically used 1971 population data (which disadvantaged states that had reduced fertility faster). Later FCs moved to 2011 data, which still penalises states like Tamil Nadu, Kerala, and Karnataka that achieved demographic transition earlier. These states argue: we invested in education and family welfare, we should not be penalised for the resulting smaller populations.
Multiple Choice
2The GST Council is significant for Indian federalism because:
The GST Council (Article 279A) is chaired by the Union Finance Minister with state Finance Ministers as members. Decisions require a three-quarters supermajority, with the Centre holding one-third of votes and states collectively two-thirds. This structure gives states real (though not equal) power — no rate change can happen without substantial state consent. It is, for all its flaws, a genuine cooperative federal institution.
Reflection
3Kerala has achieved health outcomes comparable to many middle-income countries while having a lower per-capita income than several Indian states. What combination of fiscal, institutional, and political factors explains this? What would need to change for the Kerala model to be replicable in Bihar or Uttar Pradesh?
Consider fiscal capacity (own-tax revenues, Finance Commission allocations), political economy (Left parties' role in social sector investment), civil society strength, and initial literacy levels.
Sources: Rao & Singh (2005); 14th and 15th Finance Commission Reports; Ministry of Finance CSS rationalisation documents; GST Council proceedings; NIPFP fiscal federalism working papers.
Module 06 — Policy Process

Bureaucracy & Implementation

Why the same law produces different outcomes across Indian states — and what determines frontline delivery quality.

Essential Reading
Lipsky, M. (1980). Street-Level Bureaucracy. Russell Sage Foundation. (Introduction and Chapter 1)
Iyer, L. & Mani, A. (2012). "Traveling Agents." Review of Economics and Statistics 94(3).
Muralidharan, K. et al. (2016). "Building State Capacity: Evidence from Biometric Smartcards in India." American Economic Review.
Vandana Soni
Implementation failure is the most common frustration I hear from practitioners. The Lipsky framing — that frontline workers are making policy, not just executing it — reframes the problem productively. Rather than blaming individuals, we ask: what are the accountability structures around discretion? Let's work through this for your programme context.
Book 1:1 Coaching
Check Your Understanding
Multiple Choice
1Lipsky's concept of "street-level bureaucracy" is important for Indian development practice because:
Lipsky's insight reorients implementation analysis. If frontline workers are making policy, then improving implementation requires changing their incentives, accountability structures, and working conditions — not just writing better rules. An ASHA worker who skips marginalised households is making a policy decision; the question is what would change that decision.
Multiple Choice
2Iyer and Mani (2012) found that high IAS officer transfer rates in Indian districts:
Iyer and Mani's empirical finding confirms the intuition: administrative continuity matters for implementation quality. Officers who know they will be transferred before a programme matures have reduced incentives to invest in it. Political control of postings — using transfers as rewards and punishments — creates this instability. The finding has direct policy implications for civil service reform.
Reflection
3You are designing a monitoring system for a government health programme where ASHA workers make critical decisions about which households to prioritise. Using the street-level bureaucracy framework, what accountability mechanisms would you design to reduce inappropriate discretion while preserving the legitimate flexibility ASHAs need?
Consider community-based monitoring, peer accountability, performance data, and how accountability structures interact with ASHA motivation and working conditions.
Sources: Lipsky (1980); Iyer & Mani (2012) REStat; Muralidharan et al. (2016) AER; CAG reports on CSS implementation; PAHAL DBT evaluation; Drèze & Khera on PDS reform.
Module 07 — Public Finance

Public Finance & Fiscal Federalism

The architecture of government revenue and expenditure in India — who collects what, who spends what, and how money moves between levels of government.

Essential Reading
Rao, M.G. (2018). "Fiscal Federalism in India." NIPFP Working Paper 236.
CAG of India. Annual Reports on Union Government Finance Accounts.
RBI (annual). State Finances: A Study of Budgets. Reserve Bank of India.
Varna
Reading government budgets is a skill that most development practitioners never formally learn. If you want to be able to assess whether a state is fiscally positioned to expand a programme, or whether a scheme's budget is credible, we can work through real budget documents together.
Book 1:1 Coaching
Check Your Understanding
Multiple Choice
1The "primary deficit" is a useful indicator of current fiscal policy because:
A high-debt government may have a large fiscal deficit even if it is pursuing responsible current policy — because interest payments on past debt inflate the deficit. The primary deficit (fiscal deficit minus interest payments) shows the deficit attributable to current revenue and expenditure decisions, allowing comparison across governments with different debt histories.
Multiple Choice
2Why does India's official fiscal deficit figure understate the government's true borrowing?
Off-budget financing is the systematic use of entities outside the budget — PSU borrowing, Food Corporation of India's credit from NSSF, state guarantees for utilities — to fund government activity without it appearing in the headline deficit. The CAG and NIPFP have documented these off-budget liabilities repeatedly. India's "true" consolidated fiscal position is consistently worse than official figures suggest.
Reflection
3India's fertiliser subsidy costs approximately ₹1.75 lakh crore annually and benefits primarily larger farmers who use more fertiliser. A targeted alternative — cash transfers to all farmer households regardless of fertiliser use — would cover more farmers at lower cost. Why has this reform not happened, and what does the answer tell us about the political economy of subsidy reform generally?
Consider who benefits from the current system besides farmers (fertiliser companies, input dealers), the information required for targeting, and how "visible" versus "invisible" beneficiaries affect politics.
Sources: Rao (2018) NIPFP; CAG Union Finance Accounts; RBI State Finances Study; Ministry of Finance Budget Documents; FRBM Review Committee Report (2017).
Module 08 — Public Finance

Budgeting in India

Reading the Union Budget: documents, process, political economy, and what the numbers actually mean.

Essential Reading
Ministry of Finance (annual). Budget at a Glance. Government of India.
Chakraborty, P. (2016). "Gender-Responsive Budgeting in India." NIPFP Working Paper.
PRS India Budget Analysis (prsindia.org) — annual ministry-by-ministry breakdowns.
Vandana Soni
Gender Budget analysis is an under-used tool in civil society. Comparing Part A allocations to actual releases, and then to ground-level programme reach, often tells a very different story than the headline numbers. If you're working in gender or social protection, this is worth a focused session.
Book 1:1 Coaching
Check Your Understanding
Multiple Choice
1The most reliable way to assess whether a government programme is actually being funded — not just announced — is to compare:
The BE-RE-Actuals tracking exercise is the essential budget analysis skill. Budget Estimates are aspirational; Revised Estimates show mid-year adjustment; Actuals show what was actually spent. A scheme with a large BE but consistently small Actuals is either administratively incapable of absorbing money or is being quietly starved despite the announcement.
Multiple Choice
2India's Gender Budget Statement (Statement 13) is criticised primarily because:
The substantive critiques are about methodology (arbitrary Part A classification, no enforcement) and about what is measured (allocations, not outcomes). Including ICDS entirely in Part A inflates the women's budget figure without necessarily reflecting gender-responsive design. The statement is useful as a starting point for gender budget analysis, not as a reliable outcome indicator.
Reflection
3Take any Union Budget scheme relevant to your work (e.g., PM Poshan, PM Awas Yojana, Jal Jeevan Mission). Using publicly available Demands for Grants data from the Ministry of Finance website, compare its Budget Estimates and Revised Estimates for the last three years. What pattern do you see? What does this tell you about the government's actual resource commitment to this scheme?
PRS India's budget analysis pages compile this data accessibly. Look for the gap between BE and RE as a percentage — gaps above 15–20% are significant.
Sources: Ministry of Finance Budget Documents (annual); CAG Finance Accounts (annual); Chakraborty (2016) NIPFP; PRS India Budget Analysis; CBGA India Gender Budget Analyses.
Module 09 — Public Finance

Taxation & Revenue

India's tax architecture, its chronic underperformance, the political economy of exemptions, and the demonetisation episode as a case study.

Essential Reading
Acharya, S. (2016). "Demonetisation: The Costs." Business Standard.
Srinivasan, T.N. (2019). "India's Fiscal Policy" in Kapur & Mehta (eds.) Rethinking Public Institutions.
Revenue Foregone Statement — Union Budget documents (any recent year, available at indiabudget.gov.in).
Varna
The demonetisation episode is a rich case study in policy design, evidence use, and political economy all at once. If you want to work through the welfare effects — who bore the costs and who gained — and what it implies for using macroeconomic instruments for anti-corruption goals, this is a productive coaching topic.
Book 1:1 Coaching
Check Your Understanding
Multiple Choice
1India's agricultural income tax exemption is economically problematic primarily because:
The agricultural income exemption was designed when most farmers were poor and subsistence-oriented. Today, large commercial farmers, agribusiness firms, and individuals routing income through agricultural enterprises benefit disproportionately. It creates a perverse incentive to classify income as agricultural to avoid tax, and removes a potential revenue base without targeting the intended beneficiaries (small and marginal farmers who have little income in any case).
Multiple Choice
2The most documented economic finding about demonetisation is:
The near-complete return of demonetised currency is the defining empirical fact of the episode. It directly contradicts the theory of change — that black money holders would be unable to exchange notes without revealing income. What it showed instead is that black money is held primarily in other asset classes (real estate, gold, foreign accounts), not in currency, and that the informal economy rapidly converted currency through formal channels.
Reflection
3India's tax expenditures (exemptions and deductions) total more than ₹8 lakh crore annually and are largely invisible to public scrutiny — they don't appear in the budget as spending, yet they represent the same fiscal choice. Make the case for why tax expenditures should face the same annual Parliamentary vote and public scrutiny as direct spending. What political obstacles would this reform face?
Think about who benefits from specific exemptions (SEZs, corporate depreciation allowances, agricultural income), whether they have organised political representation, and how "visibility" affects political accountability.
Sources: RBI Annual Reports; CSO national income data; Revenue Foregone Statement (indiabudget.gov.in); Acharya (2016) Business Standard; Srinivasan (2019); NIPFP GST analysis.
Module 10 — Regulatory & Sectoral

Regulatory Governance

How India's independent regulators work, the capture problem, and sector-by-sector analysis.

Essential Reading
Stigler, G. (1971). "The Theory of Economic Regulation." Bell Journal of Economics.
Mehta, P.B. & Khilnani, S. (eds.) (2010). The Oxford Companion to Politics in India. Chapter on regulatory governance.
CERC Annual Reports and FORUM of Regulators publications (cercind.gov.in).
Vandana Soni
Development organisations increasingly need to engage with regulatory processes — in health, environment, education, and social protection. Understanding how to participate in regulatory consultations, how to read regulatory orders, and how to identify capture is directly useful. Let's work through a sector you're engaged with.
Book 1:1 Coaching
Check Your Understanding
Multiple Choice
1Stigler's regulatory capture theory predicts that over time:
Stigler argued that regulation is "acquired by the industry and is designed and operated primarily for its benefit." The mechanisms are: information asymmetry (industry knows more than regulators), revolving doors (career paths link regulators and regulated), and concentrated benefits to industry vs. diffuse costs to consumers who are too numerous to organise. This doesn't mean all regulation is captured — but it identifies the structural pressure regulators face.
Multiple Choice
2India's electricity sector DISCOMs have accumulated ₹6+ lakh crore in debt despite repeated central bailout schemes primarily because:
The DISCOM crisis is structural, not cyclical. State governments set agricultural tariffs at zero or near-zero for political reasons. SERCs cannot set cost-reflective tariffs when state governments block them. AT&C losses mean a quarter of electricity is unaccounted for. Central bailouts (Ujwal DISCOM Assurance Yojana, Revamped Distribution Sector Scheme) address liquidity without resolving the political economy of tariff subsidies.
Reflection
3Design the ideal appointment process for a sector regulator (pick any: SEBI, TRAI, or a new Social Protection Regulatory Authority if one existed). What criteria would govern selection? Who would do the selecting? What would prevent political capture? What accountability mechanisms would you build in, and how would you balance independence with democratic legitimacy?
Consider merit-based selection committees, fixed non-renewable terms, post-service employment restrictions, mandatory Parliamentary confirmation, and performance review mechanisms.
Sources: Stigler (1971) Bell Journal; Mehta & Khilnani (2010); CERC and SERC annual reports; Forum of Regulators publications; PRAAPTI portal DISCOM data; CAG electricity sector audit reports.
Module 11 — Regulatory & Sectoral

Social Policy Architecture

India's health, education, nutrition, and social protection systems — design, performance, and the convergence failure.

Essential Reading
Drèze, J. & Sen, A. (2013). An Uncertain Glory, Chapters 5–7 (Health, Education, Nutrition).
NFHS-5 (2019–21). National Family Health Survey. Ministry of Health and Family Welfare.
Selvaraj, S. et al. (2022). "PM-JAY: Coverage, Utilisation and Equity." Economic and Political Weekly.
Varna
The convergence failure is something I think about a great deal in terms of development practice. If your organisation works on nutrition, WASH, or early childhood, understanding the ministerial architecture and where convergence points exist is essential for programme design. Let's work through your specific context.
Book 1:1 Coaching
Check Your Understanding
Multiple Choice
1The primary structural concern about PM-JAY is:
PM-JAY's design assumes adequate hospitalisation supply. Where public hospitals are insufficient, the scheme effectively subsidises private hospital care — creating incentives for over-provision of procedures (particularly Caesarean sections) and fraudulent claims. Without simultaneous investment in public hospitals, PM-JAY primarily reduces out-of-pocket costs at private facilities rather than making healthcare genuinely public.
Multiple Choice
2India's persistently high child stunting rates despite economic growth are best explained by:
Stunting is determined by water quality, sanitation, women's anaemia levels, birth weight, breastfeeding practices, dietary diversity, and healthcare access — all sitting in different Ministries. Even well-funded individual programmes fail when the other determinants are neglected. This is the convergence failure: each Ministry optimises for its own targets while the multi-sectoral outcome — a child's growth trajectory — has no institutional owner.
Reflection
3Tamil Nadu achieves better social outcomes than Uttar Pradesh despite UP having a larger per-capita central transfer. This is not primarily explained by money. Using evidence from this module, explain the institutional, political, and historical factors that account for the difference. What specific reforms would you propose for UP, and which are politically feasible in the short term?
Consider party competition for welfare constituencies, civil service stability, public ration system history, women's literacy as a social capital input, and historical investment in district-level institutions.
Sources: Drèze & Sen (2013); NFHS-5 (2021); Selvaraj et al. (2022) EPW; NHM annual reports; MoE NEP 2020 document; POSHAN Abhiyaan progress reports; India State-Level Disease Burden Initiative.
Module 12 — Regulatory & Sectoral

Environmental & Land Policy

Environmental regulation, land acquisition, tribal rights, and the development-environment tension in India.

Essential Reading
Ramanathan, U. (2006). "Illegality and the Urban Poor." Economic and Political Weekly.
Bhushan, C. (2016). "Land Acquisition and Conflict." Centre for Science and Environment.
MoEFCC (2022). India's Updated NDC. Ministry of Environment, Forest and Climate Change.
Vandana Soni
Environmental and land policy intersects with most development sectors — livelihoods, displacement, tribal rights, climate adaptation. If your work involves community land claims, environmental clearance processes, or climate vulnerability, I'd be glad to work through the regulatory terrain with you.
Book 1:1 Coaching
Check Your Understanding
Multiple Choice
1The most significant innovation in the LARR Act 2013 compared to the 1894 Land Acquisition Act was:
The 1894 Act required only "public purpose" — defined expansively by the acquiring authority — and provided compensation at market value without consent. The 2013 Act's requirement of 80% landowner consent and Social Impact Assessment represents a fundamental shift in the doctrine of eminent domain — from the state's unilateral right to acquire, to a process requiring community participation. This is what made the 2014–15 amendment attempt so contested.
Multiple Choice
2India's failure to pass a national climate law means:
Without a climate framework law, there is no statutory carbon budget, no legally mandated sectoral targets, no independent climate authority, and no mechanism to hold ministries accountable to NDC commitments. The contrast with the UK (Climate Change Act 2008 with five-year carbon budgets) or the EU (European Climate Law) shows what institutionalised climate governance looks like. India's approach relies on political commitment and administrative direction, which may not survive political transitions.
Reflection
3A coal mine is proposed in a Schedule V area with significant forest cover and a dense tribal population. The land contains forest that was in the process of being titled under FRA when the mining clearance application was filed. Walk through the legal and regulatory framework: what rights does the gram sabha have, what clearances are required, and where are the institutional gaps that allow displacement to proceed despite protections on paper?
Consider FRA gram sabha consent requirements, Forest Conservation Act clearance, EIA process, PESA protections, and how the non-completion of FRA titling is used to argue that FRA rights don't yet exist.
Sources: Ramanathan (2006) EPW; Land Conflict Watch India; MoEFCC EIA Notification (2006); LARR Act 2013 text; Tribal Affairs Ministry FRA implementation data; India NDC (2022).
Module 13 — Evaluation & Reform

Evidence, Evaluation & Policy Learning

What counts as evidence in Indian policy, how governments use (and ignore) evaluation, and how policy learning happens across states.

Essential Reading
Deaton, A. & Cartwright, N. (2018). "Understanding and Misunderstanding Randomized Controlled Trials." Social Science & Medicine.
CAG of India — any recent Performance Audit Report on a social sector programme (cag.gov.in).
NITI Aayog SDG India Index and Dashboard (sdgindiaindex.niti.gov.in).
Varna
Knowing how to use CAG reports, Standing Committee reports, and NITI Aayog data to build evidence-based policy advocacy is a skill that development organisations rarely invest in. If you want to build this capacity — including how to read a CAG performance audit and use it in your policy work — this is excellent coaching territory.

Check Your Understanding
Multiple Choice
1The "equilibrium effects" problem with using RCTs to design national policy refers to:
NREGA wage effects illustrate this: in districts with high NREGA penetration, private agricultural wages rose (a general equilibrium effect on the labour market). A small RCT testing NREGA in a few villages would not have captured this because control villages' wage levels were also affected. When scale changes from a village to the national economy, the equilibrium — prices, wages, behaviour — is different.
Multiple Choice
2CAG performance audit reports are particularly valuable for development practitioners because:
CAG performance audits use government's own financial and administrative records to assess whether stated objectives were achieved. This makes them credible — they cannot be dismissed as biased external criticism — and forensically useful. The NREGA audit reports, for example, have documented specific payment delay patterns, ghost muster rolls, and asset quality issues that civil society and researchers have used for advocacy.
Reflection
3A programme you know well has a strong RCT showing it works at small scale. The government is now considering national rollout. What additional evidence would you want before advocating for this? What would you want to know about implementation infrastructure, political economy, and equilibrium effects before recommending scale-up?
Consider: Can the government actually deliver this at scale with its current administrative capacity? Are there feedback effects at scale that would change the intervention's impact? What are the political conditions for sustained funding and implementation fidelity?
Sources: Deaton & Cartwright (2018) SSM; J-PAL Policy Lessons; CAG Annual Reports; NITI Aayog SDG Index methodology; PRS Parliamentary Productivity Reports.
Module 14 — Evaluation & Reform

Political Economy of Reform

Why good policies fail to get adopted, why bad ones persist, and how reforms actually happen in India.

Essential Reading
Olson, M. (1965). The Logic of Collective Action. Harvard University Press. (Chapters 1–2)
Rodrik, D. (1996). "Understanding Economic Policy Reform." Journal of Economic Literature.
Jenkins, R. (1999). Democratic Politics and Economic Reform in India. Cambridge University Press.
Vandana Soni
Political economy analysis — mapping who gains, who loses, and what the veto points are — is a skill that NGOs and CSOs often lack. Building it into your advocacy strategy is the difference between proposing reforms and actually achieving them. Let's work through a reform you're trying to advance.
Book 1:1 Coaching
Check Your Understanding
Multiple Choice
1Olson's collective action logic explains why subsidy reform is politically difficult because:
This is the core of Olson's insight. Fertiliser industry workers, large farmers who use subsidised inputs, and dealers in the PDS chain are each individually more motivated to organise politically to protect their benefits than any individual taxpayer is to organise against the subsidy. The asymmetry of motivation — concentrated losers from reform, diffuse gainers — is what makes subsidy rationalisation politically costly even when economically obvious.
Multiple Choice
2India's 2020 Labour Codes were enacted by Parliament but have largely not come into force because:
The Labour Codes require both central and state notification of rules. Most states — including BJP-governed ones — have not notified, because labour law reform is politically sensitive at the state level where elections are held. This is a microcosm of India's federal reform dynamic: central legislation can be enacted but without state implementation, substantive change doesn't occur.
Reflection
3India has been trying to reform its agricultural marketing system — allowing farmers to sell outside the APMC mandi system — since at least 2003. The 2020 Farm Laws attempted this and were repealed in 2021 after massive protests. Using the political economy framework from this module, explain why this reform has repeatedly failed. Who are the concentrated losers? What political miscalculation did the 2020 attempt represent? What would a politically viable reform path look like?
Consider the role of commission agents (arhatiyas) in APMC mandis, Punjabi and Haryanvi farmer organisations, the BJP's electoral calculus in these states, and how the MSP guarantee demand emerged as a counter-demand.
Sources: Olson (1965); Rodrik (1996) JEL; Jenkins (1999) Cambridge; Tsebelis (2002) Veto Players; Ministry of Labour Labour Code documents; PRS India farm laws analysis.

Comparative Policy: South Asia

What India can learn from Bangladesh, Pakistan, Sri Lanka, and Nepal — and what the regional comparison reveals about Indian policy.

Essential Reading
Drèze, J. & Sen, A. (2013). An Uncertain Glory, Chapter 3 — India in comparative South Asian perspective.
Hossain, N. (2017). "The Problem of 'Aid Dependence' and BRAC's Development Model." Development Policy Review.
World Bank (2022). South Asia Economic Focus — any recent edition for regional overview.
Varna
The Bangladesh comparison is one of the most productive conversations I have with practitioners — particularly those who assume India's size and democracy automatically translate to better outcomes. If you're working on gender, education, or social protection, the Bangladesh and BISP models are worth studying carefully.
Book 1:1 Coaching
Check Your Understanding
Multiple Choice
1Bangladesh's HDI rank exceeding India's despite lower per-capita income is primarily attributed to:
The female-centred explanation is best supported: the female secondary stipend programme transformed girls' enrollment; garment employment generated female economic independence at scale; BRAC and Grameen filled service delivery gaps. These factors — particularly female empowerment — have strong evidence links to the health, nutrition, and education outcomes where Bangladesh outperforms India.
Multiple Choice
2Sri Lanka's 2022 economic crisis is most relevant to Indian policy as:
Sri Lanka's crisis was triggered by the Rajapaksa government's 2019 tax cuts that reduced revenue by 2% of GDP, combined with COVID impacts and commodity price shocks. The lesson is not that welfare states are unsustainable — Sri Lanka's welfare commitments delivered real human development for decades — but that fiscal recklessness can rapidly destroy the resource base that funds them. India's own tax expenditure burden and off-budget liabilities make this comparison relevant.
Reflection
3BRAC's development model — delivery through a large NGO operating as a quasi-state — has been credited with Bangladesh's social outcomes but criticised as creating donor dependency and undermining state capacity. India has chosen a different model: rights-based legislation implemented through the state with civil society accountability. What are the respective trade-offs? Under what conditions would India's model outperform Bangladesh's, and vice versa?
Consider sustainability, scale, accountability, political economy, and the conditions under which state capacity can be built vs. substituted.
Sources: Drèze & Sen (2013); Hossain (2017); World Bank South Asia Economic Focus; UNDP HDR data; Bangladesh BRAC Annual Reports; Pakistan BISP Impact Assessments; Sri Lanka IMF programme documents.
Module 16 — Capstone

Capstone Project

Apply the full policy analysis framework to produce a rigorous, practitioner-relevant policy brief or governance assessment.

Project Architecture

Week 1
Problem Definition and Context
Select a specific policy or programme — narrow enough to analyse in depth. Define the problem it is meant to solve. Trace its legislative or executive origin. Identify which tier of government is primarily responsible and how funding flows. Map the institutional actors: which ministry, which regulator, which state body.
Week 2
Political Economy and Process Analysis
Who benefits from the current policy? Who bears the costs? Who are the veto players for reform? Where in the policy cycle is this currently — is it being formulated, implemented, or evaluated? What political conditions would create a reform window? Draw on Modules 4, 5, and 14.
Week 3
Fiscal and Implementation Assessment
Track the budget: compare Budget Estimates, Revised Estimates, and Actuals for the last three years. What does under- or over-utilisation tell you? Assess implementation quality: is this a street-level bureaucracy problem, a fiscal transfer problem, or a design problem? Use CAG reports and/or Standing Committee reports as evidence.
Week 4
Recommendations and Reform Pathway
Develop three to five specific, actionable recommendations. For each: what exactly changes, who needs to act, what is the political feasibility, and what is the evidence base? Propose a monitoring framework. If relevant, identify the comparable international or state-level practice that supports your recommendation.

Deliverables

Policy Brief (3,500–4,500 words)
Full analysis covering problem definition, institutional context, fiscal assessment, political economy, and recommendations.
Budget Analysis Table
BE vs RE vs Actuals for three years, with commentary on what the pattern reveals. Data from Ministry of Finance Demands for Grants.
Stakeholder Map
Visual or tabular mapping of who gains, who loses, and what the veto structure looks like for your reform proposal.
Monitoring Framework (500 words)
Indicators, data sources, and accountability mechanisms for tracking whether your recommended reform is being implemented.
