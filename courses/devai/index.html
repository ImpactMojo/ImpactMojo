Why Study AI in Development M&E?

AI is reshaping how development organizations collect data, target beneficiaries, and monitor programs. But the gap between vendor promises and ground reality is vast. Organizations waste millions on tools that don't work in low-connectivity environments, or worse, deploy algorithms that systematically exclude the most vulnerable.

This course differs from typical AI hype in crucial ways: we focus on what actually works in low-resource contexts, when simpler tools outperform AI, and how to assess whether your organization is ready for AI adoption—or whether the investment would be wasted.

Evidence-Based Assessment

Move beyond vendor demos to rigorous evaluation. Learn to assess tools using development research standards, not Silicon Valley metrics.

Context-Specific Application

What works in Accra may fail in Upper East Region. Deep focus on infrastructure constraints, data quality challenges, and organizational capacity.

Ethical Frameworks

Algorithmic bias, data sovereignty, consent in low-literacy contexts. The ethical dimensions that vendor pitches never mention.

The AI-M&E Landscape

What does "AI" actually mean in development practice? This module demystifies the taxonomy of tools—from simple automation to machine learning to large language models—and maps the current state of AI adoption in the sector.

What This Module Covers

Understanding the AI landscape in development requires cutting through marketing terminology to identify what each technology actually does, what data it needs, and when it outperforms simpler alternatives. This module provides the foundational taxonomy you'll use throughout the course.

Key insight: The term "AI" in development marketing covers everything from simple if-then rules to sophisticated neural networks. A tool that auto-fills survey fields is called "AI." A tool that predicts drought from satellite imagery is also called "AI." These are fundamentally different technologies with vastly different requirements, costs, and reliability levels.

Taxonomy of AI Technologies in Development

The term "AI" is used loosely in development contexts, often conflating fundamentally different technologies. Clear taxonomy is essential for appropriate tool selection.

Key Distinction: Prediction vs. Causation

ML excels at prediction—identifying who is likely to be poor, which programs are at risk of failure. But prediction ≠ causation. Knowing that households with tin roofs are poor doesn't tell you whether providing tin roofs reduces poverty. Development requires both—ML for targeting and monitoring, RCTs for causal inference.

The "AI Hype Cycle" in Development

Development organizations tend to follow a predictable pattern with new AI technologies:

Phase 1: Hype (Months 1-6)

A conference presentation or donor initiative sparks excitement. "AI will revolutionize our M&E!" Vendor demos look impressive. Leadership is enthusiastic. Budget is allocated.

Phase 2: Reality Check (Months 6-18)

Data quality issues emerge. The tool doesn't work offline. Staff resistance grows. The vendor's demo doesn't match field conditions. Costs escalate beyond initial estimates.

Phase 3: Trough or Learning (18+ Months)

Organizations either abandon the initiative ("AI doesn't work for us") or -- more productively -- recalibrate expectations and find specific, bounded use cases where AI genuinely adds value.

"The organizations that succeed with AI are those that survive the trough of disillusionment and emerge with realistic expectations about what AI can and cannot do in their specific context." — Adapted from Blumenstock (2018), Nature

Case Study: Ghana's LEAP Program

Ghana's Livelihood Empowerment Against Poverty (LEAP) program illustrates both the promise and challenges of AI in social protection:

LEAP uses proxy means testing (PMT) with ML-enhanced models to identify eligible households. Initial models trained on 2012 census data showed 70%+ accuracy in identifying the poor. But when verified against the 2017 census, accuracy dropped to 55%—worse than random selection in some districts. Why? Economic conditions changed, and the model couldn't adapt.

Common AI Adoption Pitfalls

The Pilot Trap

Organizations run endless pilots that never scale. Without clear success criteria and transition plans, pilots become permanent experiments consuming resources without delivering value.

Solution Looking for a Problem

Starting with "we should use AI" rather than "what problem are we trying to solve." The technology should serve the mission, not the other way around.

Context Transfer Failure

Importing tools built for high-income contexts without adaptation. What works in London's NHS does not work in a rural health clinic in Rajasthan with intermittent electricity.

The AI Readiness Framework

Most AI failures in development aren't technical—they're organizational. A tool that works brilliantly in a pilot often fails at scale because the underlying conditions weren't in place.

Data Infrastructure

Do you have clean, consistent, accessible data? Most organizations underestimate data cleaning costs (typically 60-80% of AI project time).

Technical Capacity

Who will maintain the system after the consultant leaves? AI requires ongoing maintenance, model updates, and troubleshooting.

Organizational Culture

Is leadership committed to data-driven decisions? Will staff trust algorithmic recommendations?

Infrastructure Context

Connectivity, power, devices—do field conditions support the tool? What's the fallback when systems fail?

The "Is AI Even Necessary?" Checklist

1. Can a simpler solution work? (Excel formulas, conditional logic, rule-based systems)
2. Is the problem well-defined? (Vague problems produce vague AI)
3. Do you have enough data? (Hundreds of examples minimum; thousands preferred)
4. Is the data representative? (Models trained on urban data fail in rural contexts)
5. Can you afford to be wrong? (AI errors have consequences—what's the cost?)
6. Can you explain decisions? (Many contexts require human-interpretable reasoning)

Organizational Capacity Assessment

Beyond the technical checklist, organizations need honest self-assessment of their capacity to sustain AI systems. This goes beyond having a data person on staff.

Capacity LevelDescriptionAI Recommendation
Level 1: BasicPaper-based data collection, minimal digital systems, no dedicated M&E staffFocus on digitization first. AI is premature.
Level 2: EmergingDigital data collection (ODK/KoboToolbox), basic dashboards, 1-2 M&E staffRule-based automation. Explore simple ML for specific use cases.
Level 3: EstablishedIntegrated data systems, dedicated data team, regular analysis routinesReady for ML pilots. Start with well-defined, bounded problems.
Level 4: AdvancedRobust data infrastructure, technical staff, data-driven culture, API integrationsReady for sophisticated AI deployment. Consider building custom solutions.

Case Study: India's PM-KISAN Targeting

India's PM-KISAN program provides ₹6,000/year to 110 million farming families. The program explored AI-based targeting to identify eligible farmers from land records, satellite imagery, and existing databases.

The verdict: AI added limited value. Land records were incomplete (only 60% of farmers have clear titles). Satellite imagery couldn't distinguish owner-cultivators from tenant farmers. In the end, self-declaration with Aadhaar verification proved simpler and nearly as accurate—at a fraction of the cost.

Comparing AI Data Collection Approaches

Each AI-enhanced data collection method has specific prerequisites and limitations. Understanding these is critical for selecting the right tool for your context.

High literacy + smartphone access: Chatbots, mobile surveys, image recognition

Data Quality Challenges with AI-Collected Data

AI-assisted data collection introduces new quality concerns alongside the traditional ones. Understanding these is critical for designing appropriate validation protocols.

Systematic Transcription Errors

ASR errors are not random -- they cluster around specific accents, vocabulary, and speaking patterns. This creates systematic bias, not just noise. Rural speakers and women are disproportionately affected.

Response Bias from Chatbots

People respond differently to chatbots than to human interviewers. They may give shorter answers, avoid sensitive topics, or disengage entirely. Chatbot data is not a direct substitute for face-to-face interviews.

Selection Bias in Digital Surveys

Phone-based and online surveys systematically exclude those without devices, connectivity, or digital literacy. In rural South Asia, this can mean missing 40-60% of the target population.

Practical Challenges in Geospatial AI

Cloud Cover

In tropical regions (much of Sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia), cloud cover obscures 40-60% of optical satellite images. Radar-based alternatives (SAR) exist but require different expertise and produce different types of data.

Ground-Truthing Requirements

Models must be validated against ground-level data. A poverty map derived from satellite imagery is only useful if it correlates with actual household welfare. This requires expensive field surveys for calibration.

Temporal Mismatch

Satellite imagery captures a moment in time. Economic conditions can change rapidly (drought, conflict, market shifts). Models need frequent recalibration, which is rarely budgeted for.

When Satellite Imagery Adds Most Value

High value: Areas with no recent survey data, rapid change detection, large geographic coverage needs, infrastructure monitoring at scale.

Low value: When household-level targeting is needed, when the outcome of interest has no visible spatial signature, when ground surveys are already frequent and affordable.

Practical NLP Workflow for M&E Teams

For organizations new to NLP, the following workflow balances automation with quality control:

Step 1: Explore with Topic Models

Run unsupervised topic modeling (LDA) on your full text corpus to identify initial themes. This gives you a "map" of what your data contains without imposing a predetermined framework.

Step 2: Human Coding of Sample

Manually code a random sample (200-500 responses) using your theory of change categories. This creates the "ground truth" for training and validation.

Step 3: Train & Validate Classifier

Train a text classifier on your human-coded sample. Validate against a held-out test set. If accuracy is below 75%, your categories may be too ambiguous or your sample too small.

Step 4: Apply & Review

Apply the trained classifier to the full dataset. Have a human reviewer check edge cases and low-confidence predictions. Report inter-rater reliability alongside findings.

This four-step workflow has saved teams hundreds of hours. The key insight is Step 1: let the data tell you what themes exist before imposing your theory of change. You might discover issues you never thought to ask about.

Critical Limitation: Context and Nuance

NLP excels at scale—processing 10,000 survey responses that humans couldn't read. But it struggles with context: sarcasm, cultural references, implicit meanings, contradictions within a response. For sensitive topics (GBV, corruption, mental health), human interpretation remains essential. Use NLP to triage and explore; use humans to understand.

Essential Reading

  • Gentzkow, M. et al. (2019). Text as Data, Journal of Economic Literature

LLMs for Qualitative Analysis: Promise and Peril

Large language models (GPT-4, Claude, Gemini) offer new possibilities for qualitative data analysis. They can summarize long transcripts, generate initial codes, and even identify themes across hundreds of documents. But they also introduce new risks.

What LLMs Do Well

Summarization of long texts, initial thematic coding, translation between languages, generating interview guides, and identifying patterns across large document sets.

What LLMs Do Poorly

Understanding cultural context, detecting sarcasm or implicit meaning, maintaining consistency across analyses, respecting data privacy (cloud-based models send data to external servers).

Critical privacy concern: Sending interview transcripts containing personal narratives to cloud-based LLMs may violate data protection agreements and ethical review protocols. Always check whether your IRB approval and data sharing agreements permit third-party processing before using any cloud-based AI tool.

Mobile Phone Data for Poverty Prediction

Call detail records (CDRs) have been used to predict poverty in several countries. Blumenstock et al. (2015) showed that phone usage patterns (call frequency, diversity of contacts, mobility patterns) can predict wealth with moderate accuracy in Rwanda.

The mobile data paradox: Phone-based poverty prediction works best for people who use phones regularly. But the poorest populations -- those most in need of targeting -- are often those with least phone usage. This creates a fundamental coverage gap that no amount of algorithmic sophistication can solve.

Varna
Blumenstock's work is elegant, but he himself cautions against overreliance on phone data for targeting. The populations most in need of social protection are often invisible in phone data. This is a fundamental limitation, not a solvable engineering problem.

The Algorithmic Bias Problem

Algorithms can encode and amplify existing biases. If training data underrepresents marginalized groups (women, minorities, people with disabilities), the model will systematically underserve them. In development contexts, this is particularly dangerous because the groups most likely to be excluded from data are often those most in need of services.

Gender Bias

Phone-based targeting excludes women (40% gender gap in mobile ownership in South Asia). Asset-based models may miss female-headed households.

Geographic Bias

Models trained on accessible areas perform poorly in remote regions. Urban training data may not transfer to rural contexts.

Documentation Bias

Algorithms using administrative data exclude those without documents—often the most marginalized (refugees, migrants, street children).

The Shift from Periodic to Continuous Monitoring

Traditional M&E operates on quarterly or annual cycles. A typical program collects baseline data, conducts a midterm review, and runs an endline survey. Problems are often discovered months or years after they begin. AI-enabled real-time monitoring changes this paradigm fundamentally.

Why Real-Time Matters

Consider a nutrition program distributing supplements to children under 5. Under traditional M&E, if the supply chain breaks in a remote district, you might not know until the next quarterly report -- by which time months of malnutrition have occurred. AI-enabled monitoring can detect the supply break within days by analyzing distribution records, inventory data, and even satellite imagery of warehouse activity.

AI-Powered Monitoring Systems

Several organizations have pioneered AI-enabled monitoring at scale. The tools range from simple anomaly detection to complex predictive systems.

Anomaly Detection

Algorithms flag unusual patterns: sudden drops in attendance, unexpected expenditure spikes, geographic clustering of complaints. UNHCR uses this for fraud detection in cash programs.

Predictive Early Warning

ML models predict which programs are at risk of failure based on early indicators. WFP's HungerMap combines satellite data, market prices, and conflict indicators for food security alerts.

Automated Data Quality

AI identifies suspicious survey responses: impossible combinations, pattern responses, outliers. Reduces reliance on manual data cleaning.

Data Fusion: Combining Multiple Data Streams

The most powerful real-time monitoring systems combine multiple data sources. No single data stream provides a complete picture. The challenge is integrating data from different sources with different formats, frequencies, and reliability levels.

Satellite + Survey Data

Combine satellite-derived vegetation indices with household survey data to create early warning systems for food insecurity. Satellite data provides coverage; surveys provide ground truth.

Social Media + Field Reports

Monitor social media for early signals of crisis (disease outbreaks, displacement) and cross-reference with field reports. Useful for rapid assessment but prone to bias toward urban, connected populations.

Market Prices + Climate Data

Track commodity prices alongside weather data to predict food security outcomes. Price spikes in local markets can be early indicators of supply chain disruption or crop failure.

The Limits of AI in Causal Inference

Why ML ≠ RCT. Prediction vs. causation. Heterogeneity detection. Understanding what AI can and cannot tell us about program impact.

The Fundamental Distinction

Prediction: ML excels at predicting outcomes—who is poor, which programs will fail, what areas need intervention. But prediction doesn't tell you why.

Causation: To know if a program causes outcomes, you need experimental or quasi-experimental methods. Correlation in ML predictions is not evidence of causal impact.

Implication: Use ML for targeting and monitoring; use RCTs and rigorous evaluation for impact assessment. They're complements, not substitutes.

Vandana
This is arguably the most important module in the course. Every M&E professional needs to understand why ML predictions -- no matter how accurate -- cannot substitute for experimental evidence on program impact. Confusing prediction with causation leads to bad policy decisions.

Where ML Can Support Causal Inference

Heterogeneity Detection

Causal forests and other ML methods can identify which subgroups benefit most from interventions—going beyond average treatment effects.

Covariate Selection

ML can identify which variables to control for in quasi-experimental designs, improving precision without researcher degrees of freedom.

Synthetic Controls

ML-weighted synthetic control methods construct better counterfactuals for case study analysis.

Ethics, Bias & Accountability

Algorithmic fairness, data sovereignty, consent in low-literacy contexts. The ethical dimensions that every development practitioner must understand.

Why Ethics is Not Optional

In commercial AI, ethical failures mean bad press and regulatory fines. In development AI, ethical failures mean people go hungry, are denied healthcare, or are wrongly excluded from social protection. The stakes are fundamentally different, and the ethical standards must be correspondingly higher.

The asymmetry of harm: When a recommendation algorithm suggests the wrong movie, the cost is mild annoyance. When a targeting algorithm denies a family emergency food aid, the cost can be starvation. Development AI operates in contexts where errors have life-and-death consequences.

Core Ethical Principles for AI in Development

Do No Harm

AI systems can cause harm through exclusion, discrimination, or privacy violations. "Move fast and break things" is not appropriate when lives are at stake.

Informed Consent

People have the right to understand how their data is used. Consent in low-literacy contexts requires more than a signature—it requires genuine understanding.

Transparency & Explainability

People affected by algorithmic decisions deserve to know how those decisions are made. "Black box" models are often inappropriate in development contexts.

Data Sovereignty

Communities should control their own data. Extractive data practices—collecting data without benefit to communities—replicate colonial patterns.

Consent in Low-Literacy and Low-Trust Contexts

Standard consent procedures -- reading and signing a form -- are inadequate when respondents cannot read, when power dynamics make refusal feel impossible, or when the concept of "data" is unfamiliar.

Visual Consent Protocols

Use pictorial representations of data flows: who collects, who stores, who sees, for how long. Illustrated consent forms tested in South Asia showed 40% better comprehension than text-only forms.

Community-Level Consent

In collectivist cultures, individual consent may need to be supplemented with community-level discussion and agreement. But community consent cannot replace individual consent -- both are needed.

Ongoing Consent

Consent is not a one-time event. When data use changes (e.g., from monitoring to targeting), consent must be renewed. This is especially important when AI introduces new data uses not originally envisioned.

When evaluating AI vendors for development applications, standard tech industry criteria are insufficient. Development-specific factors must be assessed.

Ask for the failure cases. Any vendor can show you success stories. Ask specifically: where has this tool failed? What were the conditions? How was the failure handled? Vendors who can't answer this question either haven't been honest about their track record or haven't tested rigorously enough.

The Sustainability Challenge

The "Project Cycle" Problem

AI systems are deployed during funded project periods (3-5 years) but require ongoing maintenance. When the project ends, who pays for server costs, model updates, and technical support? Most organizations have no plan.

Model Drift

ML models degrade over time as the world changes. A targeting model trained on 2020 data becomes less accurate every year unless retrained. Budget for ongoing recalibration, not just initial development.

Staff Turnover

The person who understood the AI system often leaves before the project ends. Document everything. Ensure institutional knowledge doesn't walk out the door with one employee.

Evaluation Criteria

Analytical Rigor (35%)

Realistic recommendations with attention to implementation challenges, budget constraints, and sustainability. Could this actually be implemented?

Ethical Awareness (15%)

Thoughtful engagement with bias, consent, data sovereignty, and accountability. Are safeguards adequate for the context?
