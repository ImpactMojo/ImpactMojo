Why Study Gender?

Gender is not a personal attribute. It is a system of power — one that shapes who does which labour, who owns property, who speaks in public, who survives violence, and who gets to define what counts as knowledge. Understanding how this system works, and where it can be challenged, is foundational to any serious development practice.

What this course does differently: Most gender training in the development sector stays at the level of checklists and indicators. This course goes deeper — into feminist theory, political economy, legal architecture, and movement history — so practitioners can design interventions that address structural causes, not symptoms.

Theoretical Depth

Butler, Crenshaw, Connell, Agarwal, Kabeer, Menon, Chakravarti — primary texts and core arguments, not summaries.

What is Gender?

Foundations

Most people enter a gender studies course assuming they already know what gender is — it is about men and women, perhaps with some acknowledgment that there are more categories. This module argues that this assumption needs to be entirely dismantled before anything useful can be built. Gender is not a description of biological difference. It is a social, political, and historical system that assigns meaning, value, labour, and power on the basis of perceived sex. The distinction matters enormously for practice.

Sex, Gender, and the Biology Trap

The sex/gender distinction — sex as biological, gender as social — was enormously productive when feminist scholars first articulated it in the 1970s. Ann Oakley's 1972 work Sex, Gender and Society established that what we take to be natural female or male behaviour is almost entirely learned. Cross-cultural anthropology showed that the labour, temperament, and social roles assigned to men and women varied so substantially across societies that biology could not explain them.

But the distinction has limits. Judith Butler's Gender Trouble (1990) made the more unsettling argument that sex itself is not a pre-social fact — that the category of biological sex is produced through discourse and regulation. What counts as male or female anatomy, which bodies get assigned to which category, which chromosomal patterns are treated as standard — all of these involve interpretation and social norm. This does not mean bodies are not real. It means that the significance we attach to bodily difference is always already shaped by power.

Key Concept — Performativity

Butler's concept of gender performativity holds that gender is not something you have but something you do — repeated acts that produce the illusion of a stable inner gender identity. This is not a claim that gender is a free choice or a costume. The repetition is compelled, and deviation carries real social costs. The point is that gender norms must be continually re-enacted to persist, which also means they can be destabilised.

Gender as a System of Power

Raewyn Connell's concept of the gender order describes gender as a structure of social relations organised around reproductive distinctions — but extending far beyond reproduction into economic production, symbolic meaning, emotional life, and political authority. Within any society's gender order, particular configurations of masculinity and femininity are ranked. Connell's concept of hegemonic masculinity refers to the dominant form that legitimates men's power over women and over other men who fail to embody it. This is not about individual men being dominant but about how the gender system reproduces inequality structurally.

For development practitioners, this matters because gender inequality is not primarily a problem of bad attitudes that training can fix. It is embedded in property regimes, labour markets, legal systems, cultural production, and political institutions. Changing it requires working on structures, not just on minds.

Gender in South Asia: What the Local Data Shows

India's National Family Health Survey (NFHS-5, 2019–21) provides some of the most detailed gender data available for a large developing country. The numbers are striking. Female labour force participation in India is around 25% — among the lowest in the world and lower than many poorer countries. Son preference remains measurable: India's sex ratio at birth is 110.5 males per 100 females. Nearly 57% of women aged 15–49 are anaemic. And 29% of ever-married women have experienced spousal physical or sexual violence.

These are not random statistics. They are connected. Son preference, anaemia, low labour force participation, and domestic violence are all expressions of a single underlying gender order — one in which female bodies, labour, and lives are systematically valued less. Addressing any one of them in isolation will have limited impact without understanding the system they are part of.

History of Feminist Thought

Foundations

Feminist thought is not a single tradition. It is a set of competing and sometimes contradictory intellectual and political projects, shaped by the specific material conditions and power structures each wave of feminism was responding to. Understanding that history is necessary to evaluate contemporary debates — and to avoid reinventing wheels or repeating mistakes.

First Wave: Rights and Suffrage (1840s–1920s)

The first wave focused on formal legal equality — the right to vote, to own property, to be educated, and to hold public office. In Britain, the suffragette movement and the work of Mary Wollstonecraft; in the United States, the Seneca Falls Convention of 1848 and the long struggle for the 19th Amendment. In India, the debates were different: reform movements in the 19th century, led predominantly by men like Ram Mohan Roy, addressed practices like sati and child marriage — but on terms that often preserved upper-caste patriarchal authority while reforming specific practices.

The critical limitation of first-wave feminism, visible in retrospect, is that its universalism masked a particular subject: the educated, middle-class, often white or upper-caste woman. The suffrage movements in Britain and the US were starkly racialised — Black women were excluded from or sidelined in campaigns that claimed to speak for all women.

Second Wave: The Personal is Political (1960s–1980s)

Second-wave feminism — associated in the US with the National Organization for Women, consciousness-raising groups, and scholars like Betty Friedan, Kate Millett, and Shulamith Firestone — moved the analysis beyond formal rights to the structures of everyday life: the family, sexuality, reproductive rights, domestic violence, and the division of labour in the home. The slogan "the personal is political" captured the insight that private arrangements are maintained by public power.

Socialist and Marxist feminists within the second wave — including Juliet Mitchell, Sheila Rowbotham, and later Silvia Federici — connected women's oppression to capitalism, arguing that the unpaid domestic labour women performed was essential to the reproduction of labour power, and therefore to capitalist accumulation. This analysis anticipates the contemporary care work literature directly.

In India, the second wave took shape around specific political ruptures: the 1972–73 Mathura rape case verdict, the 1978 Rameeza Bee case, and the 1980s campaigns against dowry deaths. These galvanised a women's movement that was simultaneously engaged with anti-caste politics, labour rights, and anti-communalism — making it distinct from Western second-wave feminism in important ways.

Third Wave and Postcolonial Feminism (1990s–2000s)

Third-wave feminism — associated with Kimberle Crenshaw's intersectionality framework, bell hooks, and the backlash against second wave's homogenising tendencies — centred the differences among women: race, class, sexuality, disability, caste. It also engaged with popular culture, sexuality positivity, and queer theory in ways the second wave had not.

Postcolonial feminist critique, articulated most sharply by Chandra Talpade Mohanty in "Under Western Eyes" (1984), challenged the way Western feminist scholarship constructed "Third World women" as a monolithic, victimised category — an analytical move that produced patronising policy and bad development practice. Mohanty argued for grounded, historically specific analysis rather than universal feminist categories.

Key Concept — Postcolonial Feminism

Postcolonial feminism holds that gender analysis must be situated within histories of colonialism, racism, and imperial power. "Women" is not a universal category — what it means to be a woman in rural Bihar, in Nairobi, and in Amsterdam involves entirely different structural conditions. Gender-blind universalism in development policy often reproduces colonial knowledge hierarchies even when it claims to be liberatory.

Contemporary Feminism in India

Contemporary feminist scholarship in India — the work of Nivedita Menon, Uma Chakravarti, Flavia Agnes, Gopal Guru, Sharmila Rege, and Vrinda Nabar — is marked by several tensions the earlier literature does not resolve: the relationship between anti-caste and feminist politics, the limits of the criminal law as a tool for feminist ends, the politics of the veil and religious identity, and the question of what feminist solidarity means across caste and class lines.

Uma Chakravarti's concept of Brahmanical patriarchy is essential here: she argues that in the Indian context, caste and gender are not parallel oppressions but are structurally intertwined — the regulation of women's sexuality is the mechanism through which caste endogamy is maintained. This means gender and caste cannot be analysed separately without distorting both.

Intersectionality

Foundations

Intersectionality is one of the most cited and most misunderstood concepts in contemporary social science. In its original formulation — Kimberle Crenshaw's 1989 article "Demarginalizing the Intersection of Race and Sex" — it was a precise legal argument about why anti-discrimination frameworks failed Black women in the United States. In development practice, it has sometimes been reduced to a synonym for "multiple identities" or used decoratively without changing any analytical or programmatic logic. This module recovers the original argument and extends it to the Indian context.

Crenshaw's Original Argument

Crenshaw was analysing three employment discrimination cases in which Black women's claims were dismissed because they could not identify a disadvantage that applied to all Black people (including Black men) or to all women (including white women). The courts treated race and sex as separate tracks — but Black women's specific disadvantage existed precisely at the intersection. A workplace that discriminated against Black women while hiring Black men and white women could escape both race and sex discrimination law.

The theoretical point is not that Black women face a "double burden" — more oppression because they are both Black and female. It is that the combination produces qualitatively different experiences that cannot be derived by adding race-oppression to sex-oppression. The intersection is its own structural location, with its own specific dynamics.

Key Concept — Intersectionality

Intersectionality, in Crenshaw's formulation, holds that race, class, gender, sexuality, caste, disability, and other axes of power do not operate independently — they interact to produce distinct social positions and experiences of both advantage and disadvantage. A gender analysis that ignores caste will misidentify which women are most vulnerable and why. A caste analysis that ignores gender will misidentify what caste oppression looks like for women versus men.

Caste, Gender, and Class in South Asia

In the Indian context, the analytically equivalent framework has been developed through Dalit feminist scholarship — particularly the work of Sharmila Rege, Gopal Guru, and activists like Ruth Manorama. The caste-gender nexus operates differently at different social locations. Dalit women face violence that is simultaneously sexual and caste-based — rape as a form of caste humiliation, used deliberately to punish communities that challenge the social order. This violence is qualitatively different from what Savarna women experience and cannot be understood through a gender lens alone.

The Khairlanji massacre of 2006 — in which four members of a Dalit family, including two women, were killed and the women sexually assaulted — illustrates this. Mainstream women's organisations were initially slow to respond, partly because the caste dimension was initially underplayed. Dalit feminist organisations had to insist that the violence was simultaneously about caste domination and about gender, not one or the other.

Intersectionality in Development Practice

Development programmes frequently use gender disaggregation — breaking data down by male/female — as a proxy for gender analysis. This is necessary but not sufficient. It assumes that "women" are a homogeneous group whose interests and vulnerabilities can be represented by any woman's data. In practice, a self-help group programme that reaches upper-caste rural women while missing Dalit women and Muslim women has not "reached women" — it has reinforced the existing hierarchy among women.

Meaningful intersectional programme design requires disaggregating data by multiple axes simultaneously: gender × caste × religion × disability × age. It requires asking whose interests are represented in women's collective forums. And it requires acknowledging that some women hold power over other women — that the category "women" is not internally solidary.

Political Economy of Gender

Bodies, Labour & Economy

The political economy of gender asks: how does the organisation of gender relations shape economic outcomes — and vice versa? This is not simply about whether women are paid less than men. It is about how the entire structure of markets, households, institutions, and policy is shaped by gender hierarchies — and how those hierarchies are reproduced through economic arrangements.

Bina Agarwal and the Economics of Land

Bina Agarwal's A Field of One's Own (1994) remains the most comprehensive political economy analysis of gender and land rights in South Asia. Agarwal's argument is that women's lack of independent land rights is not just a welfare problem but a structural source of their vulnerability across multiple dimensions: bargaining power within marriage, risk of destitution in widowhood or divorce, susceptibility to domestic violence, and ability to access formal credit. Land ownership is a structural asset that activates agency across multiple domains.

Agarwal's concept of bargaining power — drawing on cooperative conflict theory — holds that outcomes within households are negotiated between members with different interests, and that the terms of negotiation are shaped by what each party can credibly threaten to exit to. A woman with no independent land, no income, and no social support for separation has very weak exit options — and this weakness shapes every negotiation, including over her own bodily autonomy.

Key Concept — Fallback Position

In bargaining theory, the fallback position is what each party gets if negotiations break down. For women, this includes: independent income, land or property ownership, legal entitlements, social support networks, and cultural legitimacy for exit. Development interventions that improve women's fallback positions — through asset transfers, legal aid, SHG membership — change the terms of household bargaining even without directly targeting intra-household dynamics.

Naila Kabeer and the Three Dimensions of Empowerment

Naila Kabeer's framework for women's empowerment — developed in her work on Bangladesh and later applied across South Asia — identifies three interlinked dimensions: resources (material and social inputs), agency (the ability to make meaningful choices), and achievements (the outcomes that follow from resources and agency). The framework emphasises that resources without agency do not produce empowerment — and that agency without resources is similarly limited.

Kabeer's work on the garment industry in Bangladesh challenged the assumption that formal employment automatically empowers women. She found that while factory work gave women independent income and weakened family control over their mobility, it also exposed them to new forms of exploitation and did not necessarily change gender norms in the household. The relationship between economic participation and empowerment is mediated by the structural conditions under which participation occurs.

Care Work & Social Reproduction

Bodies, Labour & Economy

Care work — cooking, cleaning, child-rearing, elder care, emotional labour — is the largest single economic activity in the world by time spent. It is also systematically excluded from GDP measurement, underpaid when monetised, and assigned overwhelmingly to women. Understanding care work as a political economy issue — not a cultural preference — is essential to designing development policy that does not simply transfer the costs of public sector retrenchment onto women's bodies and time.

What Is Social Reproduction?

Social reproduction refers to the work required to reproduce labour power across generations: bearing and raising children, maintaining adult workers' physical and emotional capacity, caring for the ill and elderly. Feminist economists and sociologists — Silvia Federici, Nancy Folbre, Arlie Hochschild — have argued that this work is the foundation on which the formal economy rests, and that its invisibility in economic accounting is not an oversight but a structural feature.

GDP measures production, not reproduction. When a woman cooks a meal at home, it contributes nothing to GDP. If she pays someone else to cook it, that transaction enters GDP — but the underlying work is identical. This asymmetry means that economic growth statistics systematically undercount the economic contribution of women and overcount the productivity of marketised economies relative to care-dependent ones.

Key Concept — The Care Penalty

The care penalty describes the economic disadvantage that accrues to workers who take on care responsibilities — typically women. It shows up as wage penalties for motherhood, reduced working hours, career interruptions, lower lifetime earnings and pensions, and reduced social security entitlements. In India, the care penalty is compounded by the almost complete absence of public childcare infrastructure and elder care provision, meaning that family care falls almost entirely on women.

Time Use and the Indian Evidence

India's Time Use Survey (2019) — the first national time use survey in nearly two decades — provides the most comprehensive picture of unpaid work in India. The findings are stark: women spend an average of 299 minutes per day on unpaid domestic and care work, compared to 97 minutes for men. The gap is 200 minutes — over three hours — per day. This is time that is not available for formal employment, education, rest, or political participation.

The gap is larger in rural areas, among women with less education, and among women in households with young children or elderly dependents. It is also larger in states with less public infrastructure for care — fewer functioning anganwadis, less piped water (which alone saves women hours of daily water collection), and less access to fuel-efficient cooking technologies.

Gender & Labour Markets

Bodies, Labour & Economy

Labour markets are among the most gender-stratified institutions in any economy. Women are concentrated in particular sectors, paid less for equivalent work, less likely to hold supervisory or managerial positions, and more likely to be in informal and precarious employment. These patterns are not explained by differences in productivity or human capital — they reflect the gender order operating through markets.

Occupational Segregation

Horizontal segregation — the concentration of women and men in different occupations — is remarkably persistent across economies at different development levels. Women are overrepresented in care, cleaning, and clerical work; men in construction, heavy industry, and management. Vertical segregation — the concentration of men at higher levels within any given field — means that even in feminised professions, men disproportionately hold leadership roles.

A key insight from feminist labour economics is that feminisation of an occupation tends to precede or accompany a wage decline — not because women's entry causes wages to fall, but because occupations lose social prestige and wage bargaining power as their gender composition changes. This has been documented for teaching, clerical work, and medical practice in several contexts.

Informal Work and Homebased Labour

In India, the majority of women workers are in informal employment — as agricultural labourers, homebased workers, domestic workers, street vendors, and piece-rate workers in garment and incense stick production. These workers are largely outside the protections of formal labour law: minimum wage enforcement is weak, they lack social security, and organising is difficult. The invisibility of homebased work — which often takes place within the domestic sphere — makes it particularly hard to regulate and count.

SEWA (Self-Employed Women's Association), founded in 1972 by Ela Bhatt in Ahmedabad, built the first major union for informal women workers in India. SEWA's model — combining trade union functions with cooperative development and social security provision — demonstrated that informal workers could organise collectively and that "empowerment" and "labour rights" were not separable projects.

Personal Laws & Legal Pluralism

Law, Rights & the State

India has a system of legal pluralism in family law that is distinctive globally: different religious communities are governed by different personal laws — Hindu, Muslim, Christian, and Parsi — on matters of marriage, divorce, maintenance, inheritance, and adoption. This system was inherited from colonial administration and has been preserved in independent India as a concession to minority community autonomy. Feminist legal scholars have argued that this system systematically disadvantages women across all communities while framing community autonomy as justification for denying women equal rights.

The Personal Law System

Hindu personal law was codified and partially reformed in the 1950s through the Hindu Code Bills — after intense resistance from the right wing and from conservative members of the Constituent Assembly. The Hindu Succession Act (1956) gave daughters rights to parental property for the first time, though it excluded agricultural land in many states. The 2005 amendment to the Hindu Succession Act made daughters coparceners in joint family property — a significant legal advance, though implementation has been uneven.

Muslim personal law in India is governed largely by uncodified customary practice and by the Shariat Application Act 1937. Triple talaq — the practice of instant divorce by a husband pronouncing "talaq" three times — was declared unconstitutional by a five-judge bench of the Supreme Court in 2017 (Shayara Bano v. Union of India). The subsequent Muslim Women (Protection of Rights on Marriage) Act 2019, which criminalised triple talaq, was contested by many Muslim women's organisations who argued that criminalisation of the husband would leave women in worse positions — separated but with husbands in jail and no maintenance.

Key Concept — Legal Pluralism and Gender

Legal pluralism — the coexistence of multiple legal systems within a single state — is often defended as respect for minority cultural autonomy. Feminist legal scholars like Flavia Agnes argue that this framing obscures the fact that the "communities" claiming autonomy are internally hierarchical, and that the members whose interests are sacrificed to community autonomy are almost invariably women. The Uniform Civil Code debate in India turns on this tension.

Flavia Agnes and the Limits of Legal Reform

Flavia Agnes's work — from her organisation MAJLIS and her legal scholarship — offers perhaps the most rigorous analysis of personal law, gender, and the limits of legal reform in India. Her argument is not simply that personal laws are discriminatory (they are) but that the criminal law is a blunt and often counterproductive instrument for addressing domestic relations. Women who use legal remedies frequently find that the system re-traumatises them, that cases drag for years, that they face community pressure to withdraw, and that even successful prosecutions often leave them worse off materially.

Agnes's alternative framework focuses on practical remedies — maintenance, protection orders, custody — over criminal prosecution; and on building legal literacy and support systems so women can navigate an adversarial system that was not designed for them.

Sexual Violence, Law & Justice

Law, Rights & the State

Sexual violence is among the most documented and most contested sites of feminist legal engagement in India. From the Mathura rape case of 1972 to the Nirbhaya case of 2012 and its aftermath, the criminal law on sexual violence has been substantially reformed under feminist pressure — yet the experience of survivors within the legal system remains marked by secondary victimisation, institutional failure, and impunity for influential perpetrators.

The Mathura Case and Law Reform

The Mathura rape case — in which a sixteen-year-old Adivasi girl was raped by two police constables in a police station in Maharashtra — became a landmark in Indian feminist legal history not because of its legal outcome but because of its failure. The Supreme Court acquitted the accused in 1979 on the grounds that Mathura had not cried out and therefore had consented. Four law professors — including Upendra Baxi — wrote an open letter to the Chief Justice challenging the judgment, which galvanised the women's movement and directly led to the 1983 amendments to the rape law, including the reversal of the burden of proof in custodial rape cases.

The 2012 Delhi gang rape and murder — and the subsequent Justice Verma Committee report — produced the most comprehensive overhaul of sexual offences law in Indian history. The Criminal Law (Amendment) Act 2013 expanded the definition of rape, created new offences (stalking, voyeurism, acid attack), and strengthened penalties. But Justice Verma's committee also made recommendations — on marital rape, on police reforms, on fast-track courts — that the government did not implement.

Key Concept — Secondary Victimisation

Secondary victimisation refers to the additional trauma inflicted on survivors by the institutional response — police disbelief, intrusive medical examination, character assassination in court, family and community pressure to withdraw complaints, and the sheer duration of legal proceedings. Feminist legal reformers argue that secondary victimisation is not incidental but structural — the adversarial system is not designed to protect complainants, and police and judicial culture frequently blames survivors rather than perpetrators.

Women's Political Representation

Law, Rights & the State

Women's participation in formal political institutions — legislatures, local government, the executive — is one of the most tracked gender indicators globally. Yet the relationship between formal representation and substantive policy change for women is complex and contested. This module examines the evidence on what representation achieves, the particular case of panchayati raj reservations in India, and the limits of a purely representational framework.

The 73rd Amendment and Panchayat Reservations

The 73rd Constitutional Amendment (1993) mandated one-third reservation for women in panchayati raj institutions — local self-government bodies at the village, intermediate, and district levels. Many states subsequently raised this to 50%. The scale of this intervention is extraordinary: India now has the largest number of elected women representatives in the world at the local level — over 1.3 million, representing roughly 46% of total panchayat members.

The research literature on the effects of panchayat reservations is extensive. Raghabendra Chattopadhyay and Esther Duflo's seminal study (2004) found that in West Bengal and Rajasthan, villages with women pradhans invested more in public goods preferred by women (drinking water, roads) and had higher rates of women reporting crime to police. This is the strongest evidence that descriptive representation can affect substantive policy outcomes.

Key Concept — Sarpanch Pati Syndrome

The "sarpanch pati" (headman's husband) problem refers to cases where reserved seats produce nominal women representatives whose husbands effectively exercise the powers of the position. Research shows this is more prevalent in early rounds of reservation, in more conservative social contexts, and where women have lower education and political experience. Longitudinal studies show the syndrome weakens over time as women gain experience and confidence — but it remains a challenge requiring active support and legal enforcement.

Women's Movements in India

Movements & Resistance

India has one of the longest and most internally diverse women's movement histories in the world. From the 19th-century social reform debates through the freedom struggle, the post-independence mobilisations around rape law and dowry deaths, and the contemporary intersections of feminist with anti-caste and environmental movements — the Indian women's movement is a set of contested, sometimes contradictory political projects rather than a unified campaign.

The Chipko Movement and Environmental Feminism

The Chipko movement (1973–) — in which women in Uttarakhand embraced trees to prevent their felling by commercial contractors — is often cited as a landmark of environmental feminism. Women's dependence on forests for fuel, fodder, and water made them the primary stakeholders in forest conservation. Vandana Shiva and other ecofeminist scholars drew on Chipko to argue that women have a special relationship to nature rooted in their subsistence labour — a claim that has been critiqued for essentialising gender but that captured something real about the gendered distribution of environmental risk.

Anti-Dowry and Rape Law Campaigns (1970s–80s)

The 1970s and 80s saw sustained women's movement campaigns around two specific issues: dowry deaths (wives killed for insufficient dowry, framed as "kitchen accidents") and rape law reform (following the Mathura case). These campaigns were significant not just for the legal changes they produced but as the moment when autonomous women's organisations — not attached to political parties or the Congress — became a major political force. Forums like the National Federation of Indian Women, CREA, and local autonomous groups organised marches, public trials, and legislative lobbying simultaneously.

Gender & Caste

Movements & Resistance

The relationship between caste and gender in South Asia is one of the most theoretically important — and most politically contested — areas of feminist scholarship. This module draws primarily on Dalit feminist scholarship to argue that gender and caste cannot be treated as parallel oppressions that happen to co-occur in some individuals. They are structurally co-produced: the maintenance of caste endogamy requires the control of women's sexuality, and this structural linkage means that caste-based violence is almost always simultaneously sexual violence and vice versa.

Brahmanical Patriarchy

Uma Chakravarti's concept of Brahmanical patriarchy — developed in her 1993 essay "Conceptualising Brahmanical Patriarchy in Early India" and her later work — provides the analytical foundation. Drawing on textual, inscriptional, and anthropological evidence, Chakravarti argues that the Brahmanical order was constituted through a dual structure: the caste hierarchy (ranking of varnas) and the regulation of women (particularly sexual fidelity and endogamy). These two structures are not merely concurrent — each requires the other. Caste purity requires pure women. Pure women require caste endogamy. The system reproduces itself through the control of women's sexual and reproductive choices.

Dalit Feminist Perspectives

Sharmila Rege's Writing Caste, Writing Gender (2006) brings Dalit women's voices into feminist analysis that had largely focused on Savarna women's experiences. Rege argues that Dalit women's testimony is a form of counter-memory — a challenge to hegemonic narratives about both gender and caste that are produced without them. The experience of Dalit women is not simply "more oppressed" than Savarna women — it is qualitatively different, shaped by the specific intersection of caste humiliation and gendered exploitation.

The political implication is that feminist solidarity across caste lines requires Savarna women to acknowledge their own complicity in caste privilege — not just to extend sympathy to Dalit women. This is a demand that has been resisted within mainstream feminist movements, producing ongoing tensions about whose experiences define "women's issues."

Masculinities & Non-Conforming Identities

Movements & Resistance

Gender systems do not only constrain women. They constrain all people — including men — by mandating particular performances of masculinity and femininity and imposing costs on those who fail to conform. This module examines the politics of masculinity, the specific vulnerabilities of gender non-conforming people, and the legal framework around LGBTQ+ rights in India following Navtej Singh Johar v. Union of India (2018).

The Costs of Hegemonic Masculinity

Connell's framework of hegemonic masculinity distinguishes between the cultural ideal of masculinity (which most men do not embody) and the "patriarchal dividend" that men receive from the gender order even without being at the top of the masculine hierarchy. The costs of hegemonic masculinity are borne by men who fail to conform — men who are insufficiently aggressive, insufficiently heterosexual, insufficiently economically productive — and by all men who are pushed toward dangerous, unhealthy, and emotionally constrained modes of being.

Men's health data in India shows specific patterns connected to hegemonic masculinity norms: higher rates of tobacco and alcohol use, lower rates of health-seeking behaviour, higher occupational accident rates, and near-total absence from health programmes that would benefit them. These are not random — they reflect the male gender role's demands for toughness, invulnerability, and self-reliance.

Section 377 and Its Aftermath

Section 377 of the Indian Penal Code — inherited from British colonial law — criminalised "carnal intercourse against the order of nature," effectively criminalising same-sex intimacy. The Navtej Singh Johar judgment of 2018 declared Section 377 unconstitutional to the extent that it applied to consensual same-sex acts between adults. The five-judge bench specifically cited constitutional values of dignity, autonomy, and non-discrimination.

The judgment has been celebrated as a landmark, but its limitations are equally important: it does not provide any positive rights for LGBTQ+ people — no right to marry, adopt, or access relationship recognition. The Supreme Court's 2023 rejection of petitions for same-sex marriage, while noting Parliament's authority to legislate, illustrated the gap between decriminalisation and equality.

Key Concept — Hijra and Third Gender Recognition

The National Legal Services Authority v. Union of India judgment (2014) recognised transgender persons as a "third gender" under the Constitution and directed the government to provide reservations in education and employment. The Transgender Persons (Protection of Rights) Act 2019 was criticised by transgender rights organisations for requiring medical certification for gender recognition and for failing to provide adequate social security protections — demonstrating the gap between legal recognition and substantive equality.

Vandana Soni
Development practice has been slow to incorporate LGBTQ+ rights and non-binary gender identities into gender programming. Most gender assessments still assume a male/female binary. This is not just an equity failure — it produces analytical errors. Transgender and gender non-conforming people face specific patterns of labour market exclusion, housing insecurity, family rejection, and healthcare denial that need specific programme responses. Working with local LGBTQ+ organisations to understand community-specific needs is a necessary starting point.

Gender in Development Practice

Applied & Contemporary

The mainstreaming of gender into development practice has a specific institutional history — from the Women in Development (WID) approach of the 1970s, to the Gender and Development (GAD) approach of the 1980s, to contemporary "gender transformative" programming. Understanding these shifts matters because they represent different assumptions about what gender inequality is, how it can be changed, and what development organisations can do about it.

From WID to GAD to Gender Transformative

WID (Women in Development) — associated with Ester Boserup's work and the 1975 Mexico City conference — argued that women were being left out of development, and that including them in existing economic processes would both benefit women and improve development outcomes. The limitation of this approach was that it accepted existing economic structures and sought to integrate women into them, without questioning whether those structures were themselves gendered.

GAD (Gender and Development) — emerging in the 1980s from feminist scholars and practitioners including Caroline Moser — argued that the problem was not women's exclusion but the gender relations that produced it. The analytical shift was from "women" as a category to "gender" as a relational system — asking not just what women need but how power relations between men and women need to change. This required working with men and with institutions, not just with women.

"Gender transformative" approaches — now endorsed by USAID, UN Women, and most major development organisations — aim to change the gender norms and power structures that produce inequality, not just their symptoms. In practice, this is difficult: programmes that challenge gender norms face resistance, and organisations face pressure to show short-term measurable outcomes that transformative approaches rarely produce.

Key Concept — Gender Mainstreaming

Gender mainstreaming is the strategy of ensuring that gender perspectives are integrated into all development policies, programmes, and evaluations — not just in designated "women's programmes." Adopted at the Beijing Platform for Action (1995), it is now standard policy across UN agencies and most bilateral donors. Critics argue it has been institutionalised in ways that hollow out its transformative intent — producing box-ticking rather than structural change.

Gender, Media & Digital Spaces

Applied & Contemporary

Media — from print and television to social media and algorithm-driven platforms — is one of the primary sites through which gender norms are produced, circulated, and contested. The relationship between media representation and social change is not simple or direct, but media environments shape what is imaginable and what is visible. This module examines gender in Indian media, online gender-based violence, and the gender digital divide.

Representation and Stereotype

Content analyses of Indian advertising, Bollywood, and television consistently find that women are represented primarily in domestic and relational roles; that female sexuality is either controlled (the good wife/mother) or stigmatised (the vamp); and that the cast of visible women is heavily skewed toward upper-caste and light-skinned actors. These are not merely reflections of social reality — they actively produce norms by making certain images of womanhood seem natural and others seem transgressive.

The MeToo movement in India (2018) demonstrated both the power of digital platforms for amplifying women's voices and their limits. The initial wave of public accusations — particularly in media, publishing, and the arts — produced firings and resignations. But most accusations against men in less prominent industries went nowhere, legal cases proceeded slowly, and there was a significant backlash. The movement also exposed the caste and class dynamics of which women's complaints were heard.

South Asia Comparative

Applied & Contemporary

A comparative lens across South Asia reveals both the common structural features of gender inequality in the region — rooted in shared histories of colonialism, patriarchal property regimes, and religious personal laws — and significant variation that cannot be explained by these shared factors. Bangladesh's faster progress on women's education and labour force participation than India despite lower per capita income; Sri Lanka's consistently high gender development indicators relative to regional neighbours; Nepal's constitutional gender provisions that go beyond India's — these require explanation beyond the standard poverty-equality relationship.

Bangladesh: The Garment Sector and Female Employment

Bangladesh's ready-made garment (RMG) sector employs approximately 4 million workers, of whom roughly 80% are women. The growth of female employment in the RMG sector from the 1980s has been associated with significant changes in marriage age, fertility, girls' education, and women's mobility and self-reported decision-making. This is not a simple story of liberation through employment — working conditions in the RMG sector are often hazardous (Rana Plaza, 2013), wages are at or near minimum, and organising is difficult. But the labour market integration has demonstrably changed gender norms in ways that persist beyond the workplace.

Nepal's Constitutional Gender Provisions

Nepal's 2015 Constitution contains some of the most progressive gender provisions in South Asia — including criminalisation of caste-based discrimination, provisions for gender identity, and mandatory 33% representation of women in legislative bodies. Capstone Project

Applied & Contemporary

The capstone for this course asks you to apply the frameworks developed across all sixteen modules to a specific policy, programme, or social phenomenon of your choice. The exercise is designed to test whether you can move from theory to analysis to recommendation — and whether you can do so with the specificity and structural rigour the course has been building toward.

What the Capstone Requires

You will choose one of the following options and produce a 2,000–3,000 word analysis:

  • Policy analysis: Choose a specific Indian or South Asian government policy with significant gender dimensions (MGNREGS, PMJDY, the Beti Bachao Beti Padhao scheme, the POSH Act, the 2005 Hindu Succession Act amendment, or another of your choice). Analyse it using at least three frameworks from this course — political economy, intersectionality, and one additional framework of your choosing.
  • Programme critique: Choose a gender programme run by an NGO, multilateral, or government that you have direct knowledge of. Identify which WID/GAD framework it implicitly uses, what theory of change it operates with, and what structural factors it addresses and omits.
  • Case study: Choose a specific episode of gender-based violence, political mobilisation, or legal contest from South Asia (it can be contemporary or historical). Analyse it using intersectionality, the political economy of gender, and the relevant legal framework.

Assessment Criteria

Strong capstone submissions will:

  • Name the analytical frameworks being used explicitly and apply them with precision — not as buzzwords
  • Use specific evidence (statistics, court judgments, field data, academic references) rather than general claims
  • Engage with the intersectional dimensions of the case — whose interests, which women, which structures
  • Distinguish between symptoms and structural causes, and propose recommendations that address the latter
  • Acknowledge the limits of the analysis — what the chosen frameworks illuminate and what they miss
Synthesis: What This Course Has Built

Across sixteen modules, this course has given you: a theory of gender as a system of power (not a binary attribute); a history of feminist thought and its internal debates; an intersectional framework for analysing compound disadvantage; a political economy of care work, land rights, and labour markets; a map of India's gender legal architecture and its gaps; a history of the Indian women's movement and its caste tensions; and a set of tools for gender analysis in development practice. These are not separate topics — they are interconnected analytical registers for understanding a single, complex system.
