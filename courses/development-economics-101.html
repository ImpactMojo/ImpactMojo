Module 1

What is Development Economics?

Development economics studies the economic transformation of low-income countries. It examines how economies grow, why some countries remain poor, and what policies can accelerate development.

Key Definition

"Development economics is the study of economic aspects of the development process in low-income countries." — Debraj Ray, Development Economics (1998)

Core Questions

  • Why are some countries rich and others poor?
  • What causes poverty to persist across generations?
  • How do markets function in developing economies?
  • What role should government play in development?

Key Distinctions

  • Growth: Increase in GDP/income levels
  • Development: Broader welfare improvements
  • Structural Change: Sectoral transformation
  • Capability Expansion: Human freedoms
Ray, D. (1998). Development Economics. Princeton University Press.

Evolution of Development Thinking

1950s-1960s

Modernization Era

Focus on capital accumulation, industrialization, and "big push" strategies. Key thinkers: Rostow, Lewis, Rosenstein-Rodan.

1970s

Basic Needs & Redistribution

Shift toward poverty reduction, employment generation, and meeting basic needs. ILO employment missions.

1980s-1990s

Structural Adjustment

Washington Consensus: liberalization, privatization, fiscal discipline. IMF/World Bank conditionalities.

2000s-Present

Capabilities & Evidence

Human development approach, RCTs, behavioral economics, and institutional factors. MDGs and SDGs.

Todaro, M. & Smith, S. (2020). Economic Development. 13th Edition. Pearson.

Measuring Development

Development is multidimensional. No single metric captures all aspects of human welfare and economic progress.

GDP per Capita

Total economic output divided by population. Simple but misses distribution and non-market activities.

Human Development Index

Composite of life expectancy, education, and income. Captures capabilities but misses inequality.

Multidimensional Poverty Index

Measures simultaneous deprivations in health, education, and living standards across 10 indicators.

Sustainable Development Goals

17 goals and 169 targets covering poverty, health, education, climate, peace, and institutions.

Module 1

The Capabilities Approach

"Development consists of the removal of various types of unfreedoms that leave people with little choice and little opportunity of exercising their reasoned agency."
— Amartya Sen, Development as Freedom (1999)

Core Concepts

  • Functionings: What people are able to do and be (being nourished, educated, mobile)
  • Capabilities: Freedoms to achieve functionings (the opportunity set)
  • Agency: Ability to pursue goals one values
  • Conversion factors: Personal, social, environmental

Policy Implications

  • Focus on expanding choices, not just income
  • Address structural barriers to opportunity
  • Recognize heterogeneity in needs
  • Evaluate by what people can do, not just what they have
Sen, A. (1999). Development as Freedom. Oxford University Press.
Module 1

Why Markets Fail in Development

Development economics recognizes that markets in developing countries often fail to achieve efficient or equitable outcomes due to structural features.

Information Asymmetry

Unequal information leads to adverse selection in credit markets and moral hazard in insurance.

Missing Markets

Insurance, credit, and futures markets often absent or incomplete in rural areas.

Weak Institutions

Poor contract enforcement, insecure property rights, and limited rule of law.

Coordination Failures

Individual rational decisions lead to collectively suboptimal outcomes (poverty traps).

Play: Cooperation Paradox Game
Ray, D. (1998). Development Economics. Chapter 5: Economic Inequality.
Module 1

The Role of the State

The appropriate role of government in development has been debated throughout the field's history, from dirigisme to neoliberalism to modern pragmatism.

State Functions

  • Public Goods: Infrastructure, defense, basic research
  • Regulation: Environmental, financial, consumer protection
  • Redistribution: Social protection, progressive taxation
  • Coordination: Industrial policy, standards, investment climate

State Failures

  • Capture: Policies serve elite/special interests
  • Information: States lack market information
  • Incentives: Bureaucrats may not pursue public good
  • Implementation: Policy-implementation gap

Good Enough Governance

Focus on essential institutional functions rather than first-best ideals. Prioritize reforms with highest development payoff given political constraints. — Merilee Grindle (2004)

Grindle, M. (2004). Good Enough Governance. Governance, 17(4), 525-548.

Module 1

The Lewis Dual-Economy Model

W. Arthur Lewis's 1954 model remains foundational for understanding structural transformation in developing economies with labor surplus.

Core Mechanism

In economies with surplus labor in traditional agriculture (marginal product near zero), the modern sector can expand by drawing workers at a constant wage until the surplus is absorbed—the "Lewis turning point."

Traditional Sector

  • Subsistence agriculture dominates
  • Labor paid average product (shared output)
  • Zero or near-zero marginal product
  • Disguised unemployment prevalent

Modern Sector

  • Industry and urban activities
  • Higher productivity and wages
  • Labor paid marginal product
  • Profits reinvested → capital accumulation
Lewis, W.A. (1954). Economic Development with Unlimited Supplies of Labour. Manchester School.
Module 1

Development Traps

Poverty and underdevelopment can persist due to self-reinforcing mechanisms that trap countries or individuals in low-level equilibria.

Poverty Trap

Low income → low savings → low investment → low productivity → low income. Threshold effects prevent escape.

Nutrition-Based Trap

Low income → poor nutrition → low work capacity → low earnings. Efficiency wages literature.

Human Capital Trap

Poor health/education → low productivity → underinvestment in children's human capital.

Institutional Trap

Weak institutions → rent-seeking → elite resistance to reform → persistent weak institutions.

Play: Risk & Reward Game
Bowles, S., Durlauf, S., & Hoff, K. (2006). Poverty Traps. Princeton University Press.
Module 1

Geography and Development

Geographic factors influence development through disease burden, agricultural productivity, transport costs, and climate—but are not deterministic.

Geographic Factors

  • Tropics: Higher disease burden (malaria), soil degradation
  • Landlocked: Higher transport costs, trade barriers
  • Coastal Access: Trade advantages, fishing resources
  • Resources: Natural resource curse can undermine institutions

South Asian Geography

  • Monsoon: Agricultural dependence on rainfall variability
  • Himalayas: Major rivers feed agriculture (Ganges, Indus)
  • Coastline: Cyclone vulnerability (Bangladesh, Odisha)
  • Nepal/Bhutan: Landlocked development challenges

Geography is Not Destiny

Institutions, policy choices, and technology can overcome geographic constraints. Singapore, Botswana, and South Korea show geographic disadvantages can be surmounted.

Sachs, J. (2001). Tropical Underdevelopment. NBER Working Paper 8119.
Module 1

Colonial Legacy in South Asia

Understanding South Asian development requires grappling with institutional, economic, and social legacies of colonialism that persist today.

Institutional Inheritance

Administrative systems (ICS → IAS), legal frameworks, and bureaucratic structures shaped by colonial extraction needs.

Land Systems

Permanent Settlement (zamindari), Ryotwari created distinct tenure patterns with lasting inequality effects.

Deindustrialization

Destruction of indigenous textile manufacturing and conversion to raw material exports.

Partition Effects

1947 partition created economic disruption, refugee crises, and ongoing India-Pakistan tensions.

Banerjee, A. & Iyer, L. (2005). History, Institutions, and Economic Performance. American Economic Review.
