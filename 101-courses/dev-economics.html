01 · Definition
A Field That Studies Why Nations Prosper — or Don't
Development economics examines economic, institutional, and social conditions in LMICs. It takes seriously the ways markets fail, institutions are weak, and history constrains choices.
Core question: Why do some countries grow rich while others remain poor?
  • Combines macro with micro
  • Context matters — what works in one setting may fail in another
  • Relies on field experiments, surveys, historical evidence
  • Deeply connected to policy
Development Economics
The study of economic, social, and institutional transformation of LMICs — with emphasis on market failures and state capacity.
Nearly 700M still in extreme poverty. — World Bank, 2023
India: 230M+ below the international poverty line.

03 · Poverty Trap
Why the Poor Stay Poor
Poverty Trap
Self-reinforcing mechanism where poverty prevents investments needed to escape it.
Mechanism: Low income → low savings → no investment → low human capital → low productivity → low income.
  • Credit markets fail without collateral
  • Health shocks deplete savings
  • Malnutrition reduces cognitive development permanently
  • Social exclusion limits opportunities
S-Curve: Multiple Equilibria
A one-time push across the threshold can have permanent effects.

03 · Kuznets
Does Inequality Help or Hinder Growth?
Kuznets Curve (1955)
Hypothesised inverted-U: inequality rises then falls with development. The evidence is weak.
Counter-evidence: China and India grew with rising inequality. Korea industrialised with declining inequality.
Modern consensus (IMF): High inequality is itself bad for growth sustainability.

04 · Solow
The Solow Model: Capital & Convergence
Solow Model (1956)
Output = A · K^α · L^(1-α). Countries grow by accumulating capital until steady state. Long-run growth requires technology.
  • Convergence: Poor countries should grow faster
  • Solow residual: 50–70% of rich-country growth is unexplained by K+L
  • Implication: Investment raises level but not long-run growth rate
GDP/cap Trajectories 1960–2022
Penn World Tables · Korea converged; India diverged
Policy: Savings/investment can raise output level but sustained growth depends on technology and institutions.

Lewis (1954): traditional sector with surplus labour (marginal product ≈ 0) and modern sector with higher wages. Development = labour transfer between them.

04 · Endogenous Growth
Ideas, Knowledge & Returns
Romer (1990) and Lucas (1988) endogenised technology: growth from deliberate investment in knowledge and human capital.
KEY INSIGHT
Ideas are non-rival — a blueprint can be used by everyone simultaneously. This breaks diminishing returns.
  • Romer: R&D creates new capital varieties; externalities justify subsidies
  • Lucas: Human capital accumulation generates external economies
  • Implication: State investment in education, R&D justified
For LMICs: The gap is about capacity to generate, absorb, and use knowledge. Education and technology policy are central.
India IT: Bangalore is real-world endogenous growth — but employs <4M in a 500M+ labour force.
Divergence: Countries behind in knowledge may fall further behind. Technology diffusion requires absorptive capacity.

04 · Institutions
Deep Determinants of Development
Acemoglu, Johnson & Robinson (2001): colonial settler mortality → institutions → income today. Inclusive vs extractive institutions.
"Countries with better institutions will invest more and use factors more efficiently."
— AJR · Colonial Origins · AER 2001
Two types: Inclusive (property rights, rule of law, open markets) generate growth. Extractive (concentrated power, insecure rights) impede it.
  • Land records: Unclear titles prevent collateral use — 2–3% of GDP in disputes
  • Contract enforcement: India ranks 163/190
  • Reservations: Pande (2003) shows SC/ST legislators increase targeted spending
  • Colonial legacies: Zamindari areas still have lower investment (Banerjee & Iyer 2005)
State capacity vs quality: A weak state with good rules is still a weak state. — Besley & Persson

04 · Industrial Policy
The Return of the State
Industrial policy — selective state interventions — was declared dead by the WC in the 1990s. It has returned, driven by East Asian success and climate transition needs.
East Asia: Korea, Taiwan, Japan used targeted subsidies, directed credit, and infant industry protection.
Rodrik: IP works when subsidies are temporary + tied to performance; bureaucrats have autonomy + accountability.
  • PLI Schemes (2020–): ₹1.97L crore for 14 sectors. Electronics promising, textiles lagging.
  • Make in India: Manufacturing stagnated at ~17% of GDP
  • Semiconductor: ₹76,000 crore for fabs. Strategic rationale.
  • Green hydrogen: ₹19,744 crore. First-mover logic.
Missing link: None directly targets labour-intensive sectors absorbing 7–10M new workers/year.

05 · Green Revolution
Triumph, Limits & Long Shadows
Achievement: Wheat production tripled 1965–85. Famine risk fell dramatically.
  • Geographic: Only irrigated areas benefited (40% of farmland)
  • Inequality: Large farmers with irrigation and credit gained most
  • Crop concentration: Rice–wheat monoculture replaced diverse systems
  • Groundwater depletion: Punjab/Haryana mining aquifers at 0.5–1m/year
  • Soil degradation: Yields plateaued since 1990s
  • Agrarian crisis: Input-intensive model creates debt cycles
Second Green Revolution: India needs productivity gains in eastern India — requires irrigation, storage, connectivity, not just seeds.

05 · Rural Credit
Why Credit Markets Fail the Poor
Adverse selection, moral hazard, and enforcement failure characterise rural credit.
No collateral — unclear land titles
No credit history — invisible to bureaus
High monitoring costs — small dispersed loans
Covariate risk — drought hits all borrowers at once
Result: Rural poor borrow from moneylenders at 36–120% annual interest.
Rural Credit Sources: Formal vs Informal (%)
NABARD Rural Financial Inclusion Survey
SHG-bank linkage: 12M+ SHGs linked to formal banking. Interest 14–24% — lower than moneylenders but not transformative.

06 · Education Econ
Returns, Signals & Market Failures
Human Capital
Skills, knowledge, health embodied in people. Coined by Schultz (1961) and Becker (1964).
Private returns: 8–10% earnings increase per year of schooling. Highest for primary education in LMICs and for women. — Psacharopoulos 2018
Social > private: Externalities — educated workers raise coworker productivity, civic participation, lower crime.
  • Credit failure: Cannot borrow against future human capital
  • Info failure: Families underestimate returns, especially for girls
  • Externalities: Market under-provides relative to social optimum
  • Child labour cost: Keeping a child in school has real opportunity cost

08 · Trade
Comparative Advantage & Its Limits
Comparative Advantage (Ricardo, 1817)
Countries gain from specialising in goods they produce at lower relative opportunity cost — even if one is absolutely better at everything.

Random assignment to treatment/control. Difference in outcomes = Average Treatment Effect, free from selection bias. Nobel 2019 to Banerjee, Duflo, Kremer.

09 · RCT Limits
What Randomisation Cannot Tell Us
"RCTs tell us what works in a particular place at a particular time. They cannot tell us why, whether it works elsewhere, or whether it scales."
— Synthesis of Deaton (2010), Cartwright & Hardie (2012), Ravallion (2012)
  • External validity: Kenya results may not transfer to Bihar
  • General equilibrium: Pilot effects change at scale
  • Question selection: Big questions (democracy, land reform) aren't randomisable
  • Ethics: Withholding beneficial interventions from control groups
  • Mechanism blindness: ATE without mechanism = can't fix when it breaks
  • Structural exclusion: State capacity, elite capture, caste discrimination can't be addressed by individual-level randomisation
Synthesis: RCTs essential for programme evaluation. Not a substitute for structural analysis. Best work combines both.

09 · State Capacity

  • Capital constraints: GST registration, bookkeeping prohibitive for small operators
E-Shram (2021): 300M+ unorganised workers registered. Enables targeted DBT — genuine step toward portable social protection.

11 · Care Economy
The Work That Doesn't Count
7.5hrs
Daily unpaid care work by Indian women — ILO. Men: 0.9 hrs.
$10.8T
Value of unpaid care globally at minimum wage — ILO
3R framework: Recognise (satellite accounts, time-use surveys), Reduce (piped water, gas, childcare), Redistribute (parental leave, elder care services).
  • India female LFPR (20.9%) among world's lowest — care is primary cited barrier for 60%+
  • Crèche provision catastrophically inadequate
  • Maternity Benefit Act 26 weeks — only formal sector (<10% of female workers)
Implication: Programmes targeting women's empowerment that ignore care burdens will consistently underperform. If SHG training doesn't address childcare, attendance drops.

11 · Planetary Boundaries
Development Within Ecological Limits
Rockström (2009, updated 2023): 9 biophysical boundaries. 6 of 9 now crossed — climate, biodiversity, freshwater, nitrogen, novel entities.
Development dilemma: Rich countries achieved prosperity by crossing these boundaries. Now they tell LMICs to develop differently — without adequate technology transfer or finance.
Doughnut economics (Raworth 2017): Safe and just space — social foundation (meeting needs) within planetary ceiling (ecological limits).
  • Air pollution: 15 of 20 most polluted cities are in India. 1.6M premature deaths/year. 3-5% GDP cost.
  • Water stress: 87% of freshwater for agriculture; 54% of districts face high stress
  • Soil degradation: 30% of land degraded — reducing long-run agricultural potential
Green GDP: If India subtracted environmental degradation costs, net growth would be substantially lower than headline figures.

11 · 10 Core Ideas
Every Development Practitioner Should Know
01
Context specificity
No universal formula. Diagnostic analysis beats universal prescriptions.
02
Institutions are deep determinants
Property rights, rule of law, state capacity explain more than geography.
03
Markets fail systematically
Information asymmetry, missing markets, externalities are the norm in LMICs.
04
Human capital precedes growth
Health + education investments are highest-return available.
05
Poverty is multidimensional
Income is means, not end. Capabilities matter intrinsically.
06
Distribution matters as much as growth
High-inequality growth reproduces poverty even at high rates.
07
Political economy is not optional
Who holds power, who benefits from status quo = core to design.
08
Evidence rigour matters
RCTs transformed knowledge. Use evidence — know its limits.
09
Climate is a development emergency
Adaptation is immediate priority for South Asia. Not future concern.
10
Development is ultimately political
Who benefits, whose capabilities expand = political questions.
