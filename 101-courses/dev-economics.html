Section 01 · Defining the Field
A Field That Studies Why Nations Prosper — or Don't
Development economics examines the economic, institutional, and social conditions that determine living standards in low- and middle-income countries. It is not simply applied microeconomics — it takes seriously the ways markets fail, institutions are weak, and history constrains choices.
Core question: Why do some countries grow rich while others remain poor — and what can governments and development practitioners actually do about it?
  • Combines macro (growth, trade) with micro (households, firms, credit markets)
  • Takes context seriously — what works in one setting may fail in another
  • Relies heavily on field experiments, household surveys, and historical evidence
  • Deeply connected to policy — World Bank, IMF, state governments, NGOs all draw on it
Development Economics
The branch of economics that studies economic, social, and institutional transformation of low- and middle-income countries — including growth, poverty, health, education, credit markets, and governance — with special emphasis on market failures and state capacity.
Nearly 700 million people still live in extreme poverty (below $2.15/day PPP). Understanding why — and what to do — is among the most consequential questions in social science. — World Bank, 2023
India's stake: With 230M+ people below the international poverty line and the world's largest population of MPI-poor, development economics is not abstract for South Asian practitioners — it is the intellectual backbone of the field.

Section 01 · Schools of Thought
Three Major Traditions
Tradition 01
Structural & Institutional
Development requires structural transformation — shifting resources from low-productivity agriculture to high-productivity industry and services. Institutions (property rights, rule of law, state capacity) are the deep determinants. Lewis, Acemoglu & Robinson, Rodrik, North
Tradition 02
Experimental & Micro-Empirical
Use randomised controlled trials and natural experiments to identify what actually works for poor households. Build development policy from rigorous field evidence rather than theory. Banerjee, Duflo, Kremer, J-PAL, IPA
Tradition 03
Capabilities & Human Development
Development is about expanding freedoms and capabilities — not just income. Health, education, dignity, agency, and political voice matter intrinsically. GDP growth is a means, not an end. Sen, Nussbaum, UNDP HDR, Drèze
In practice, the strongest development thinking draws on all three: structural change needs micro-foundations and institutional context, and should be judged by whether it expands human capabilities for the poorest. No single tradition is sufficient.

Foundational Perspective
"Development can be seen as a process of expanding the real freedoms that people enjoy. Focusing on human freedoms contrasts with narrower views of development, such as identifying development with the growth of gross national product, or with the rise in personal incomes, or with industrialization, or with technological advance, or with social modernization."
— Amartya Sen · Development as Freedom (1999) · Oxford University Press · Nobel Laureate in Economics, 1998
What Sen means: GDP growth that leaves people in ill health, illiterate, politically voiceless, or economically insecure is not development — even if average incomes rise. The metric must be freedom, not output.
For practitioners: Always ask — are our programmes expanding capabilities for those most constrained? Who can actually access benefits? Whose freedoms are growing and whose are not?

Section 02 · GDP
GDP Per Capita: The Default Measure & Its Limits
GDP Per Capita
Gross Domestic Product divided by total population. Measures average economic output per person. Compared across countries using PPP (Purchasing Power Parity) adjustments to control for price level differences.
What GDP Does Well
  • Easy to measure and compare across countries and over time
  • Strongly correlated with life expectancy, child mortality, literacy
  • Required for ODA eligibility, debt ratios, IMF programmes
What GDP Cannot Capture
  • Distribution: A country can grow while the poorest get poorer — GDP is silent on this
  • Unpaid work: Subsistence farming, care work, domestic labour — excluded by definition
  • Environmental costs: Deforestation and pollution boost GDP; resource depletion is invisible
  • Informal economy: 80%+ of employment in LMICs is informal, partially uncounted
  • Non-market services: Health quality and education quality are not direct components
  • Wellbeing: Life satisfaction, security, dignity, political voice are invisible to GDP
India case: India's GDP growth averaged 7%+ from 2014–2019. Yet female LFPR fell, rural real wages stagnated, and MPI-poor numbers remained in the hundreds of millions. Growth and development are not the same thing.

Gini Coefficient
A summary measure of income or wealth distribution ranging from 0 (perfect equality) to 1 (one person owns everything). Derived from the area between the Lorenz curve and the 45° line of perfect equality. Named after Italian statistician Corrado Gini (1912).

Section 03 · Poverty Dynamics
The Poverty Trap: Why the Poor Stay Poor
Poverty Trap
A self-reinforcing mechanism where poverty persists because the very conditions of being poor prevent the investments needed to escape it. Below a critical asset threshold, households cannot accumulate — they deplete savings during shocks, cannot invest in education or health, and remain locked in low-productivity equilibria.
Classic mechanism: Low income → low savings → no investment in children → low human capital → low adult productivity → low income. Each link reinforces the others across generations.
  • Credit market failure: banks won't lend without collateral, so the poor can't invest in productive assets
  • Health shocks deplete savings and pull children from school simultaneously
  • Malnutrition in early childhood reduces cognitive development and lifetime earnings capacity
  • Social and spatial exclusion limits information access, networks, and opportunities
Multiple Equilibria: The S-Curve Model
S-curve crosses 45° at 3 points: two stable equilibria (poor / rich), one unstable threshold. Below threshold: trapped. Above: escapes. Policy implication: a one-time push across the threshold can have permanent effects.

Section 03 · Rural Poverty in South Asia
Who Are the Rural Poor? Structural Identities & Overlapping Disadvantages
  • Landless agricultural labourers — no productive assets, dependent on seasonal wages, no buffer against shocks. ~140 million in India.
  • Marginal farmers (<1 ha) — too small to benefit from mechanisation. Cannot access institutional credit. Average Indian holding: 1.1 ha.
  • Scheduled Castes & Tribes — structural discrimination in labour, land, and credit markets. SC/ST poverty rates 2–3x the national average.
  • Women-headed households — triple burden of productive work, unpaid care, and social exclusion. Restricted mobility limits participation.
  • Seasonal migrants — circulate between rural distress and urban informal labour. No legal protections at destination.
  • Forest-dependent Adivasi communities — land and forest rights eroded by conservation, mining, and infrastructure.

Section 03 · Gendered Poverty
The Feminisation of Poverty
Women face structural disadvantages in labour markets, asset ownership, credit access, and within-household resource allocation — making them disproportionately vulnerable. This is structural, not individual.
  • Wage gap: Women earn 20–40% less for comparable work. Gap widest in agriculture.
  • Asset poverty: Women own <13% of land in India despite 65%+ of agricultural work.
  • Unpaid care: Women provide 5–10x more unpaid care — massive invisible subsidy to economy
  • Within-household inequality: Women and girls often eat last, withdraw from school first during shocks
  • Mobility restrictions: Social norms limit access to labour markets — North India's "missing women in workforce" pattern

Section 03 · Inequality & Growth
Does Inequality Help or Hinder Growth? The Kuznets Debate
Kuznets Curve (1955)
Simon Kuznets hypothesised an inverted-U: as countries develop, inequality first rises (labour shifts to higher-wage industry) then falls (as education spreads, redistribution grows, modern sector absorbs workforce).
The evidence is weak: China and India grew rapidly post-1990 while inequality rose sharply. South Korea and Taiwan industrialised with declining inequality. Bangladesh grew without the U-curve. History does not confirm the hypothesis.
Modern consensus (IMF, Ostry et al., 2014): High inequality is itself bad for growth sustainability — reduces HC investment among the poor, fuels instability, constrains domestic demand, weakens institutions.

Section 04 · Neoclassical Growth
The Solow–Swan Model: Capital Accumulation & Convergence
Solow Model (1956)
Output is produced by capital (K) and labour (L) with diminishing returns. Countries grow by accumulating capital until reaching steady state. Long-run growth requires exogenous technological progress (A).
PRODUCTION FUNCTION
Y = A · Kα · L1-α
Y = output · A = total factor productivity · K = capital · L = labour · α ≈ 0.33
  • Convergence: Poor countries should grow faster — higher marginal returns to capital
  • Conditional convergence: Only holds with similar savings rates, institutions, and technology
  • Solow residual: 50–70% of growth unexplained by K+L — this is TFP, driven by technology and institutions
GDP per Capita Trajectories 1960–2022: Convergence?
Penn World Tables 10.0 · Korea converged; India & SSA diverged from rich countries
Key policy implication: Increasing savings/investment raises the level of output but not the long-run growth rate — which depends entirely on technological progress. This is why institutions and innovation matter.

Section 04 · Beyond Solow
Endogenous Growth Theory: Ideas, Knowledge & Returns
Solow treats technology as exogenous. Romer (1990) and Lucas (1988) endogenised it: growth comes from deliberate investment in knowledge, human capital, and ideas — which are non-rival unlike physical capital.
KEY INSIGHT: IDEAS ARE NON-RIVAL
A machine can only be used by one firm. But a blueprint can be used by everyone simultaneously. This breaks diminishing returns and can generate sustained long-run growth.
  • Romer: R&D creates new varieties; monopoly rents incentivise innovation; externalities justify public subsidies
  • Lucas: Human capital accumulation generates external economies — social return exceeds private return
  • Policy: State investment in education, R&D, infrastructure is justified — market alone under-invests due to externalities
Why this matters for LMICs: If knowledge drives growth, the gap between rich and poor countries is about capacity to generate, absorb, and use knowledge. Education and technology policy are central.
India's IT success: Bangalore's software cluster is a real-world example — knowledge spillovers, agglomeration, learning-by-doing. But it employs <4 million in a country with 500M+ workers.
Divergence problem: Endogenous growth also explains divergence — countries that fall behind in knowledge may fall further behind. Technology diffusion requires absorptive capacity: educated workers, functional institutions.

Section 04 · Deep Determinants
Institutions as the Deep Determinant of Development
Acemoglu, Johnson & Robinson (2001): settler mortality determined colonial institutions — extractive vs inclusive. These persist and explain income differences today better than geography or culture.
"Countries with better institutions, more secure property rights, and less distortionary policies will invest more and use factors more efficiently to achieve greater income."
— AJR · "Colonial Origins of Comparative Development" · AER 2001
Two types: Inclusive (secure rights, rule of law, open markets, checks on power) generate growth. Extractive (concentrated power, insecure rights, elite capture) impede it.
  • Land records: Unclear titles prevent collateral use, impede investment — est. 2–3% GDP in litigation
  • Contract enforcement: India ranked 163/190 — high transaction costs for firms
  • Electoral accountability: Pande (2003) — SC/ST reservations increase targeted spending

Section 05 · Agricultural Transformation
The Green Revolution: Triumph, Limits, and Long Shadows
Norman Borlaug's HYV seeds for wheat and rice, combined with irrigation and fertiliser, transformed South Asian food production from the mid-1960s. India moved from food aid dependency to self-sufficiency within a decade.

  • Inequality: Large farmers with irrigation and credit benefited most. Landless labourers gained less.
  • Crop monoculture: Rice–wheat replaced diverse systems, reducing nutritional diversity and ecological resilience
  • Groundwater depletion: Punjab and Haryana mining aquifers that took millennia to fill. Water tables dropping 0.5–1m/year. Viability threatened within decades.
  • Soil degradation: Intensive chemicals reduced organic matter. Yield growth plateaued since 1990s.
  • Agrarian crisis: Input-intensive model requires borrowing — creating debt cycles when harvests fail. Structural root of farmer distress.
Second Green Revolution: India needs productivity revolution in eastern India (Bihar, eastern UP, Bengal, Odisha) where yields remain low and poverty concentrates — but requires irrigation, storage, and connectivity, not just seeds.

Section 07 · Risk Management
Risk, Vulnerability, and Informal Insurance
The poor face higher risk AND have fewer tools to manage it. They rely on informal networks (mutual aid, rotating savings, kinship transfers) that are partial, imperfect, and fail when everyone is hit simultaneously.
Idiosyncratic vs Covariate Risk
Idiosyncratic risks (one household's illness) can be pooled within a village. Covariate risks (drought, flood, pandemic) hit everyone simultaneously — only formal insurance, savings, or state transfers can address them.
Anti-insurance trap: To cope with shocks, poor households sell assets, withdraw children from school, reduce food, take high-interest loans. These coping strategies stabilise immediate consumption but deepen long-run poverty.
  • Basis risk: Indexed insurance pays on an index, not actual loss. If index doesn't track your loss, you get nothing.
  • Low take-up: Cole et al. (2013) — demand for rainfall insurance very low at fair prices. Farmers don't trust payouts.
  • Adverse selection: Those who buy are more likely to suffer losses — premiums must rise.
  • PMFBY: Low penetration, slow settlement, private insurers unwilling to cover high-risk districts.
Social protection as insurance: Where formal markets fail, MGNREGS/PM-KISAN/food subsidies act as covariate risk insurance — one of the strongest justifications for universal social protection.

Comparative Advantage (Ricardo, 1817)
Countries gain from specialising in goods they produce at lower relative opportunity cost — even if one country is absolutely better at everything. The most robust result in economics, yet deeply contested in development policy.
The evidence: Trade openness is associated with higher income. Export-led growth in East Asia is the 20th century's most dramatic success. Globalisation of supply chains lifted hundreds of millions in China and Bangladesh.
Stolper-Samuelson (1941): Trade creates winners and losers. If labour markets are segmented, if gains are captured by capital, or if trade displaces workers without alternatives, outcomes can be regressive.
  • Low trade ratio: India's merchandise trade ~43% of GDP — far below comparable economies
  • High tariffs: Average ~17% (WTO 2023) — among most protectionist G20 economies
  • RCEP withdrawal (2019): Citing manufacturing vulnerability to Chinese competition
  • Services advantage: India's genuine export strength is IT, BPO, pharma, generics — not labour-intensive manufacturing
Dilemma: Comparative advantage says India should specialise in IT services and import manufactured goods. But IT employs few low-skilled workers. Manufacturing creates mass employment. Trade policy cannot be separated from employment policy.

Section 09 · The Evidence Revolution
The RCT Revolution in Development Economics
RCTs entered development economics in the late 1990s through Kremer's Kenya work and rapidly transformed the field. The 2019 Nobel to Banerjee, Duflo, and Kremer was explicitly for this contribution.
Randomised Controlled Trial
Random assignment to treatment and control. Randomisation ensures comparability on all dimensions. Difference in outcomes = Average Treatment Effect (ATE), free from selection bias.
Why RCTs matter: Before RCTs, most evaluations suffered selection bias — people who got a programme were systematically different from those who didn't. Randomisation solves this, transforming what we know about what works.

Key findings that changed policy: Deworming ↑ attendance; microfinance ≠ poverty transformation; cash transfers ≠ reduced work effort; information provision shifts health behaviour; bed nets work even when free.

Section 09 · Critique of RCTs
The Limits of RCTs: What Randomisation Cannot Tell Us
"RCTs tell us what works in a particular place at a particular time. They cannot tell us why, whether it works elsewhere, or whether the combination of factors can be replicated at scale."
— Synthesis of Deaton (2010), Cartwright & Hardie (2012), Ravallion (2012)
  • External validity: What works in rural Kenya may not work in urban Bihar. Context shapes replication.
  • General equilibrium: Pilot effects change at scale — parasite ecology changes at 90% vs 10% coverage.
  • Question selection: Big questions (democracy, land reform) aren't randomisable. The "credibility revolution" risks focusing on what's measurable, not what matters.
  • Ethics: Withholding potentially beneficial interventions from control groups in severe deprivation contexts.
  • Mechanism blindness: RCTs tell you ATE but not why. Without mechanism, can't fix when it breaks or adapt to new contexts.
  • Structural exclusion: The biggest barriers — state capacity, elite capture, caste discrimination — cannot be addressed through individually randomised interventions.
Synthesis: RCTs are essential for programme evaluation and policy learning. They are not a substitute for structural analysis. The best development research combines RCT evidence with historical, institutional, and political economy analysis.

Section 11 · Planetary Boundaries
Development Within Ecological Limits
Rockström's planetary boundaries (2009, updated 2023): 9 biophysical boundaries. 6 of 9 crossed — climate, biodiversity, freshwater, nitrogen, novel entities.
Development dilemma: Rich countries achieved prosperity by crossing these boundaries. Now they tell LMICs to develop differently — without adequate technology transfer, finance, or policy space. This is the climate justice dimension.
Doughnut economics (Raworth 2017): A safe and just space — social foundation (meeting human needs) within planetary ceiling (ecological limits). Development must work within both constraints simultaneously.
  • Air pollution: 15 of 20 most polluted cities globally in India. 1.6M premature deaths/year. Economic cost 3–5% GDP.
  • Water stress: 87% freshwater for agriculture; 54% of districts face high-extreme stress. Depletion accelerating.
  • Forest: India claims net gain — contested. Eastern India, Western Ghats face real degradation even as plantation grows.
  • Soil: 30% of land degraded. Organic carbon depletion, salinity, erosion reduce long-run agricultural potential.
Green GDP: If India subtracted environmental degradation costs, net growth would be substantially lower. World Bank estimates negative genuine savings — consuming natural capital faster than building other wealth.
